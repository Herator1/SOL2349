Combat in _SOL 2349_ is fast, dangerous, and strongly shaped by **positioning, timing, cover, and resource pressure**. Characters are capable, but not invulnerable. A good angle, a well-timed reaction, or a single mistake can decide an engagement very quickly.

At its core, combat is built around the same systems that govern the rest of the game: the [[Dice System]], [[Time & Actions]], [[Survivability - Guard, Health & Wounds]], and the constant trade-off between speed, control, and risk.

This page explains the overall flow of combat. More detailed rules are covered in the linked subpages.

## Combat Overview

A combat encounter is resolved in **rounds**.  
Each round represents roughly **five seconds** of in-world time.

During each round:

1. determine turn order
2. each character takes their turn
3. characters may spend unused Actions reactively during the round
4. once all turns and valid reactions are resolved, the round ends and a new one begins

Combat continues until one side is defeated, withdraws, surrenders, or the situation otherwise changes.

## Turn Structure

On their turn, a character normally has **3 Actions**.

Actions can be used for:

- movement
- attacks
- activating implants or abilities
- interacting with objects or systems
- other significant tasks

Unused Actions may be held for later in the round and spent as **Reactive Actions**, if a valid trigger occurs.

A character can never use more than **5 Actions in one round** unless a rule explicitly says otherwise.

For the full timing rules, see [[Time & Actions]] and [[Initiative & Turn Order]].

## Core Combat Principles

Combat in _SOL 2349_ is shaped by a few core principles:

**There is no passive defense stat.**  
Characters avoid being hit through [[Defense, Cover & Positioning]], movement, angles, and timely reactions.

**Successful hits do not always mean real injury.**  
Damage is applied to Guard first, then Health, and only then causes Wounds. See [[Survivability - Guard, Health & Wounds]].

**Repeated actions become less efficient.**  
Making multiple attacks, repeated movement, or repeated implant use in the same round creates penalties or rising cost.

**Technology creates opportunity, not immunity.**  
Weapons, implants, armor, and gear matter, but positioning, timing, and tactical judgment matter just as much.

## Attack Resolution

Most attacks follow the same basic sequence:

1. choose a target
2. determine whether the target can be attacked at all
3. calculate the target’s final DC
4. declare any valid reactive defenses
5. roll the attack
6. if the attack hits, roll damage
7. apply damage to Guard, then Health, then Wounds if necessary

### Step 1: Choose a Target

The attacker selects a valid target within line of sight, weapon range, and any other relevant limitations.

### Step 2: Confirm the Attack is Possible

An attack may be impossible if:

- the target is behind **full cover**
- line of sight is fully blocked
- the weapon or attack mode cannot affect the target
- another rule prevents the attack

If the target cannot be attacked, the action is wasted unless the GM rules that the attacker can redirect it immediately.

### Step 3: Determine Target DC

A standard attack targets:

**Base DC 10**

Then apply all relevant modifiers, such as:

- cover
- stance
- target movement
- angle of attack
- reactive defense such as Evade
- environmental conditions
- other situational effects

For full details, see [[Defense, Cover & Positioning]].

### Step 4: Declare Reactions

If the defender has an unused Action and a valid trigger, they may declare a defensive **Reactive Action** before the attack roll is made.

The most common example is Evade.

Reactive defense must be declared:

- after the attack is declared
- before the attack roll is made

### Step 5: Roll the Attack

Attack rolls use the normal resolution system:

**1d20 + Attribute + Skill**

The roll succeeds if it meets or exceeds the target’s final DC.

Common combat modifiers may include:

- repeated attack penalties
- **Tactical Edge**
- **Setback**
- penalties from Reactive Attacks
- weapon or fire mode modifiers
- penalties from Wounds, Load, or Mental Conditions

A natural **20** is a critical success.  
A natural **1** is a critical failure.

For general resolution rules, see [[Dice System]].

### Step 6: Roll Damage

If the attack hits, roll the weapon’s damage.

Some attacks modify this through:

- critical hits
- fire modes
- weapon traits
- implants
- special effects

A critical hit normally **doubles the number of damage dice rolled**, but does not double flat modifiers.

### Step 7: Apply Damage

Damage is applied in order:

1. **Guard**
2. **Health**
3. **Wounds**

If Guard is reduced to 0, remaining damage carries over to Health.

If Health is reduced below 0, the character suffers **1 Wound**, is temporarily incapacitated, and Health resets to **0**, unless another rule says otherwise.

When a character reaches their maximum Wounds, they enter the **Dying** state.

For full details, see [[Survivability - Guard, Health & Wounds]].

## Reactive Actions in Combat

Combat allows characters to hold Actions and spend them later in the round in response to specific triggers.

Common Reactive Actions include:

- Evade
- reactive attacks
- activating an implant
- firing on a target that leaves cover
- other short, valid responses

Reactive Actions:

- use a normal Action unless a rule says otherwise
- require a clear trigger
- must be fast enough to happen in the moment
- are resolved in GM-determined order if multiple characters react at once

Reactive Actions are an essential part of combat flow and should be used actively.

## Movement, Cover, and Positioning

Combat effectiveness depends heavily on where a character is, how exposed they are, and whether they are moving.

Important factors include:

- **cover** increases attack DC
- **crouched** and **prone** positions change exposure
- moving targets are harder to hit at range
- a better angle can reduce or negate cover
- full cover prevents direct attacks entirely

These rules are covered in [[Defense, Cover & Positioning]] and [[Movement]].

## Fire Modes and Weapon Use

Ranged weapons may offer different fire modes such as:

- **Single Shot**
- **Burst Fire**
- **Full Auto**

These trade precision, damage, control, and ammunition pressure against one another.

Special rules such as **Low Ammo**, **Suppressive Fire**, and **Area Fire** are covered under [[Fire Modes]] and [[Weapons and Tactical Gear]].

## Damage, Injury, and Survival

Combat becomes increasingly dangerous as a character loses control of the situation.

- **Guard** absorbs the first layer of incoming harm
- once Guard is gone, damage goes to **Health**
- if Health drops below 0, the character suffers a **Wound**
- repeated Wounds eventually lead to **Dying**

This means many attacks that “hit” may still represent glancing impacts, shock, or near misses that wear down combat readiness before causing real bodily injury.

## Implants and Combat

Implants interact directly with combat by allowing characters to:

- improve speed or accuracy
- bypass normal limitations
- react faster than normal
- gain access to unusual tactical options

But every activation creates **Load**, and overuse risks **Overload**.

Implants do not replace tactical judgment. They allow brief moments of advantage at increasing physiological and system cost.

See [[Load]] and [[Implant Overview]].

## Mental Pressure in Combat

Combat does not only damage the body. Near death, overload, environmental terror, and psychological pressure can also create **Mental Conditions**.

Mental strain can:

- reduce concentration
- weaken technical or medical performance
- eventually trigger a **Breaking Point**

This is covered in [[Mental Strain & Psychological Pressure]].

## Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side:

- is incapacitated
- withdraws
- surrenders
- loses the will or ability to continue
- or when the scene changes enough that structured round-by-round action is no longer needed

Once combat ends, characters may begin recovering, stabilizing allies, regrouping, and reassessing the situation.

This connects directly to [[Rest & Recovery]] and [[Medicine & Stabilization]].
