In _SOL 2349_, combat is resolved in **rounds**, but initiative is kept intentionally simple. The system does not use a detailed initiative ladder or individual initiative rolls by default.

Instead, turn order is determined by **narrative logic, situational clarity, and table flow**.

## Core Principle

Who acts first depends primarily on **what is happening in the fiction**.

Use the following priority:

1. obvious narrative advantage
2. clear situational trigger
3. player side first, if no other answer is clearer
4. roll-off only if the order is genuinely contested and matters immediately

This keeps combat fast and avoids unnecessary initiative bookkeeping.

## Round Structure

A combat encounter is divided into **rounds**.

Each round:

- every relevant character gets a turn
- characters normally have **3 Actions**
- unused Actions may be held for **Reactive Actions**
- once all turns and valid reactions are resolved, the round ends

Then a new round begins.

## Determining Who Acts First

At the start of combat, the GM decides who acts first based on the situation.
Examples:
- the ambushing side acts first
- the side already aiming at the other acts first
- the character who clearly initiated the violence acts first
- a prepared defender may act before a rushing attacker
- if both sides become active at the same moment and nothing clearly decides it, **players act first**

The goal is not simulation of reflex timing down to fractions of a second, but a clear and credible start to the exchange.

## Narrative Initiative

Turn order should usually follow the logic of the scene.
Ask:
- who was ready first?
- who exposed themselves first?
- who is reacting, and who is initiating?
- who has the stronger tactical position?
- what order makes the situation easiest to understand at the table?

If the answer is obvious, use it.

Do not force a roll where the fiction already gives a clear answer.

## Player Priority

If no clear narrative answer exists, **player characters act first**.

This is the default tiebreaker and keeps the game moving without turning initiative into a repeated subsystem.

It also supports the intended tone of the game: the players are under pressure, but they still need enough agency to act decisively.

## Roll-Offs

If the order is genuinely unclear and immediately important, the GM may call for a **simple roll-off**.

Each side rolls:

**1d20 + relevant Attribute or Skill, if clearly applicable**  
or simply **1d20 vs 1d20** if no clear modifier makes sense

The higher result acts first.

Use roll-offs sparingly. They are a fallback, not the default initiative system.

Good reasons for a roll-off include:

- both sides detect each other at the same moment
- two characters go for the same critical action simultaneously
- neither narrative logic nor player priority produces a satisfying answer

## Turn Order During Combat

Once the first turn is established, play proceeds in a sensible order across the round.

In most cases, the GM should keep turn order:

- stable within the current exchange
- easy to follow
- based on the existing situation rather than constant re-sorting

The exact order does not need to be re-evaluated every single turn unless the situation changes significantly.

## Changing Order Mid-Scene

The GM may adjust turn order if the fiction changes in a major way.

Examples:

- a new combatant enters unexpectedly
- someone breaks line of sight and re-engages from a new angle
- an ambush interrupts an ongoing exchange
- a character delays openly and waits for a trigger
- the flow of the fight changes enough that a different order is now more natural

Initiative should remain flexible, but not chaotic.

## Simultaneous Actions

If two actions happen at nearly the same moment, resolve them in the order that makes the most sense in the fiction.

If that still does not resolve the issue:

- favor the clearer dramatic sequence
- or use a quick roll-off

In rare cases, the GM may allow both actions to resolve effectively at the same time, especially if neither would realistically prevent the other.

## Reactive Actions and Turn Order

Reactive Actions do not change who normally acts on a turn. They are short responses that interrupt or answer a trigger inside the round.

A Reactive Action:

- uses an available Action unless a rule says otherwise
- happens when its trigger occurs
- may interrupt another turn if the timing makes sense

If multiple characters want to react to the same trigger, the GM decides the order based on:

- narrative plausibility
- clarity of sequence
- current turn order
- or a roll-off, if necessary

## Keeping Combat Fast

The purpose of this system is to support:

- speed
- clarity
- minimal bookkeeping
- strong narrative logic

Do not stop play to optimize initiative unless the exact order really matters.

If the table is unsure, use the simplest solution and move on.

## Summary

Initiative in _SOL 2349_ follows this order of priority:

1. **narrative logic**
2. **situational clarity**
3. **players first**
4. **roll-off if truly necessary**

This keeps combat readable, fast, and grounded in the fiction rather than in a separate initiative minigame.
