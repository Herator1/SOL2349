Movement in _SOL 2349_ depends heavily on environment, gravity, available surfaces, and equipment. Moving under **full gravity**, **low gravity**, **microgravity**, or **zero-g** are fundamentally different experiences, and the rules reflect that without turning movement into a detailed physics simulation.

Movement is handled through the normal [[Time & Actions|Action Economy]]. Each movement action allows a character to reposition, but repeated movement in the same round becomes less effective.

## Core Rule

Movement usually costs **1 Action**.

The distance gained depends on how often the character has already moved in the current round:

|Use|Distance|
|---|---|
|1st movement action|10 m|
|2nd movement action|5 m|
|3rd movement action|3 m|
|4th movement action|3 m|
|5th movement action|3 m|

Each movement action is resolved separately.

Movement may include:

- advancing
- retreating
- crossing open ground
- shifting between cover
- changing relative position in close quarters

It may also include smaller physical adjustments such as crouching, leaning, or stepping, if the GM judges that these do not require a separate action.

## Splitting Movement

A movement action may normally be split around other actions during the same turn, as long as the total distance does not exceed the amount granted by that movement action.

Example:

- move 4 m out of cover
- fire
- move 6 m back behind another obstacle

The GM may restrict splitting movement if the environment is too hazardous, cramped, or unstable.

## Position Changes

Changing stance affects exposure and combat interaction.

|Position|Effect|
|---|---|
|Standing|no modifier|
|Crouched|+1 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Prone|+2 DC vs ranged attacks, −2 DC vs melee attacks|

Changing position is usually part of movement, but may cost **1 Action** if:

- space is restricted
- the character is injured
- the surface is unstable
- the change is unusually difficult under current conditions

## Movement and Combat

Movement has direct combat effects.

A moving target is harder to hit at range:

|Target State|Effect|
|---|---|
|Stationary|no modifier|
|Moving|+1 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Fast movement / sprinting|+2 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Erratic motion or unstable drift|+2 DC or Setback, GM discretion|

These bonuses only apply while the movement is actually relevant to targeting.

A character who moved earlier in the round but is currently still does not automatically count as moving.

## Gravity Environments

Different environments change how movement works in play.

## Full Gravity

**Full Gravity** means approximately Earth-normal gravity, or any environment heavy enough that body weight, traction, and fatigue behave normally.

In full gravity:

- movement works normally
- characters rely on footing and body strength
- sprinting, climbing, lifting, and sudden direction changes are physically demanding
- armor, injury, and encumbrance matter strongly

Full gravity is the baseline for most movement rules.

## Low Gravity

**Low Gravity** includes environments where gravity is present, but noticeably weaker than normal planetary gravity.

Examples may include:

- Luna-like conditions
- small moons
- lightly spun habitat sections
- weak planetary or station gravity

In low gravity:

- jumps, leaps, and vertical movement become easier
- sudden stops and sharp direction changes become harder
- characters can cover ground efficiently, but are more easily destabilized
- recoil, collision, and momentum matter more than in full gravity

Mechanical guidance:

- normal movement distances still apply
- the GM may grant **Tactical Edge** on jumps, climbing, or movement-based repositioning where reduced weight is beneficial
- the GM may apply **Setback** when balance, braking, or rapid control become difficult

Low gravity should feel more mobile, but less stable.

## Microgravity

**Microgravity** is an environment with only trace apparent gravity. Characters are not fully weightless in a practical sense, but normal movement through walking is no longer reliable.

Examples may include:

- damaged rotating sections
- transitional spin zones
- weak inertial drift environments
- unstable ship interiors during thrust changes or failure states

In microgravity:

- characters often move by pushing off surfaces, pulling along rails, or using controlled body movement
- footing is unreliable
- motion carries farther than intended
- stopping cleanly requires surfaces, restraints, or preparation

Mechanical guidance:

- normal movement actions still provide repositioning, but they usually require nearby surfaces, rails, handles, or controlled contact
- if no useful surfaces are available, movement may require a test such as **Reflexes + EVA** or **Fitness + Low-G Movement**
- the GM may apply **Setback** to sudden directional changes, melee attacks, and precision movement
- collisions, drifting, and overshooting become meaningful risks

Microgravity should feel awkward, unstable, and transitional.

## Zero-G

**Zero-G** means effective weightlessness. A character cannot walk or run in the normal sense and must move through push-offs, handholds, tethers, thrusters, or EVA systems.

Examples include:

- open space
- non-rotating vacuum environments
- fully weightless compartments
- free-fall situations

In zero-g:

- every movement action represents controlled drift, push-off, pulling movement, or thrust-assisted repositioning
- momentum matters constantly
- changing direction is often harder than starting motion
- movement without handholds, thrust, or preparation is limited and risky

Mechanical guidance:

- a character can move normally in zero-g only if they have a plausible way to do so, such as:
    - handholds
    - rails
    - tether systems
    - maneuvering thrusters
    - EVA propulsion support
- if movement is uncontrolled, difficult, or contested, the GM may require **Reflexes + EVA** or **Fitness + Low-G Movement**
- the GM may apply **Setback** or reduced movement for:
    - sudden changes of direction
    - braking
    - evasive movement
    - fighting while drifting
- characters without control may keep drifting after movement unless they stabilize themselves

Zero-g should feel dangerous, momentum-driven, and fundamentally unlike ground combat.

## Stabilized vs Unstabilized Movement

In **microgravity** and **zero-g**, it helps to distinguish between controlled and uncontrolled movement.

### Stabilized Movement

A character is stabilized if they have:

- secure footing
- a handhold
- magnetic boots
- a tether
- active maneuvering support
- another credible means of controlled positioning

A stabilized character can move and act with normal rules unless another modifier applies.

### Unstabilized Movement

A character is unstabilized if they are:

- drifting freely
- tumbling
- moving after an uncontrolled push
- in zero-g without support
- unable to stop or brace properly

An unstabilized character may:

- suffer **Setback** on precision-based or physical actions
- count as moving for targeting purposes
- be unable to Evade effectively
- drift further than intended
- require a test to stop, turn, or regain control

## Sprinting and Fast Movement

A character may use movement aggressively to cross space quickly and unpredictably.

In most grounded environments, this is treated as **fast movement / sprinting**:

- target gains **+2 DC vs ranged attacks**
- the GM may restrict careful actions during the same moment of movement
- in unstable gravity, sprinting may also require control checks

In low gravity, microgravity, or zero-g, fast movement often means:

- strong push-offs
- aggressive directional changes
- use of thrusters
- committing to momentum over control

This may increase speed, but often reduces stability.

## Difficult Movement

The GM may reduce movement, require a test, or apply Setback when movement is hindered by:

- heavy armor or bulky gear
- wounds or exhaustion
- smoke, darkness, or bad visibility
- debris, clutter, ladders, narrow maintenance shafts
- unstable footing
- vacuum hazards
- damaged station interiors
- strong thrust changes or vibration

Movement should stay playable, but the environment should matter.

## Evade and Defensive Movement

[[Evade]] is a short defensive movement used as a **Reactive Action**.

It does not replace normal movement, but represents a sudden shift, drop, lean, or push to avoid immediate harm.

In unstable environments, Evade may:

- be reduced in effect
- grant only **+1 DC**
- impose risk of drift or loss of control
- be impossible if no movement is physically possible

See [[Defense, Cover & Positioning]].

## Example: Full Gravity

A character uses one movement action to sprint from one cargo stack to another in a station corridor.

- spends **1 Action**
- moves up to **10 m**
- counts as moving for targeting
- gains **+2 DC vs ranged attacks** while actively sprinting across open ground

## Example: Low Gravity

A character crosses a lightly spun habitat section and uses the reduced gravity to clear a gap.

- spends **1 Action**
- moves normally
- the GM grants **Tactical Edge** on the jump
- landing on a narrow surface may still require control

## Example: Microgravity

A character in a failing spin section pushes off a bulkhead to reach a control panel.

- spends **1 Action**
- movement is possible because surfaces are nearby
- the GM calls for **Reflexes + EVA**
- on failure, the character overshoots and ends up drifting past the panel

## Example: Zero-G

A character in open vacuum without handholds tries to change direction after drifting.

- spends **1 Action**
- the GM requires **Reflexes + EVA**
- without thrusters or a surface to push from, movement is limited and unstable
- failure may leave the character drifting and unable to stop cleanly
