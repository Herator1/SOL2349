Space combat in _SOL 2349_ is resolved through **Crew Actions**.  
The ship acts as a shared combat platform, and its performance depends on what the crew does under pressure.

Each round represents a short burst of coordinated activity aboard the ship: maneuvering, targeting, firing, point defense, damage control, and command decisions.

Unlike personal combat, space combat is not built around every character attacking independently. The crew keeps the ship alive and dangerous by operating different systems at the right moment.

## Combat Round

A combat round represents a short period of intense action aboard the ship.

During a round:

- each occupied station may usually perform **one meaningful Crew Action**
- each Crew Action should normally involve **one player roll**
- not every station is equally important in every round
- the value of a station depends on range, threat, and situation

Typical Crew Actions include:

- maneuvering
- targeting
- direct fire
- point defense support
- system repair
- coordination

## Core Resolution

Crew Actions use the normal core mechanic:

**1d20 + Attribute + Skill**

The GM sets the difficulty based on the situation.

Typical difficulties:

|Difficulty|DC|
|---|---|
|Easy|10|
|Standard|12|
|Hard|15|

## Results

Crew Actions usually have three outcome levels:

|Result|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Success**|The action works and grants its normal effect|
|**Critical Success**|Natural 20. The effect is stronger or grants an extra advantage|
|**Critical Failure**|Natural 1. The action fails and creates a setback, complication, or system problem|

## Crew Stations

|Station|Typical Attribute + Skill|Function|
|---|---|---|
|**Pilot**|Reflexes + Piloting|Maneuvering, range control, evasive flying|
|**Sensors**|Intellect + Systems|Locking targets, scanning, electronic warfare|
|**Gunnery**|Reflexes + Gunnery|Direct fire weapons and intercept fire|
|**Engineering**|Coordination + Mechanics|Repairs, rerouting power, stabilizing systems|
|**Command**|Presence + Command|Coordination, timing, tactical pressure|

The exact station layout depends on the ship.  
Some vessels may combine roles, while larger ships may split them more finely.

## Movement Inside the Ship

Crew members may move between stations if the ship layout allows it.

As a baseline:

- moving to another nearby station usually costs **1 Action**
- this may be harder if the ship is damaged, cramped, poorly designed, under hard maneuver, or partially in zero-g
- under normal thrust, shipboard movement is possible, but rarely comfortable
- most ships in combat are not pulling constant high acceleration; **0.1–0.3 g** is more typical, with harder thrust used only briefly

The GM should treat movement between stations like ordinary movement under unstable working conditions, not like free repositioning in a stable office.

## Pilot Actions

**Typical roll:** **Reflexes + Piloting**

|Action|Success|Critical Success|Critical Failure|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Evasive Maneuver**|The ship gains **Tactical Edge** on its next defensive flying or evasion-related roll|Also cancel one ordinary movement or handling penalty this round|The ship suffers **Setback** on its next defensive flying roll|
|**Change Range**|Shift the engagement by **1 Range Band** if the situation allows|Also gain a favorable angle, positioning benefit, or setup advantage|No range change|
|**Railgun Alignment**|The ship’s next railgun attack gains **Tactical Edge**|Also ignore one ordinary firing penalty|Railgun shot loses alignment and cannot be prepared again this round|

## Sensor Actions

**Typical roll:** **Intellect + Systems**

|Action|Success|Critical Success|Critical Failure|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Target Lock**|One allied missile or direct-fire attack gains **Tactical Edge**|Two attacks gain the benefit, or one attack also ignores one ordinary tracking penalty|The enemy gains useful counter-tracking data; their next defense gains **Tactical Edge**|
|**Electronic Countermeasures**|One incoming missile or hostile targeting action suffers **Setback**|Also degrade one additional hostile tracking or lock attempt|Your emissions create a vulnerability; the next hostile lock attempt gains **Tactical Edge**|
|**Scan Weakness**|Reveal one useful weakness, opening, or unstable system; the next relevant allied action gains **Tactical Edge**|Also reveal a second useful point or remove uncertainty entirely|No useful data|

## Gunnery Actions

**Typical roll:** **Reflexes + Gunnery**

Direct-fire weapons use the normal crew action roll unless a specific weapon says otherwise.

|Action|Success|Critical Success|Critical Failure|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Fire Railgun**|The weapon hits if the attack succeeds normally|Also increase damage or effect modestly|The weapon misaligns and cannot be fired again this round|
|**Fire Laser Array**|The weapon hits if the attack succeeds normally|Also improve damage or suppress an enemy defensive option|The array overheats or loses coherence; it suffers **Setback** on its next firing action|
|**Intercept Fire**|Point-defense or direct-fire interception gains **Tactical Edge**|Also affect an additional incoming threat if the GM allows|Intercept timing is poor; the next interception attempt suffers **Setback**|

## Engineering Actions

**Typical roll:** **Coordination + Mechanics**

|Action|Success|Critical Success|Critical Failure|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Boost Point Defense**|The ship’s next point-defense action gains **Tactical Edge**|Also stabilize one ordinary system strain affecting point defense|The next point-defense action suffers **Setback**|
|**Boost Weapons**|One ship weapon attack this round gains **Tactical Edge**|Also improve damage or remove one ordinary firing penalty|Weapons synchronization slips; the next weapon action suffers **Setback**|
|**Repair Systems**|Remove one complication or restore one damaged ship function to limited operation|Also restore an additional minor function or remove a second light issue|The repair fails and costs time; no effect|

## Command Actions

**Typical roll:** **Presence + Command**

|Action|Success|Critical Success|Critical Failure|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Coordinate Attack**|One allied Crew Action gains **Tactical Edge**|Two allied Crew Actions gain the benefit|The next allied Crew Action suffers **Setback** from confusion or mistiming|
|**Tactical Assessment**|Reveal the best immediate tactical option, and one allied action following it gains **Tactical Edge**|Also expose an enemy weakness, pattern, or timing window|The assessment is wrong or mistimed; the enemy gains a small situational advantage|
|**Hold the Ship Together**|Remove panic, confusion, or hesitation from the crew’s current response|Also allow one strained crew member to act at full effectiveness this round|The crew loses confidence; the next Command action suffers **Setback**|

## Unmanned Stations

If a station is not occupied, the ship cannot perform that station’s special Crew Actions.

|Missing Station|Effect|
|---|---|
|**No Pilot**|The ship cannot meaningfully maneuver or change range|
|**No Sensors**|No target lock, proper scanning, or electronic warfare|
|**No Gunnery**|Direct-fire weapons cannot be used effectively|
|**No Engineering**|No system boosts, emergency rerouting, or repairs|
|**No Command**|No coordination bonuses or command-based tactical actions|

One character normally operates only one station at a time unless a rule, implant, or ship design says otherwise.

## Design Principle

Space combat is defined by **crew coordination**, not by individual hero pilots acting alone.

A ship survives and wins because:

- the pilot keeps it in a viable position
- the sensors operator creates firing solutions
- the gunner converts opportunities into damage
- the engineer keeps systems online
- the commander turns separate actions into one coherent response

The ship is the combat unit. The crew makes it function.
