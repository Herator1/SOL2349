Space combat in **SOL 2349** is rare, dangerous, and usually decisive. Unlike the heroic dogfights of older science fiction, combat in the vacuum of space is tense, calculated, and unforgiving. A single mistake can disable a ship, cripple its life support, or leave an entire crew drifting toward death.

Ships are expensive, crews are highly trained, and even small combat-capable vessels carry systems that can inflict catastrophic damage. Because of this, most factions prefer intimidation, maneuver, interception, or negotiation over open battle. But conflict still happens.

Ships may engage in combat to enforce law, protect trade routes, intercept smugglers, raid shipping lanes, or defend valuable infrastructure. Corporate security fleets, government patrol craft, and independent raiders all operate under different rules, but they share one reality: every engagement is dangerous, and escalation is costly.

Even when destruction is possible, most ship combat aims to **disable rather than annihilate**. A crippled vessel can be boarded, captured, salvaged, or forced to surrender. Deliberately destroying a ship—and killing its crew—is a serious escalation that many factions avoid unless they are prepared for the political and strategic consequences. Long-standing conventions discourage the use of extreme weapons such as nuclear devices or large-scale orbital bombardment, and breaking those norms can have consequences far beyond a single battle.

Combat in space unfolds across vast distances. Missiles and torpedoes may travel for long periods before impact, sensor warfare can matter as much as direct fire, and positioning often determines the outcome before weapons are exchanged. Crews fight as teams: pilots manage vector and orientation, gunners track and engage, engineers stabilize failing systems, and other specialists keep the ship functional under pressure.

For play, space combat is built around **crew actions**. The ship is treated as a unified combat entity whose capabilities depend on the people operating it. Players do not each control separate miniature ships. Instead, they take actions that activate, improve, protect, and sustain their vessel: adjusting course, enhancing targeting, boosting defenses, intercepting incoming fire, restoring damaged systems, or creating opportunities for the rest of the crew.

This keeps space combat focused on **coordination, pressure, and ship survival** rather than large fleet battles. The rules are designed primarily for encounters involving **one ship and its crew**, sometimes against one or a small number of opponents. Massive engagements, fleet actions, and highly technical simulation are outside the main focus of the current system.

The core experience of space combat in _SOL 2349_ is therefore not “fighter ace heroics,” but **crew-driven survival aboard a fragile machine under extreme pressure**. Incoming missiles, evasive thrust, damaged systems, failing point defense, overloaded reactors, and desperate last-second repairs are what define a battle in the void.
