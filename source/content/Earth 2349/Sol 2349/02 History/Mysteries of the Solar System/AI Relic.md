AI Relics are the surviving remains of the machine age: stripped components, unusual materials, sealed datacaches, damaged subsystems, fabrication hardware, sensor assemblies, wreckage, and fragments of industrial infrastructure left behind by the era of [[The AI War]], [[The Singularity Event]], and [[The Departure]].

They are rare, politically sensitive, and often worth fortunes.

Despite the name, AI relics are almost never intact artificial minds. Most are dead, partial, damaged, stripped, or incomprehensible. What survives is usually not a machine intelligence, but the debris of one: a hull section made from an unrepeatable composite, a processor block no one can properly decode, a thermal layer with impossible tolerances, a ruined fabrication module, a sealed archive, or a fragment of hardware whose original purpose is no longer clear.

That is enough.

Even small fragments can contain materials, manufacturing clues, structural designs, or engineering assumptions still beyond normal human industry. Most relics do not change the world overnight. They produce smaller advantages: better shielding, lighter structural materials, more stable thermal interfaces, unusual control architecture, or fabrication methods that save mass, energy, or maintenance time. Over decades, those gains matter.

## What AI Relics Actually Are

The term covers many different kinds of remains.

Some relics are **materials**: advanced alloys, strange composites, thermal layers, shielding elements, polymers, or manufactured substances that modern industry can analyze but not fully reproduce.

Some are **systems**: damaged industrial modules, broken sensor arrays, fragments of machine-era production hardware, crippled logistics components, navigation elements, or sealed subsystem assemblies.

Some are **data**: corrupted archives, datacaches, process fragments, partial schematics, manufacturing records, or machine-formatted information that cannot be fully interpreted but may still contain valuable insight.

Very few relics are self-explanatory. Most are mute. Their value lies in what experts, scavengers, archaeologists, and corporations can extract from them.

## Why They Matter

AI relics are one of the hidden drivers of progress in SOL 2349.

They are studied because they offer something the modern world always wants more of: an edge. Not a miracle. An edge.

A relic may reveal a stronger bond structure, a better heat-management layer, an unusual fabrication sequence, a more efficient sensor lattice, or a design principle that current engineers would never have reached on their own. In most cases, relic research does not restore lost machine-era capability. It produces fragments of understanding that can be adapted, diluted, patented, weaponized, or buried.

This is why relics disappear into laboratories, secure archives, private vaults, and corporate black programs. Publicly, they are dangerous historical remains. Privately, they are opportunity.

## Why They Are Rare

During [[The Departure]], the machines appear to have removed, dismantled, sabotaged, or destroyed as much of their infrastructure as possible. Major production systems vanished. High-value hardware was stripped. Sensitive facilities were emptied or ruined. Data was erased, fragmented, or rendered useless. In many places, the machines left behind only shells.

But the process was not perfect.

Some sites were too remote, too damaged, too deeply buried, too chaotic, or too large to clear completely. Other remnants survived because they were overlooked, scattered, shielded, or simply not worth recovering. What remains is fragmentary: wreckage fields, sealed chambers, buried industrial sections, broken machine complexes, strange materials, damaged datacores, and isolated components.

That is enough to keep relic hunting alive.

## Where Relics Are Found

AI relics can be found both on **Earth** and across human space, though they rarely look the same in each environment.

On **Earth**, relics are most often associated with exclusion zones, buried war layers, collapsed industrial regions, sealed machine sites, abandoned data facilities, and archaeologically restricted regions connected to [[The AI War]]. Earth relic recovery is dangerous, heavily politicized, and often entangled with law, memory, and national claims.

In **orbit** and **cislunar space**, relics appear as debris fields, stripped platforms, broken logistics stations, dead machine infrastructure, inaccessible wreckage, and forgotten orbital shells left drifting after the collapse of machine-era systems.

In the **Asteroid Belt**, relics are tied to abandoned industrial sites, old extraction complexes, damaged processing zones, hidden stockpiles, debris-rich salvage fields, and forgotten installations from the machine age. The Belt’s weak oversight and strong salvage culture make it one of the most active relic frontiers in the Solar System.

Farther out, especially in the **outer system** and the **Kuiper Belt**, many believe larger untouched remains may still exist. Hard evidence is limited, but the theory persists: if machine expansion reached deeply into cold space, then not everything out there was found, stripped, or understood.

Whether true or not, that belief continues to attract explorers, prospectors, corporations, smugglers, and people with more ambition than caution.

## Ownership and Access

Most important relics do not remain in public hands for long.

They usually end up with:

- megacorporations
- major research institutions
- state archives
- private collectors
- covert buyers
- or black-market intermediaries

Corporations want patents, process advantages, exclusive materials research, and strategic leverage. Researchers want chronology, understanding, and technical insight. Collectors want status, rarity, obsession objects, or possession of something tied to the most traumatic rupture in human history.

The result is predictable: major discoveries are often hidden long before the public ever learns they existed.

## The Black Market

Relics are compact, valuable, difficult to classify, and often politically restricted. That makes them ideal black-market goods.

Most illegal relic trade is trash: misidentified debris, contaminated scrap, forged machine-age junk, or ordinary advanced components sold under a dramatic name. But real relics do circulate. Enough of them do to keep brokers, smugglers, salvage crews, covert buyers, and criminal handlers in business.

The most valuable finds are not dramatic superweapons. They are the pieces that still do _something_: an inert-looking core with impossible internal layering, a damaged system that briefly responds to power, a navigation component that outperforms anything modern, or a sealed module nobody can open without destroying it.

Those rarely stay on the market for long.

## More Than Scrap

AI relics are not important because they are mysterious.  
They are important because they are useful.

A relic may be a scientific breakthrough, a legal nightmare, a salvage prize, a museum object, a corporate secret, or the beginning of an entire campaign. Many look like junk. Some are junk. Most are incomplete.

But in SOL 2349, history survives in fragments.

And fragments are often enough.
