## Overview

The **Free Space Tribunal** is the highest widely recognized judicial authority for major disputes arising outside clear planetary jurisdictions, tightly controlled moon systems, and fully internal corporate legal shells. In practice, it functions as something close to a supreme court for the **[[Asteroid Belt]]**, for many independent stations, and for large parts of legally ambiguous deep space.

It exists because space beyond the major centers cannot function on custom, intimidation, and improvised contract law alone. Wrecks drift across jurisdictions. Rescue obligations collide with profit. Salvage becomes ownership. Dangerous maneuvers can kill people long before any ship fires a weapon. Habitats, docks, and route operators need some higher body able to define precedent when local law is weak, overlapping, or openly contested.

The Tribunal is not a parliament, not a fleet command, and not a universal government for space. It does not rule free space in the ordinary sense. What it does provide is something more specific and often more important: **recognized legal legibility**.

A captain can ignore a ruling.  
A station can reject a claim.  
A corporation can try to bury a case.

But once the Tribunal has spoken, ignoring it often becomes expensive. Insurers take note. Docking networks take note. Salvage buyers take note. Freight contracts, recognition compacts, and arbitration bodies across human space begin adjusting around the ruling. In that sense, the Tribunal’s real power lies less in force than in the ability to make certain people, ships, and claims legally radioactive.

---

## Why It Exists

The expansion of human civilization into stations, corridors, debris fields, mining regions, and weakly governed routes created a constant legal problem: most meaningful disputes in space do not remain neatly local.

A wreck may drift through multiple recognized claim zones. A distress call may be heard by ships under different legal traditions. A collision may begin as a maneuvering dispute, become a salvage conflict, and end as an insurance war. A station may refuse docking for political reasons while claiming technical justification. A corporation may insist a case belongs to its private jurisdiction while everyone else insists it happened in free space.

Without some higher authority, these disputes would routinely escalate into violence, extortion, or endless paralysis. The **Free Space Tribunal** emerged as the answer to that problem. It does not eliminate conflict, but it gives conflict a place where it can be turned into precedent instead of wreckage.

## Nature of the Tribunal

The Tribunal is best understood as a hybrid of a supreme court for free-space disputes, a high arbitration body, and a precedent-setting authority for extra-planetary law.

It deals with matters such as:
- salvage rights
- wreck ownership
- abandoned cargo
- rescue and distress obligations
- denial of aid
- illegal or negligent maneuvers
- corridor liability
- inter-habitat disputes
- claim conflicts beyond stable local jurisdiction
- appeals from major Belt or deep-space cases

It matters most where:
- no major planet can claim obvious jurisdiction
- multiple stations or corridors are affected
- local rulings conflict
- or a case has become too economically or politically important to remain local

This is one reason it matters far beyond the Belt itself. It is formally associated with frontier law, but in practice its precedents shape much of what serious actors consider legally defensible outside strong planetary sovereignty.

---

## Jurisdiction

The **Free Space Tribunal** has no universal territorial monopoly. Its authority is recognized unevenly, but widely enough to matter.

It is most relevant in:
- the **[[Asteroid Belt]]**
- independent habitat networks
- salvage zones
- disputed wreck fields
- deep-space transit corridors
- and extra-planetary environments beyond strong Earth, Martian, or Jovian internal jurisdiction

It is weakest inside tightly controlled corporate zones, strong planetary internal legal shells, and places where hard force clearly overrides legal process.

Even actors who dislike the Tribunal often rely on it indirectly. When ownership must be proven, salvage legitimized, or liability made insurable, Tribunal precedent becomes difficult to avoid.

## How the Judges Are Chosen

The Tribunal is not elected by universal popular vote. That would be too unstable, too slow, and too vulnerable to capture by the largest population centers. At the same time, it is not simply appointed by corporations or planets, because that would destroy its legitimacy in free space.

Instead, the Tribunal uses a **layered nomination and confirmation system** built around recognized extra-planetary stakeholders.

### Sources of Nomination

Judicial candidates are typically drawn from or nominated by:
- major independent habitat networks
- recognized docking and rescue compacts
- freight and arbitration bodies
- Belt legal institutes
- free-space civic and commercial associations
- and, in smaller numbers, major planetary legal systems with accepted external credibility

No single bloc is supposed to dominate the process. That does not mean dominance never happens. It means the system is built to make it difficult.

### Confirmation

Candidates are reviewed through a confirmation process involving legal competence review, conflict-of-interest disclosure, precedent hearings, and professional scrutiny by already recognized judicial and arbitral bodies.

### Terms

Judges typically serve **long, staggered terms**, often twelve to fifteen years depending on chamber and rank, with partial rotation to preserve continuity. Removal is difficult and usually requires severe misconduct, corruption, incapacity, or overwhelming loss of recognition.

This reflects the Tribunal’s core logic: free-space law only works if precedent remains more stable than the actors trying to game it.

---

## Internal Structure

The Tribunal usually operates through several chambers rather than as one undifferentiated bench.

### Claims Chamber
Handles salvage, wreck ownership, cargo disputes, extraction claims, and questions of abandonment.

### Passage Chamber
Handles maneuver law, corridor liability, emergency right-of-way, docking disputes, denial of passage, and dangerous navigation behavior.

### Recognition and Appeals Chamber
Handles the hardest cross-jurisdictional cases, precedent review, appeals from lower free-space tribunals, and disputes involving multiple habitats or compacts.

This structure allows the Tribunal to remain specialized without becoming fragmented beyond use.

## What the Tribunal Regulates

The Tribunal’s law is dry, procedural, and built around recurring practical questions.

### Salvage

One of its most important domains is **salvage law**.

Key questions include:
- When is a wreck legally abandoned?
- When does ownership persist despite loss of control?
- When does rescue override salvage?
- Who has priority when multiple parties reach a site?
- What counts as lawful recovery rather than opportunistic seizure?
- How are high-risk or restricted finds, including certain classes of [[AI Relic]], to be handled?

The Tribunal’s rulings on salvage are among the most economically important in human space.

### Rescue and Duty to Assist

The Tribunal also defines legal obligations around distress signals and endangered crews.

Key questions include:
- Who must help?
- Under what conditions may aid be refused?
- How much risk must a ship accept to render assistance?
- When does denial of docking become criminal negligence?
- What counts as genuine incapacity and what counts as convenient refusal?

This area is politically explosive because it sits directly between survival ethics and profit logic.

### Illegal Maneuvers

The Tribunal hears cases involving dangerous burns, intimidation by maneuver, debris creation, negligent approach behavior, concealment within traffic streams, and other actions that fall short of declared warfare but still threaten lives and infrastructure.

In free space, a bad maneuver can be as deadly as a weapon. The Tribunal treats that seriously.

### Inter-Habitat and Deep-Space Jurisdiction

The Tribunal also serves as the highest recognized forum for disputes spanning multiple habitats, routes, or free-space jurisdictions. This includes cases where no local authority is strong enough to settle the matter credibly on its own.

## Limits of Enforcement

The Tribunal has no universal police force and no fleet large enough to impose its will directly. Its rulings matter because they are recognized, not because it can always send ships.

Its enforcement tools are mostly indirect:
- legal recognition or non-recognition
- claim invalidation
- docking blacklists
- loss of insurance legitimacy
- contract contamination
- sanctions through allied compacts
- precedent that makes future trade and arbitration more difficult

In practice, this means the Tribunal is strongest against actors who still need to function inside a wider system of trade, salvage sale, refueling, docking, and legal credibility.

It is weaker against actors willing to become fully rogue. But becoming fully rogue is expensive, and that is precisely where the Tribunal’s strength begins.

---

## Relationship with Other Institutions

The Tribunal is generally valued by civic Belt actors and independent habitats because it provides one of the few higher legal bodies capable of making free space more than an extraction zone governed only by contracts and force.

It is often treated more cautiously by harder, more pragmatic station networks, which prefer rulings that preserve practical order, docking logic, and salvage predictability over elegant legal theory.

Its relationship with freight-arbitration bodies is complementary but tense. Commercial councils and arbitration forums tend to focus on dense trade environments and contract resolution. The Tribunal is broader, harsher, and more concerned with frontier ambiguity, survival obligations, and the overlap between legality and physical risk.

Its relationship with [[Kaiten]] is similarly pragmatic. Kaiten respects the Tribunal only as long as it remains grounded in route reality and crew survival. It has little patience for rulings that are legally elegant but operationally absurd.

## Foundational Principles

The Tribunal is built around a body of recognized free-space constitutional principles, often referred to collectively as the **Articles of Free Passage and Claim**. These are not a planetary constitution, but they function as the closest thing free space has to one.

Some commonly cited excerpts include:

> **Article II — On Distress:**  
> A vessel or station in receipt of a verified distress signal shall not deny acknowledgment without cause demonstrable in record.

> **Article IV — On Preservation of Life:**  
> Rescue claims shall take priority over salvage claims wherever living persons remain in reasonable expectation of recovery.

> **Article VII — On Abandonment:**  
> Property in free space shall not be presumed abandoned merely by loss of contact, but may be judged constructively abandoned where no practical attempt at retention, recovery, or lawful marking has been maintained.

> **Article IX — On Hazardous Maneuver:**  
> No captain, station master, or controlling authority shall employ reckless burn, deceptive vectoring, debris shedding, or coercive approach in any manner foreseeably likely to create lethal navigational hazard outside declared combat conditions.

> **Article XII — On Refusal of Shelter:**  
> Denial of emergency docking, atmosphere, or minimal repair access to distressed persons may constitute actionable abandonment where such refusal cannot be justified by reciprocal threat of severe loss.

> **Article XV — On Common Legibility:**  
> No claim in free space shall be held secure if it cannot be rendered legible to other recognized authorities through record, mark, witness, or recoverable chain of control.

These articles are argued over endlessly. That is one reason they matter.

## Reputation

Across human space, the **Free Space Tribunal** is respected, disliked, and depended on in almost equal measure.

Captains resent it when it limits opportunism. Stations resent it when it tells them what their emergency obligations mean. Corporations resent it when frontier precedent interferes with cleaner contractual seizure. Habitat populations often trust it more than many stronger institutions, because it is one of the few bodies that still insists that law in space must mean more than whoever reached the wreck first with armed people.

Its reputation is therefore dry rather than glorious.

It is the court people complain about while still hoping the other side will be forced to obey it.

---

## Role in the Solar System

The **Free Space Tribunal** is one of the clearest signs that human civilization beyond the major worlds has matured into something more than scattered stations and private contracts. It exists because free space became too inhabited, too valuable, and too dangerous to leave entirely to custom and intimidation.

It does not rule the void.  
But it helps ensure that the void is not governed only by force.

In that sense, the Tribunal is one of the most important legal institutions in **SOL 2349**:

the place where wrecks become arguments,  
arguments become precedent,  
and precedent becomes the thin line between frontier and anarchy.
