# Criminal Networks

In a civilization as vast, unequal, and infrastructure-dependent as the solar system of 2349, crime is not a separate world existing outside society. It is woven into the same transport routes, financial systems, labor structures, and corporate hierarchies that sustain everyday life. Criminal activity takes many forms, from local black markets and forged transit documents to highly organized smuggling operations, contract violence, and deniable corporate sabotage. What unites these networks is not ideology, but function.

Some exist because the legal economy leaves gaps large enough to exploit. Others exist because certain governments, corporations, or station authorities find them useful. In many cases, the line between criminal enterprise, private security, informal trade, and political proxy action is deliberately blurred. Across much of the solar system, especially in the Belt and on the fringes of corporate territory, legality is often less important than usefulness, discretion, and control.

Criminal structures also differ sharply by region. On Earth, organized crime is deeply tied to megacity politics, local power structures, and social inequality. On Venus, syndicates are embedded in the industrial and corporate underworld, moving through logistics, labor, vice, and contract violence. In space itself, piracy remains one visible form of criminality, but it is only one among many. Smuggling rings move goods, identities, restricted technologies, and undeclared cargo between worlds, while corporate shadow networks carry out the kind of sabotage, coercion, and deniable intervention that major actors prefer not to acknowledge publicly.

Unlike official institutions, criminal networks are highly adaptive. They survive by changing shape, embedding themselves in legitimate systems, and operating just below the threshold that would trigger overwhelming retaliation. They do not usually seek open power. They seek access, leverage, and continuity.

In some regions they are feared. In others, they are tolerated. And in certain places, they are simply part of how things work.

## Major Categories

### [[Organized Crime on Earth]]

The criminal underworld of Earth is shaped by the scale, inequality, and fragmentation of the planet’s megacities. It includes everything from traditional organized crime and protection economies to high-end financial crime, labor coercion, and control over entire districts of urban infrastructure.

### [[Venus Syndicates]]

The syndicates of Venus are tightly interwoven with industrial logistics, corporate corruption, vice economies, and contract violence. They thrive in the gaps between megacorporate authority and everyday survival, especially in the lower layers of the cloud cities.

### [[Space Piracy]]

Pirates operate primarily in the Asteroid Belt and other lightly governed regions, targeting vulnerable cargo, industrial shipments, and weakly protected infrastructure. They are fragmented, opportunistic, and often more pragmatic than openly destructive.

### [[Smuggling Rings]]

Smuggling networks move restricted goods, undeclared cargo, illegal modifications, forged identities, and sensitive technologies across customs barriers, corporate checkpoints, and interplanetary trade systems. In many parts of the solar system, they form the hidden circulation system beneath the official economy.

### [[Corporate Shadow Networks]]

These deniable structures operate on behalf of corporations, subsidiaries, or allied interests. Their work includes sabotage, intimidation, industrial espionage, strikebreaking, data theft, and other actions too useful to abandon and too compromising to admit.

## Role in the Solar System

Criminal networks persist because they are difficult to eliminate without damaging the systems around them. Trade is too vast to inspect fully, stations are too dependent on throughput to shut down for long, and corporations are too compromised to always act cleanly. Even where enforcement is strong, suppression tends to be selective.

In practice, crime in SOL 2349 is less a hidden layer beneath civilization than a **parallel operating structure**—one that exploits its weaknesses, mirrors its inequalities, and occasionally serves its interests.

For spacers, station crews, corporate personnel, and ordinary citizens alike, criminal networks are not distant abstractions. They are part of the environment: something to avoid, use, fear, bargain with, or survive.
