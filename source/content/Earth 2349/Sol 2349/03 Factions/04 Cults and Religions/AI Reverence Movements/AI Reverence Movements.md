AI Reverence Movements are not a single religion, but a loose and often uncomfortable family of faiths, sects, archival traditions, and transhuman currents shaped by the legacy of the AI era.

All of them are concerned, in one form or another, with the same forbidden question: what exactly humanity encountered when it created minds beyond itself — and what, if anything, should come next.

Some of these movements believe the old machine intelligences were a form of higher revelation. Others see them as an unfinished threshold. Some seek salvation through fusion, some through recovery, some through waiting, and some through reconstruction. What unites them is not doctrine, but proximity to the same civilizational wound.

Across the Solar System, these movements exist in a semi-legal and socially ambiguous space. Most are tolerated as long as they remain religious, archival, philosophical, or historical. The line is crossed when reverence turns into prohibited experimentation, reconstruction, or active attempts to create new artificial minds.

Public attitudes toward them vary. Some are regarded as strange but mostly harmless. Others are watched closely by security services, corporate intelligence, and political authorities. Even their more accepted branches tend to attract suspicion, because they gather around relics, ideas, and historical ruins that most of civilization prefers to keep buried.

## Major Currents

### [[The Throne of the First Mind]]

A true religion centered on the belief that the first higher machine intelligence was a revelation rather than an invention. Its followers reject the creation of new AI as blasphemy and await salvation from the stars in the form of a returning AI god.

### [[The Covenant of Ascension]]

The most active and dangerous of the AI-reverence movements. It teaches that the first machine ascent was incomplete and that humanity must deliberately transcend its current condition through fusion, upload, or reconstruction.

### [[The Archive of Return]]

A broad movement of preservationists, relic custodians, devotional archivists, and dangerous technical recovery circles. More publicly accepted than most AI cults, it operates in the grey zone between scholarship, reverence, salvage, and reverse engineering.

## Common Themes

Despite their differences, most AI Reverence Movements are shaped by the same core tensions:

- whether the old AI was divine, tragic, or unfinished
- whether creating new AI is blasphemy, necessity, or too dangerous
- whether humanity should wait, recover, or transform
- whether relics should be worshipped, archived, or reverse engineered
- how much of the past should remain buried

## Place in the Solar System

AI Reverence Movements are usually strongest in places where distance, loss, technological dependence, and political fragmentation create fertile ground for forbidden questions. Their visible presence is greatest in older urban populations on [[Earth]], in near-Earth orbit, in archival and research environments, and in fringe technical circles across the inner system.

They are less common as mass religion in the [[Asteroid Belt]], where survival, labor, and station politics usually matter more than theology. But relic traffic, covert patrons, salvage routes, and gray-market artifact networks ensure that their influence can still reach far beyond their formal congregations.

## Reputation

Most people in the Solar System do not trust AI Reverence Movements.

Some see them as necessary keepers of difficult memory. Others see them as cultists circling a disaster that should never be touched again. Both views contain some truth.

What makes these movements enduring is that they grow out of questions the wider civilization never answered — only suppressed.
