## Overview

Neo-Amish Communities are small technology-restrictive religious societies found almost exclusively on [[Earth]]. Unlike most other major faiths in 2349, they have little meaningful presence in orbital habitats, on [[Mars]], or elsewhere in human space. Their way of life depends on land, local continuity, and tightly controlled community boundaries, all of which are much harder to preserve in the social and technical conditions of off-world life.

These communities are not a single church. They are a loose family of deeply conservative, tradition-oriented societies that reject large parts of modern technological civilization, especially systems that increase dependence on distant institutions, corporate infrastructure, automation, or artificial social environments.

Most Neo-Amish groups are small, insular, suspicious of outsiders, and committed to preserving what they see as a human scale of life: land, family, ritual, manual labor, and communal authority.

## Distribution

Neo-Amish Communities survive only in a handful of regions on Earth where geography, isolation, and local continuity made long-term separation from mainstream development possible.

### Island Enclaves

A few small island communities persisted into the 24th century by combining geographical isolation with strict social control. The best-known examples are found on remote North Atlantic and subpolar island chains, where limited outside access, harsh weather, and local food systems supported long continuity.

Examples often cited by outsiders include:

- isolated settlements in the **Faroe and outer North Atlantic island zone**
- a handful of religious enclaves in the **southern Pacific island fringe**, usually on smaller non-strategic islands ignored by major development corridors

These communities tend to be small, poor, and intensely conservative.

### Steppe and Interior Rural Communities

A second cluster survives in remote inland regions where large-scale modernization remained patchy and state control fluctuated over long periods. These are most often found in parts of **rural Russia**, **Central Asia**, and the **steppe belt**, where harsh land, long distances, and conservative agrarian traditions favored insular survival.

Examples include:

- scattered settlements in the **Altai–steppe fringe**
- isolated religious farming communities in the **northern Central Asian interior**

These populations are often more austere and less visible than their western counterparts.

### The Alpine Corridor

The largest and most infamous Neo-Amish zone is what outsiders call the **Alpine Corridor**: a patchwork of conservative highland communities stretching across narrow mountain valleys and upland regions in parts of **Tyrol**, **eastern Switzerland**, **Salzburg**, **Bavaria**, and adjacent alpine areas.

These regions were relatively sheltered from some of the worst direct disruption of [[The AI War]], and many local communities retained far greater continuity of settlement, property, religion, and social authority than most of urbanized Europe. Over time, this continuity hardened into ideology.

The Alpine Corridor presents itself as a bastion of endurance, order, land, family, and ancestral life. Its critics describe it less generously as xenophobic, clerical, deeply provincial, and obsessed with preserving hierarchy under the language of tradition.

Formally, the Corridor remains democratic. In practice, that democracy has been politically frozen for more than three and a half centuries. The same broad Christian-conservative political bloc has remained in power for over 350 years, sustained by tight communal networks, religious authority, inherited local influence, and the simple fact that most people who might seriously challenge the system either leave or never fully belong in the first place.

Its economy is similarly concentrated. Beyond farming and tourism, much of everyday economic life is dominated by a small number of entrenched institutions, most notably **Raiffeisen Bank** and **Lagerhaus**, which together influence or manage large parts of finance, supply, storage, local trade, agricultural inputs, and practical distribution. To outsiders, this often makes the Corridor feel less like a free rural democracy and more like a landscape-sized cooperative machine that has been running on inertia, piety, and mutual surveillance for centuries.

From the outside, the region has a contradictory reputation:

- stubbornly anti-modern, except where tourism profits are involved
- publicly hostile to outsiders, except paying guests
- morally rigid, but economically selective in what it chooses to tolerate
- deeply suspicious of urban and off-world culture
- obsessed with continuity, bloodline, property, and local belonging

The Corridor is also infamous for the stubborn persistence of traditions that many other parts of Earth abandoned long ago. It remains one of the few places where animal husbandry for meat still survives as a culturally central way of life rather than a niche luxury practice.

Its detractors also point to the uglier side of long-term isolation: extreme conservatism, dynastic family structures, closed marriage networks, and a persistent tendency toward marrying far too close within the same social pool. Locals usually deny this is unusual. Outsiders usually disagree.

One of the region’s favorite self-descriptions is that it has preserved “the old way.” One of the favorite outside jokes is that the old way apparently includes mandatory church, permanent Christian-conservative government, Raiffeisen everywhere, Lagerhaus everywhere else, compulsory skiing, hereditary politics, and making absolutely certain the lifts keep running no matter what century it is.

## Beliefs and Social Order

Neo-Amish communities differ from each other, but most share several key principles:

- technology should remain limited, local, and comprehensible
- dependence on distant systems is spiritually and socially corrupting
- communal authority matters more than individual preference
- inherited custom is safer than constant innovation
- off-world life represents moral and social drift rather than progress

These communities are usually not fully anti-technology. They are selective. They may tolerate or even quietly rely on certain tools, medicines, transport systems, or trade goods when necessary. What they reject is open-ended technological integration into everyday life.

## Relationship with the Wider World

Neo-Amish Communities have little direct influence in interplanetary politics, but they remain symbolically important on Earth. To some, they are living proof that a society can survive without surrendering completely to automation, augmentation, or corporate dependence. To others, they are reactionary enclaves preserved mostly by geography, stubbornness, and hypocrisy.

The contradiction is often real. Many communities preach separation from the wider world while still benefiting indirectly from tourism, selective trade, legal protection, or the strategic irrelevance that allows them to be left alone.

In the case of the Alpine Corridor, outsiders often note that hatred of modernity becomes much easier to maintain when wealthy visitors are willing to pay well for picturesque authenticity, mountain air, and the comforting illusion that someone, somewhere, still hates the 24th century on principle.

## Reputation

Across most of the Solar System, Neo-Amish Communities are seen as marginal curiosities rather than major religious actors. Off-world populations usually regard them with a mix of fascination, contempt, and anthropological interest.

On Earth, reactions are sharper. Supporters admire their continuity, discipline, and localism. Critics see them as insular, nationalist, authoritarian, and socially stagnant.

Both views contain some truth.
