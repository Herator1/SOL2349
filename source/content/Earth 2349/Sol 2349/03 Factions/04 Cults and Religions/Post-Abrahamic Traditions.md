## Overview

Post-Abrahamic traditions remain the largest religious sphere in human civilization. They include the evolved descendants of Christianity, Islam, Judaism, and a wide range of reformist, sectarian, and institutional branches that continued into the space age.

These traditions remain strongest on [[Earth]] and in the orbital sphere closest to it. There, old sacred geography, long-standing institutions, dense populations, and historical continuity still matter. Humanity may have expanded across the Solar System, but the cultural and institutional center of these religions remains near its planetary origin.

Further from Earth, these traditions persist, but they usually become more fragmented, more practical, and less socially dominant. In the [[Asteroid Belt]], nearly every major religion can still be found, but religion usually plays a smaller role in everyday public life than it does on Earth. Distance, contract labor, migration, and mixed station populations tend to weaken old confessional boundaries and encourage a more private or communal form of belief.

## Judaism

Judaism continues to exist in relatively recognizable form compared to many other major religions. While space habitation forced practical adaptations in calendar observance, prayer timing, and dietary law, many Jewish communities placed strong emphasis on continuity, legal interpretation, and preservation of tradition.

Jewish religious life remains concentrated on Earth, in near-Earth diaspora communities, and in established inner system settlements. Smaller Jewish communities also exist across the Solar System, especially in commercial and academic environments, but they are generally most stable where long-term communal continuity is possible.

As in earlier eras, Judaism in 2349 is less defined by centralization than by scholarship, law, memory, and community continuity.

## Christianity

Christianity remains the most internally diverse part of the Post-Abrahamic sphere. It includes old institutional churches, orbital dioceses, migrant churches, monastic communities, reform movements, and countless sectarian offshoots.

One of its most visible symbolic developments is the continued existence of a papacy with an orbital presence. The **Vatican Orbital Mission** serves as an extension of the Church’s activity in near-Earth space and among major habitat populations. It does not replace Rome, but reflects the fact that the Church followed humanity into orbit rather than remaining purely planet-bound.

Christian traditions remain especially strong on Earth, in orbital habitats close to Earth, and in older settlements with stable family structures. Farther out, Christian communities still exist, but are more likely to appear as local chapels, migrant churches, station fellowships, or sectarian enclaves rather than dominant public institutions.

## Islam

Islam remains one of the great religious traditions of the Solar System and has likewise diversified under the conditions of interplanetary life. Questions of prayer direction, fasting, pilgrimage, legal jurisdiction, and communal authority became especially important once Muslim communities spread into orbit and beyond.

Islam remains strongest on Earth and in near-Earth and Lunar populations with strong historical and cultural continuity. In these regions, major traditions and scholarly institutions remain influential. Further into the Solar System, Muslim communities often adapt through practical rulings, local leadership, and flexible interpretation shaped by work cycles, transport schedules, and habitat conditions.

As with Christianity, Islam in the Belt and outer settlements is still present, but often less institutionally central and more community-based than in the old heartlands.

## Distribution Across the Solar System

The overall pattern is clear: the older Abrahamic religions remain most influential where humanity stayed closest to Earth, both physically and culturally.

- On **Earth**, they remain major civilizational traditions.
- In **near-Earth orbit** and on **Luna**, they remain highly visible and institutionally organized.
- On **Mars**, they persist but compete more strongly with secular civic identities and newer social formations.
- In the **Belt**, all of them are represented, but usually as minority communities within a harsher, more fragmented, more transactional social environment.
- In the **outer system**, old religions still exist, but newer spiritual movements are generally more likely to emerge and gain influence.

## Social Role

In many parts of Earth and the inner system, Post-Abrahamic institutions are still more than places of worship. They provide continuity, charity, education, counseling, funerary rites, and community identity.

Their role tends to weaken with distance from Earth. In the Belt especially, religion is usually present but rarely dominant. Station identity, work contracts, language mixing, and immediate survival concerns tend to matter more in daily life than formal confessional belonging.

That does not mean faith disappears. It becomes more private, more local, and often less institutionally powerful.

## Internal Divisions

The Post-Abrahamic sphere remains deeply diverse and often divided. Major points of disagreement include:

- how far ritual life can be adapted to life in space
- whether extensive body modification violates divine intention
- how much authority Earth-based institutions still possess
- how religion should function in mixed and transient habitat societies
- how to respond to transhumanist thought and AI-related taboo questions

These divisions have produced countless reform movements, sects, local schools, and habitat-specific communities across the Solar System.
