# Solar Communion

| Category | Information |
|---|---|
| Type | New religious movement |
| Estimated Followers | ~300–400 million |
| Core Beliefs | The Sun as the source of life, unity, and spiritual orientation for humanity |
| Major Locations | Orbital habitats, solar observatories, [[Mercury]], artistic and countercultural communities |
| Sacred Places | Solar temples, observatories, heliocentric shrines, light sanctuaries |

## Overview

Solar Communion is one of the best-known new religious movements of the space age. Its followers believe that humanity became a truly unified species only when it left Earth and began to live consciously within the greater structure of the Solar System.

At the center of the movement stands the Sun. For believers, it is not merely a star in the astrophysical sense, but the visible source of life, energy, order, and common belonging. Humanity may be politically divided, economically fragmented, and culturally dispersed, but all human life in the Solar System still depends on one star.

For that reason, Solar Communion treats the Sun as both physical reality and spiritual symbol. Some branches speak of reverence rather than worship. Others openly treat the Sun as a sacred presence.

## Character

To outsiders, Solar Communion often appears as a loose blend of spiritual collectivism, cosmic environmentalism, communal ritual, and countercultural lifestyle politics. Its communities are widely associated with artists, idealists, drifters, solar engineers, long-term spacers, relationship communes, and people dissatisfied with the rigid social order of corporate civilization.

Many of its settlements and congregations are known for relaxed social norms, ceremonial partnerships, collective child-rearing, and highly theatrical public rites. Group marriages, multi-partner unions, and ritualized communal vows are common in some branches, especially in habitats where believers deliberately reject older legal or religious family models.

This has given the movement a reputation, fair or not, as a religion of space hippies, sun mystics, and impractical romantics.

## Beliefs

Solar Communion is not fully unified, but several ideas are widespread:

- humanity is a solar species
- the Sun is the common center of human existence
- artificial divisions between worlds, habitats, and peoples are spiritually false
- life should be lived in conscious harmony with larger cycles of light, energy, and motion
- extreme corporate individualism has alienated humanity from its cosmic condition

Some branches remain vague and mystical. Others are surprisingly disciplined and philosophical. A few verge on open cult behavior around charismatic leaders or closed communal living.

## Rituals and Practices

Common practices include:

- sunrise observances and light rituals
- collective meditations during orbital dawn or major conjunctions
- ceremonies held in direct solar viewports or filtered light chambers
- communal marriage rites and bond ceremonies
- seasonal festivals tied to local orbital cycles
- naming rituals based on light, heat, or celestial alignment

Large public ceremonies can be visually striking and sometimes deliberately provocative, especially in conservative habitats. Loose robes, gold-white reflective fabrics, body paint, and carefully staged illumination are common parts of Solar Communion aesthetics.

## Social Role

Solar Communion is strongest in places where people feel detached from older planetary identities but still seek belonging, transcendence, and emotional community. It is especially attractive to populations shaped by mobility, unstable kinship structures, and life in artificial environments.

In some habitats it functions mostly as a harmless spiritual subculture. In others it has become a serious communal institution with schools, rituals, marriage networks, and mutual aid systems.

Its critics accuse it of anti-institutional irresponsibility, shallow mysticism, and emotional manipulation. Its followers argue that it offers something corporate society does not: meaning, beauty, shared ritual, and a sense that human life in space can be more than survival and contract work.

## Reputation

Solar Communion has a mixed reputation across the Solar System.

- On [[Earth]], it is often dismissed as a fashionable space-born cult.
- On [[Mars]], it is tolerated but rarely taken fully seriously outside artistic or fringe circles.
- In orbital habitats, it ranges from respectable spiritual community to embarrassing counterculture scene.
- In the [[Asteroid Belt]], small Solar Communion circles exist, but survival, labor, and station politics usually matter more than cosmic idealism.

For many people, Solar Communion is not threatening. It is simply a little too sincere, a little too theatrical, and a little too convinced that staring at the Sun can fix civilization.
