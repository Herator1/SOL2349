# [[Earth Megacities]]

Modern Earth is shaped above all by its **megacities**: vast urban civilizations whose scale exceeds the old idea of the city by such a degree that they function more like regional systems than municipal entities. They dominate population, governance, trade, finance, culture, infrastructure, and access to orbital civilization.

But Earth is not a planet of megacities alone.

Even in 2349, billions still live outside the great urban giants—in secondary cities, coastal belts, agricultural regions, infrastructure corridors, underwater settlements, smaller arcology zones, and the partially restored landscapes of the [[Rewilded North]]. The megacities may define the political and economic image of Earth, but they do not contain all of it.

Still, they contain a great deal.

Based on the major megacities currently defined in the setting, roughly **5.5 to 7.3 billion people** already live within the largest urban systems described so far. Given an Earth population of around **11 to 13 billion**, that suggests roughly **4.5 to 7.5 billion** people still live outside these currently listed megacities—either in other large urban regions not yet detailed, or in smaller but still highly significant population systems across the planet.

This makes Earth a world where the megacity is not the exception.  
It is one of the dominant human settlement forms.

## Currently Defined Major Megacities

### [[Delhi Prime]]
**~2.6–3.0 billion**  
The largest continuous urban region on Earth and the single biggest population concentration in the Solar System. Stable, immense, and highly managed, Delhi Prime represents civilization at impossible scale—functional, quiet, and socially constraining.

### [[Greater Lagos]]
**~1.1–1.6 billion**  
A vast hybrid water-land megacity of adaptation, trade, repair, and human improvisation. Greater Lagos is one of Earth’s most alive and resilient urban regions, deeply tied to orbital labor pipelines, agricultural flows, and coastal commerce.

### [[Mombasa Corridor]]
**~0.7–1.1 billion**  
A decentralized East African–Arabian trade civilization built on ports, markets, orbital logistics, and negotiated disorder. Open, culturally vibrant, and difficult to govern, it remains one of the freest and most dynamic great cities on Earth.

### [[Nova São Paulo]]
**~0.9–1.3 billion**  
The financial capital of Earth and the clearest expression of vertical inequality in the planetary urban system. It is a city of extreme wealth above, hard survival below, and capital power made physically visible.

### [[New Sydney Arc]]
**~180–260 million**  
The smallest of the great megacities, but the richest per capita and one of the most tightly controlled. Clean, wealthy, highly surveilled, and quietly authoritarian, it represents prosperity through exclusion and order.

## Population Context

The currently defined megacities account for approximately:

- **~5.48–7.26 billion people total**

That means that, depending on final calibration and future additions to the setting, approximately:

- **~4.5–7.5 billion people**

still live **outside these currently described megacities**.

This remaining population is likely distributed across:
- other major urban regions not yet fully defined
- secondary cities and linked coastal belts
- agricultural and inland infrastructure zones
- [[Underwater Cities]]
- smaller arcology systems
- partially restored northern territories in the [[Rewilded North]]

---

## Role on Earth

Earth’s megacities are not just large cities. They are the main engines through which the home world remains politically, culturally, and economically relevant in a Solar System where off-world infrastructure increasingly defines the frontier.

They are overcrowded, unequal, adaptive, powerful, and deeply varied in character.  
Together, they form the real human geography of Earth in 2349.
