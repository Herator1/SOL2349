The **Atlantic Arcologies** are a network of **coastal-linked hybrid habitats** built along major Atlantic shorelines, combining above-water urban structures, semi-submerged infrastructure, subsea industrial systems, and marine logistics platforms. They are not fully separate underwater cities in the dramatic sense, but extensions of coastal civilization into the sea.

Their importance lies mainly in three areas: **seabed resource extraction, water-based agriculture, and maritime trade**.

Many of these arcologies are directly tied to nearby coastal megacities, ports, or industrial corridors. In practice, large parts of their population do not live permanently under water. Instead, many residents live on the coast or in partially elevated hybrid districts and commute into offshore or subsea work zones for extraction, maintenance, marine engineering, food production, and logistics. This gives the Atlantic Arcologies a distinct social character: less isolated than deep-ocean habitats, but more marine and infrastructural than ordinary coastal cities.

Water agriculture is especially important in some regions, with controlled marine cultivation, protein production, algae systems, and other intensive aquatic food infrastructures integrated into broader trade networks. Combined with offshore industry and seabed resource systems, this makes the Atlantic Arcologies some of the most economically useful underwater-adjacent habitats on Earth.

They are not the deepest or most extreme subsea environments.  
They are the places where the ocean has become part of everyday coastal industry.
