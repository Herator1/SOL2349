Space stations and docking facilities are the **connective tissue of human civilization beyond planetary surfaces**. They serve as hubs for transportation, trade, maintenance, administration, and daily life, linking distant worlds through a vast network of orbital infrastructure. From small refueling platforms to massive rotating habitats, stations provide life support, cargo handling, ship repair, crew rotation, and the countless support functions that make slow interplanetary civilization possible.  
  
Broadly speaking, orbital infrastructure in **SOL 2349** falls into two major categories. **Orbital stations** are built directly around planets, moons, or industrial worlds, where they support logistics, trade, energy transfer, manufacturing, and administration. **Lagrange stations**, by contrast, occupy positions of gravitational stability between large bodies and tend to function as transfer hubs, staging areas, observation sites, communication nodes, or distributed support infrastructure.  
  
Together, these systems form the practical framework that keeps interplanetary civilization running.

# Orbital Stations  
  
Orbital stations are built where traffic, industry, and energy demand are highest. Some exist primarily to handle cargo and docking, while others function as corporate headquarters, industrial command centers, diplomatic nodes, or large-scale permanent habitats. In many parts of the Solar System, orbital stations matter more than the planetary surface below them.

# Role in the Solar System  
  
Stations, docks, and transfer hubs form the **operational backbone of interplanetary civilization**. Planets and moons may dominate maps and political narratives, but it is orbital infrastructure that allows civilization to function across distance. These are the places where cargo is sorted, ships are repaired, routes are coordinated, contracts are enforced, and large systems remain connected.
