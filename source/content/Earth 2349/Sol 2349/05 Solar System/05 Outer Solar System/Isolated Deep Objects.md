Beyond the more structured regions of the [[Kuiper Belt]] and the unstable dynamics of the [[Scattered Disc]], the outer solar system contains a number of **isolated objects**—dwarf planets, minor bodies, and distant remnants that exist at the margins of known orbital structure.

These objects do not form a continuous region. They appear instead as **widely separated points in deep space**, often following extreme or unusual orbits that carry them far beyond the already thinly inhabited outer system. Some pass through the far edges of the Kuiper Belt before vanishing again into darkness for centuries or longer. What defines them is not composition, but **distance, irregularity, and isolation**.

The spaces between them are immense. There are no natural trade routes, no shared infrastructure, and no meaningful regional identity connecting one to the next. Reaching one of these bodies is always a deliberate act of navigation and intent—never something encountered casually in transit.

Because of this, they occupy a unique place in the Solar System. They are not part of any stable network. They are destinations that must be **specifically chosen**.

---

# Role in the Setting

In **SOL 2349**, isolated deep objects function as **natural story anchors in otherwise empty space**. Their distance, weak oversight, and total disconnection from larger systems make them ideal locations for anything that requires separation from ordinary human civilization.

A single object can host a research station, a covert installation, a listening post, a hidden cache, a failed colony experiment, or the remains of a long-forgotten expedition—completely detached from any broader political or logistical framework.

In a region defined by emptiness, these objects become rare points of narrative gravity.  
Not because they are important by themselves, but because there is almost nothing else around them.
