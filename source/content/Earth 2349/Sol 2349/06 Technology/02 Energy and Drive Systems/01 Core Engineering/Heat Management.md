## 🌡️ Heat Management & Thermal Control

In space, heat is a far greater problem than cold. There is no air to carry it away, no convection, no simple cooling — only radiation. Every watt of energy generated inside a ship must ultimately be emitted as heat into space. In a fusion-powered vessel, this becomes one of the central engineering challenges.

Under normal operation, a significant portion of thermal load is buffered internally. **Propellant mass** plays a crucial role here: it absorbs excess heat and is then expelled during thrust, effectively carrying energy away from the ship. In lower activity phases, or when propellant reserves are limited, parts of the **ship’s structure itself** act as temporary heat sinks, soaking up thermal energy until it can be radiated away. This is not sustainable indefinitely — it merely buys time.

The only true way to remove heat is through **radiators**. These are large external surfaces designed to emit infrared radiation into space. Most ships carry **fixed radiator arrays** integrated into their hull or mounted along structural booms, positioned to avoid direct exposure to drive exhaust and to maintain clear emission angles.

However, fixed radiators alone are not sufficient for peak operations. During high-thrust burns or energy-intensive maneuvers, ships deploy additional systems such as **extendable radiator panels** or more advanced **droplet radiators**. The latter operate by releasing streams or clouds of heated liquid — often metallic or high-temperature-stable fluids — which radiate heat efficiently before being recollected. These systems dramatically increase heat rejection capacity, but they are complex, vulnerable, and generally only used when absolutely necessary.

Thermal management is not just about efficiency — it is about survival. Heat builds up continuously during operation, and if it cannot be removed fast enough, systems begin to fail. Electronics degrade, structural materials weaken, and reactor stability becomes increasingly difficult to maintain. Unlike many other failures, overheating is often cumulative and irreversible once critical thresholds are crossed.

> **Radiators destroyed = ship lost.**

Damage to radiator systems is among the most dangerous scenarios a vessel can face. Without the ability to reject heat, even a fully functional reactor becomes unusable within a short timeframe. The ship is forced to shut down high-energy systems, drift, and hope that passive cooling and remaining capacity are enough to avoid total system collapse.

For this reason, radiators are both **critical infrastructure and strategic vulnerability**. They are protected, redundantly designed, and carefully managed — but they can never be fully armored without compromising their function.

In SOL 2349, every burn, every system load, and every operational decision is ultimately constrained by one simple truth: 
**Heat must go somewhere.**
