# Technology Lore

The world of **SOL 2349** is built on technology that feels powerful, dangerous, and expensive rather than magical. Ships cross the Solar System, stations survive in vacuum, habitats keep millions alive, and whole worlds depend on energy, logistics, shielding, and industrial systems operating at enormous scale. But none of it is effortless. Distance still matters. Heat still matters. Radiation still matters. Mass still matters.

This section of the wiki collects the **technical lore** of the setting: the engineering logic, physical constraints, and scientific ideas that shape everyday life in human space. These pages are meant to explain complex topics in a way that is readable, grounded, and useful for worldbuilding, campaigns, and stories.

You will not get your PhD in astrophysics from reading them.  
But you will get a much better sense of **why the Solar System of 2349 looks and works the way it does**.

Some chapters are broad overviews. Others focus on specific systems such as propulsion, shielding, weapons, mining, or augmentation. Together, they form the technical backbone of the setting and help explain why life in **SOL 2349** is often harsh, improvised, and infrastructure-dependent even in a highly advanced age.

Many of these topics are also highly relevant at the table. Travel times, beam power, reactor failures, salvage economics, cybernetic strain, kinetic impacts, or a badly designed habitat are not just background flavor. They can become plot points, mission constraints, hazards, and opportunities.

---

## Core Themes of Technology in SOL 2349

A few broad principles shape almost everything in this section:

- technology follows **physical logic**, not convenience  
- advanced systems are powerful, but usually expensive, maintenance-heavy, and specialized  
- humanity has solved many engineering problems, but not abolished heat, radiation, gravity, distance, or material limits  
- infrastructure matters as much as invention  
- many technologies are best understood not as gadgets, but as parts of larger industrial systems  

This is not a setting of miracle devices that make hard problems disappear.  
It is a setting of machines built to survive hard problems.

## How to Use This Section

You can read these pages in different ways depending on what you need.

If you want a broad understanding of how the setting works, start with **[[Basic Technological Concepts of Sol 2349]]** and move outward from there.

If you are preparing a campaign or writing scenes, it is often more useful to jump directly to the practical topic that matters:
- travel and propulsion
- radiation and habitat safety
- cybernetics and body modification
- combat systems
- mining, salvage, and industrial logistics
- major infrastructural projects

Some pages are more immediately relevant to everyday play than others. A torch drive, a failing radiator, a pressure breach, a cargo dispute, or a dangerous relic component may matter more to your story than the full theory behind them. That is fine. This section exists to support the setting, not to overwhelm it.

---

## Tone and Intent

These chapters are written for readers who enjoy hard-science-fiction ideas but still want them presented in clear, readable language. The goal is not textbook completeness. The goal is to make the technology of **SOL 2349** feel believable, understandable, and fun to engage with.

In other words:

**enough science to make the world feel real**  
**enough clarity to keep it usable**  
**and enough style to make it worth reading**
