# 🌩️ **OUTCOMES AND REWARDS**

## **Resolution**

By the end of Cloudfall, the players have not solved Aphrodite Prime. They have only forced one decision inside it.

That matters.

AP-LC-47 is only one lower cluster in a city built on selective maintenance, filtered visibility, and unequal stability. But the players have reached the point where the city’s logic becomes visible, and where their actions can change how that logic is applied — at least in one case, and at least for a moment.

No ending should feel clean. None of the available outcomes represent a true victory, because the system itself remains larger than the players. Aphrodite Prime does not collapse, reform, or become moral because of one confrontation. It absorbs shocks, redistributes strain, updates procedures, and continues.

That is exactly what makes the choice meaningful.

The players do not decide whether the city survives.  
They decide what survives **within** it.

The consequences should unfold quickly, but not theatrically. Even in crisis, Aphrodite Prime reacts like a city-machine: systems shift, corridors reroute, districts are reprioritized, narratives harden, and people adapt because they have to.

The tone of the ending should therefore remain controlled, even when the human cost is obvious.

## **Outcome A — Maintain Stability**

The players accept Varga’s logic, or at least choose not to break it. They leave the smoothing layer intact, allow the official narrative to hold, and do not force the city to acknowledge AP-LC-47 as an openly destabilizing problem.

This is not passive inaction. It is active cooperation with managed continuity.

### **Read-Aloud — Maintain Stability**

> The system settles back into its rhythm without resistance. Data flows cleanly, without deviation. Corridor displays remain stable, legible, unquestioned.
> 
> AP-LC-47 continues to operate, just as before. The fluctuations remain — small, controlled, unacknowledged. Maintenance crews keep working. Residents keep adjusting. Nothing stops.
> 
> Life does not improve.
> 
> It simply continues under a version of the truth the city can afford.

### **What Happens**

In practical terms, AP-LC-47 remains active, but under continued managed decline. Maintenance remains insufficient. The smoothing layer continues to suppress escalation. No major external intervention is triggered. The city stays stable, at least by its own preferred definition.

Velasco understands exactly what has happened. He may not confront the players directly, but he will know they had a chance to force change and chose not to. His disappointment should be quieter than anger.

Yara, if still present, continues working under the same pressure as before — but now with full certainty that nobody above intends to fix the problem as long as it remains containable.

### **What the Players Gain**

Working with Varga should carry clear, real benefits.

Their actions at Node 3 are quietly reclassified as emergency diagnostics rather than sabotage. Their employer is protected. Audit trails are narrowed or sanitized. The crew may receive a bonus, contract extension, or privileged standing for future “sensitive” work in Aphrodite Prime or similar Venusian environments.

They gain:

- continued access to corporate-level contracts
- increased trust from system-aligned actors
- protection from direct legal or procedural fallout
- a reputation for discretion and usefulness under pressure

This path may open future jobs that are profitable, prestigious, and morally uncomfortable.

### **Long-Term Tone**

The players walk away with security, money, and institutional protection — and with the knowledge that they preserved a version of order built on selective abandonment.

That should hurt.

## **Outcome B — Expose the Truth**

The players reject the city’s preferred version of events and force raw data, unsmoothed instability, or suppressed warning conditions into broader visibility. They may do this through public-facing displays, internal alert systems, supervisory relays, external channels, or a combination of all three.

This is the most direct rejection of Varga’s logic. It is also the outcome most likely to make the city turn on them personally.

### **Read-Aloud — Expose the Truth**

> For a moment, the system stops pretending.
> 
> Data fractures across displays and interfaces. Values drift, spike, contradict one another. AP-LC-47 appears in raw form — unstable, unresolved, impossible to smooth quickly enough.
> 
> The response is immediate. Not silence, but sound. Not control, but movement. Doors cycle. Alerts propagate. Systems begin to react faster than the city can narrate them.
> 
> Aphrodite Prime does not collapse.
> 
> But for the first time, it is forced to admit that stability was never the same thing as safety.

### **What Happens**

The city reacts quickly, but unevenly. Partial emergency responses spool up. Lower-level evacuation or containment protocols may begin in fragmented form. Some internal staff now see the truth. Some public displays reveal enough to damage confidence. The illusion of perfect control cracks.

People in AP-LC-47 now have a better chance of survival — but not a guaranteed one. Some residents are moved, others are delayed, and some will inevitably be caught in the confusion of a system that reacts faster to visible instability than to hidden suffering.

Velasco becomes active immediately, organizing, warning, pushing people to move. Yara, if alive, becomes a crucial witness and potential future ally. Both now understand not only that the system is cruel, but how it protects itself.

### **What the Players Risk**

This is the path where Varga’s warning becomes real.

The city now has every reason to frame the event around the players’ interference. Their unauthorized access to Node 3 becomes a clean narrative anchor for blame. Their employer may be investigated, restricted, fined, or quietly blacklisted. Their routes, names, and contract records are all now part of the incident trail.

They may save lives — but they do not get to do it anonymously.

### **What the Players Gain**

They gain:

- a strong reputation among lower-level populations and anti-system actors
- access to underground or informal networks
- potential leverage with outside civic or watchdog bodies
- moral legitimacy in the eyes of those directly affected

But they likely lose:

- easy access to corporate contracts
- procedural protection
- clean legal standing inside Aphrodite Prime

This outcome should feel brave, necessary, and dangerous.

### **Long-Term Tone**

The players become visible to the city in the worst way and to its residents in the best way. They may have forced a more honest response — but honesty in Aphrodite Prime is expensive.

## **Outcome C — Controlled Resolution**

The players engage Varga on system terms and force a compromise. They do not shatter the city’s logic, but they succeed in bending it. This is the most politically subtle outcome and, in many ways, the most “Venusian.”

It preserves the system while exposing its priorities.

### **Read-Aloud — Controlled Resolution**

> The system adjusts — not fully, not honestly, but with purpose.
> 
> Values shift across the network. Warnings appear, but remain contained. Priority routes change. Support capacity is moved. Below, movement begins. Not panic. Not rescue. Direction.
> 
> Some sectors are stabilized long enough to clear. Some residents are moved first. Some are told to wait.
> 
> The structure holds.
> 
> Not because it was saved.
> 
> But because someone decided exactly how much of it would be.

### **What Happens**

AP-LC-47 receives some form of controlled intervention:

- a stabilization window
- selective evacuation
- temporary support allocation
- prioritized extraction of the most vulnerable or most “valuable” sectors

This is the ending where the city most openly reveals its inequality. Help arrives, but not equally. Access to safety depends on category, location, dependency, usefulness, and timing.

Velasco may accept the outcome because it saves people, but he should do so without satisfaction. He knows what was traded away to make it happen. Yara survives and understands more clearly than ever that even successful intervention on Venus often looks like managed triage rather than justice.

### **What the Players Gain**

This outcome combines risk and reward in the most complex way.

Because they stayed inside the system enough to negotiate, Varga may still protect them procedurally. Their employer may survive. Their trail may be softened. They may even gain influence with people who now recognize them as contractors capable of operating at the edge of the system without collapsing it.

They gain:

- balanced reputation across corporate and informal spheres
- continued access to future work
- a chance at long-term leverage inside system-level decisions
- recognition as pragmatic operators who can alter outcomes without breaking structure

This is the outcome most likely to produce future allies **and** future enemies.

### **Long-Term Tone**

The players changed the outcome, but not the rules. They preserved lives without destroying the machine that sorted those lives into categories. That should feel like a success with a bitter aftertaste.

## **Outcome D — Force Escalation**

The players reject both containment and compromise and push the system beyond its designed ability to absorb or narrate the event. This is not simple exposure. It is active disruption: a break in synchronization, a forced cascade, a destabilization that the city cannot fully route away in time.

This is the most dangerous and least controllable ending.

### **Read-Aloud — Force Escalation**

> The system reacts too late.
> 
> For a moment, everything desynchronizes. Data no longer aligns. Outputs no longer match inputs. Controlled delay gives way to raw response.
> 
> Then the structure shifts.
> 
> Not gradually. Not predictably.
> 
> AP-LC-47 appears one last time — not as a system state, but as movement.
> 
> The city does not fail.
> 
> It adapts.
> 
> But something has already been lost.

### **What Happens**

AP-LC-47 destabilizes rapidly. Parts of the cluster may drop, shear, or become structurally unsafe faster than even emergency response can meaningfully compensate for. The city absorbs the damage elsewhere, but the district itself pays the immediate price.

There may be large casualties. Velasco may die, disappear, or survive in a broken and diminished role depending on how hard you want the ending to land. Yara’s fate can also remain contingent on the players’ earlier choices and how much practical help they gave before the final act.

The event becomes part of Aphrodite Prime’s history: recorded, contained, and publicly reframed as a systems incident or sabotage event depending on how the city needs it to read.

### **What the Players Gain**

This path should not be empty. It is costly, but it can still produce rewards of a darker kind.

They may gain:

- access to fragmented or restricted system data ripped loose during the disruption
- leverage among fringe operators, smugglers, black-market analysts, or hostile watchdog actors
- a reputation as dangerous, unpredictable, and capable of hurting structures larger than themselves

They almost certainly lose:

- safe access to formal systems
- easy movement inside Venusian administrative space
- protection from legal or private retaliation

### **Long-Term Tone**

This ending should feel like truth weaponized badly. The players refused the city’s terms and broke its ability to manage the moment cleanly — but in doing so, they may also have broken the people they wanted to save.

## **Player-Facing Consequences Across All Outcomes**

One thing should be consistent across all endings: the players themselves are changed by this.

Even if they survive materially intact, they have crossed a line. They have seen how a floating Venusian megacity distributes safety, hides strain, and decides who gets preserved. They now know that stability in Aphrodite Prime is not neutral. It is political, economic, and brutally selective.

Depending on the outcome, this may mean:

- they are rewarded and quietly absorbed into more sensitive work
- they become trusted by people below and distrusted by systems above
- they emerge as pragmatic intermediaries who can move between layers
- they become marked, watched, or quietly hunted by administrative actors

The important thing is that the ending should not just change AP-LC-47.  
It should change the players’ relationship to the world.

## **Final Note for the GM**

No ending in Cloudfall should feel like “the good ending.”

Each one represents a different relationship with the system:

- acceptance
- exposure
- compromise
- disruption

Aphrodite Prime continues in all cases. Venus continues. Production continues. Administration continues. Freight routes continue. The city-machine survives.

What changes is how visible the cost becomes, who pays it, and whether the players remain inside the system’s protection or outside it.

That is the real reward structure of the scenario.

### **Closing Line**

> The system continues.
> 
> The difference lies in what it is forced to admit
