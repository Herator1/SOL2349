## Purpose of This Page

This page gives the GM the structural overview of **Ghost Station**.

It is not a script.  
It is the scenario backbone.

Use it to understand how the situation begins, how pressure escalates, what the main discoveries are, and how the player characters can shape the outcome.

Ghost Station works best when the GM treats it as a failing situation already in motion.

---

# Core Situation

The crew of the **Asterion** is completing a routine asteroid survey in the Belt. Only one target remains on the scan route.

As they approach the final asteroid, they detect a weak and corrupted signal. It appears at first like a degraded distress call, but the signal is incomplete, distorted, and difficult to locate precisely.

Further analysis reveals that the transmission is not coming from the asteroid itself. It is coming from a damaged station built into a hollowed asteroid body.

The station’s communication array is badly damaged. It cannot send a clear message. Instead, it broadcasts fragments: broken identifiers, emergency pings, corrupted audio, partial telemetry, and repeating distress markers.

As the Asterion approaches, it enters a limited debris field. The debris comes from the station itself: torn hull plating, broken docking hardware, antenna fragments, cargo equipment, container parts, and wreckage from recent fighting.

The ship is struck and damaged.

The crew now has a practical problem: they cannot simply complete the survey and leave. They need information, repairs, parts, shelter, or another way out. The station is dangerous, but it may also be their best chance of survival.

---

# What Really Happened

The asteroid was hollowed out long ago by independent miner crews. After the original mining operation ended, the remaining cavity and infrastructure were reused by other groups.

Over time, the site became a hidden station: part salvage depot, part pirate refuge, part black-market transfer point, part failing habitat.

The station was never built as one clean project. It is a layered retrofit of old mining infrastructure, patched pressure systems, improvised habitat modules, cargo equipment, hidden docking structures, and undocumented modifications.

Recently, a sealed container containing an [[AI Relic]] or AI-era technological fragment was brought into the station.

The container triggered a final split inside the station’s old order.

**Arman Volkov**, an old station patriarch and one of the figures who helped keep the station alive for years, tried to prevent the container from leaving. His daughter, **Mira Volkov**, broke away with a smaller faction and tried to use the container, the freighter, or both as a way out.

The conflict escalated inside fragile infrastructure.

Weapons fire, sabotage, pressure loss, power failures, and panic damaged critical station systems. The communication array was crippled. The Main Docking Spine was damaged. Cargo systems failed. The rotating habitat became unstable. Survivors scattered, fortified, hid, or tried to seize control of remaining resources.

During the fighting, the container was moved, redirected, hidden, lost, or abandoned.

Nobody knows exactly where it is now.

The artifact is not in plain sight.  
It is not automatically found.  
It is not simply sitting inside the freighter.

The player characters can only find it through active investigation.

# Structural Truths

Use these truths to keep the scenario consistent.

## The Station Is Built Inside a Hollowed Asteroid

The asteroid itself is mostly old mining volume, shielding, and industrial history. The important play space is the station built inside and through it.

## The Main Docking Spine Is What the Crew Sees First

The Main Docking Spine protrudes from the asteroid and forms the first visible access route. It is damaged, exposed, and surrounded by limited station debris.

## The Central Spine Is the Internal Anchor

Once inside, the Central Spine connects docking, cargo, maintenance, spin-transfer, old mining routes, and the path toward the Secondary Spine.

It should remain the players’ main spatial reference point.

## The Freighter Is Not Obvious at First

The freighter is docked at a **Secondary Spine / Freighter Berth** deeper in the station structure.

It is offset from the first approach vector and partly hidden by rock, shadow, station superstructure, and damaged external scaffolding.

It should be discovered through scans, station records, survivor testimony, exploration, or a changed viewing angle.

## The Container Is Missing

The container was central to the conflict, but it is no longer where it was expected to be.

Finding it requires reconstruction: cargo logs, video fragments, sensor glitches, physical traces, survivor testimony, and faction behavior.

# Scenario Flow

Ghost Station is structured in three acts.

Each act has a main function:

- **Act 1** traps the crew in the situation.
- **Act 2** lets them understand the station and its conflict.
- **Act 3** gives them a pressure sandbox where they choose what matters.

The GM should not force the players toward one solution.

The scenario works best when the crew has several bad options and limited time to decide between them.

---

# Act 1 — The Last Survey Target

## Function

Act 1 establishes normal operations, introduces the signal, damages the Asterion, and forces the crew toward the station.

The mood should shift from routine work to technical uncertainty to immediate survival pressure.

## Starting Situation

The Asterion is approaching the final asteroid on its assigned scan route.

The mission is almost complete. The crew expects a standard pass: scan, classify, record, transmit, leave.

The target appears unremarkable at first. It is a depleted rock body with old surface irregularities and no obvious active beacon.

Then the sensors detect a weak corrupted signal.

## Key Beats

### 1. Routine Survey Pass

The crew performs standard scans of the final asteroid.

This establishes their professional role and gives technical characters something useful to do before the danger begins.

Possible early findings include old mining scars, irregular internal voids, faint thermal irregularities, weak radio noise, traces of artificial metal, and no stable transponder lock.

At this stage, the station should not be fully obvious.

The crew should suspect that something is hidden or damaged, not instantly understand the whole situation.

### 2. Corrupted Signal

The signal resolves into a damaged transmission from the station’s broken communication array.

It may include partial station identifiers, emergency pings, corrupted telemetry, old docking handshake attempts, unreadable distress codes, and broken audio fragments.

The signal should feel technical and damaged, not supernatural.

The crew may try to clean it up, triangulate it, identify the source, or determine whether it is automated or live.

### 3. Station Contact

Further scans reveal that the asteroid has been hollowed out and modified.

The clearest visible structure is the damaged Main Docking Spine protruding from the asteroid shell. Around it drifts a limited but dangerous debris field.

The station is partially hidden by the asteroid itself, old mining geometry, shadow, and wreckage.

The freighter should not be clearly visible yet.

### 4. Debris Strike

As the Asterion approaches or maneuvers to gather better data, it is struck by station debris.

This should not feel like random punishment. It is the direct consequence of entering an unstable debris field around a damaged station.

The strike may damage maneuvering, sensors, communications, radiators, docking hardware, hull integrity, power routing, or navigation alignment.

The Asterion is not destroyed.

It is damaged enough that leaving immediately is dangerous, uncertain, or impractical without repairs.

### 5. Decision to Board

The crew must decide how to proceed.

They may inspect the damage, scan for a safe approach, send a drone, attempt remote contact, prepare EVA, or identify a docking route.

The GM should make one thing clear:

The station is risky, but ignoring it does not solve the crew’s problem.

## Required Act 1 Outcomes

By the end of Act 1, the players should know:

- the final asteroid is not just a rock
- a damaged station is built into it
- the signal comes from the station
- the debris field comes from the station
- the Asterion is damaged
- boarding the station is the most practical path forward

They do not yet need to know:

- the full history of the station
- the identity of the factions
- the location of the freighter
- the nature of the container
- where the artifact is
- whether anyone inside can be trusted

# Act 2 — Boarding the Station

## Function

Act 2 turns the station from an external mystery into a dangerous, physical environment.

The crew enters through the damaged Main Docking Spine, reaches the Central Spine, discovers signs of factional fighting, and begins reconstructing what happened.

This act should emphasize exploration, bad infrastructure, partial information, and first contact with survivors or evidence of survivors.

## Key Locations

Act 2 is centered on the **Main Docking Spine** and **Central Spine**.

The Docking Spine controls the first entry.

The Central Spine becomes the station’s main hub:

- external docking access
- cargo handling
- power routing
- pressure control
- maintenance routes
- camera and log systems
- spin-transfer access
- old mining routes
- the later route toward the Secondary Spine and freighter berth

The hollowed asteroid provides shielding and history, but the important play space is the station infrastructure built inside it.

## Key Beats

### 1. Approach and Entry

The crew must reach or attach to the Main Docking Spine.

Possible approaches include manual docking, EVA crossing, drone scouting, emergency airlock access, cutting into a damaged service hatch, or using old docking protocols recovered from the corrupted signal.

The entry should be tense and technical.

The station should feel physically unsafe before any human threat appears.

### 2. Dead Systems, Not a Dead Station

The first station areas appear badly damaged but not abandoned.

Signs of life and recent crisis may include patched pressure doors, temporary power cables, used emergency masks, spent ammunition, blood traces, manually barricaded corridors, fresh tool marks, unstable life support, and active but unreliable systems.

The message should be clear:

The station is not a ruin.  
It is a failing place where people recently fought and some may still be alive.

### 3. Establishing the Central Spine

The crew reaches the Central Spine.

This is where the station layout becomes understandable. From here, the crew can identify possible routes toward cargo, maintenance, spin-transfer, the rotating habitat, old mining service spaces, and eventually the Secondary Spine.

The GM should give the players enough orientation to make meaningful decisions without giving them a complete map.

### 4. Evidence of Two Factions

The crew finds signs that the fighting was internal.

Evidence may include opposing barricades, different markings, contradictory warnings, separated casualties, two access override sets, damaged camera footage, and logs blaming different crews for betrayal.

At this stage, the players should understand that this was not an outside raid.

The station tore itself apart.

### 5. The Volkov Split

The faction conflict should gradually become personal.

The Holdouts follow **Arman Volkov**, an old station leader trying to preserve the habitat and what remains of the station order.

The Breakaway Crew follows **Mira Volkov**, his daughter, who believes the station is already lost and wants to escape with the container, the freighter, or enough leverage to survive elsewhere.

Do not reveal their full history in one speech.

Let the players discover it through survivors, faction markings, overheard arguments, logs, and contradictory testimony.

### 6. The Container Appears as a Mystery

The container should be introduced as an absence, not an object.

The crew may find a cargo manifest fragment, interrupted transfer route, empty cradle, mass discrepancy, damaged video fragment, sensor anomaly, or survivor reference to “the box,” “the sealed cargo,” or “the thing they brought in.”

Nobody should yet be able to say exactly where the container is.

### 7. The Freighter Becomes a Lead

The freighter should not appear as an obvious escape option from the start.

In Act 2, the crew may learn that a freighter is docked somewhere deeper in the station structure, attached to a Secondary Spine or offset berth.

This may come from docking records, cargo logs, survivor testimony, partial sensor data, or faction movement.

The freighter becomes a new objective, not an initial visual fact.

### 8. First Contact or First Threat

Depending on player choices, the crew may encounter a wounded survivor, hidden observer, faction patrol, barricaded group, body with useful data, or someone using the crew as leverage.

First contact should create ambiguity.

The players should not immediately know who is telling the truth.

## Required Act 2 Outcomes

By the end of Act 2, the players should know:

- the station was built into an old hollowed asteroid
- the Central Spine links the major station systems
- the station is actively failing
- recent fighting happened between Arman’s Holdouts and Mira’s Breakaway Crew
- the conflict has a personal Volkov family core
- a valuable sealed container was involved
- the container is now missing
- finding it requires active investigation
- a docked freighter may exist at the Secondary Spine
- survivors or faction members are still influencing events

They do not yet need to know:

- the exact location of the container
- the full nature of the artifact
- which faction, if any, is more trustworthy
- whether the freighter can safely leave
- the best way to escape
- whether stabilizing the station is possible

# Act 3 — Pressure Sandbox

## Function

Act 3 is not a linear finale.

It is a pressure sandbox.

The crew now understands enough to make dangerous choices. They can focus on escape, the container, the freighter, station repair, faction negotiation, survivor rescue, salvage, or simply getting out.

Every meaningful action should consume time, expose them to danger, reveal information, or shift faction behavior.

## Main Act 3 Questions

The act should revolve around practical decisions:

- How badly is the Asterion damaged?
- What does the crew need to leave safely?
- Is the freighter usable as an alternate escape option?
- Where did the container go?
- Which faction is still capable of action?
- Who is lying?
- How much time remains before station conditions become critical?
- Is the artifact worth the risk?
- Who gets left behind?

## Possible Player Objectives

The crew may pursue any combination of the following:

- repair the Asterion
- search for the container
- stabilize station systems
- negotiate with Arman
- negotiate with Mira
- reach the freighter
- rescue survivors
- salvage useful equipment
- leave with partial answers

They will probably not have time to do all of them.

The GM should let the players choose and use pressure to make those choices matter.

# Investigation Paths

The container can be found through several methods. The GM should allow different approaches, but none should be automatic.

## Video Reconstruction

The crew restores or pieces together damaged camera footage.

This may reveal the container being unloaded, redirected, blocked by a cargo lift failure, hidden during fighting, or last seen before cameras died.

## Cargo and System Logs

The crew accesses cargo records, power routing, lift commands, pressure door activity, docking logs, or access overrides.

This may reveal inconsistent mass records, manual route changes, missing container IDs, cargo bay decompression events, or a route into a damaged section.

## Sensor Glitches

Science, Operations, or Engineering characters notice local anomalies.

The artifact may cause subtle EM interference, timing errors, false mass readings, repeated data corruption, unexplained thermal irregularities, or sensor dropouts around a sealed compartment.

These glitches should not identify the artifact directly. They should help narrow the search area.

## Physical Search

The crew follows material evidence.

Possible traces include scraped cargo rails, torn restraints, broken pallet locks, boot marks, blood, lubricant, cut seals, emergency lighting, blocked doors, or shifted freight.

## Faction Information

Survivors may know pieces of the truth.

No single survivor should know everything.

One may know where the container was unloaded.  
Another may know who redirected it.  
Another may know which camera system still works.  
Another may be lying to send the crew into danger.

# Pressure System

Track three pressures during Act 3.

## Asterion Condition

The Asterion starts damaged after the debris strike.

Pressure increases when the crew delays repairs, leaves the ship exposed, stresses docking systems, or allows factions near it.

Pressure decreases when the crew finds compatible parts, patches key systems, recalibrates navigation, retrieves station docking data, or clears a safer departure option.

At high pressure, leaving with the Asterion becomes dangerous or impossible without sacrifice.

## Station Collapse

The station is already failing.

Pressure increases when air quality worsens, pressure zones fail, power systems overload, spin-transfer destabilizes, fire or fumes spread, or factions sabotage systems.

Pressure decreases when the crew stabilizes life support, restores pressure control, reroutes power, repairs doors or pumps, restarts scrubbers, or stabilizes spin-transfer access.

At high pressure, routes close, survivors die, and the station may fail beyond recovery.

## Faction Movement

The factions are active.

Pressure increases when the crew is seen, helps one side, approaches the container trail, reveals that the Asterion may still fly, exposes lies, or makes the freighter reachable.

Pressure decreases when the crew negotiates a temporary truce, repairs shared survival systems, trades useful supplies, redirects a faction, or creates distance between rivals.

At high pressure, factions force action through ambush, ship seizure, sabotage, hostage-taking, betrayal, or a rush toward the container or freighter.

# Possible Endgames

Ghost Station does not have one correct ending.

The finale should emerge from player priorities and the state of the station.

## Repair and Leave

The crew finds enough parts, data, or temporary fixes to get the Asterion away.

They may leave without the container or the full truth.

## Escape by Freighter

The crew repairs, seizes, or bargains for access to the docked freighter.

This may require abandoning the Asterion, fighting for control, or deciding who gets evacuated.

## Find and Take the Container

The crew locates the lost container and removes it.

This creates immediate problems: mass, handling, ownership, faction response, legal risk, and future attention.

## Destroy or Abandon the Artifact

The crew determines the artifact is too dangerous, politically toxic, impossible to move, or not worth dying for.

Destroying it may be difficult or incomplete. Abandoning it leaves the problem for someone else.

## Stabilize Part of the Station

The crew focuses on repairs and rescue.

They may save survivors or preserve enough of the station for later recovery, but this likely costs time and may mean losing the container.

## Choose a Faction

The crew backs Arman or Mira for practical reasons.

This may give them access, manpower, information, or escape options, but also makes them complicit in that faction’s choices.

## Leave Everyone Behind

The crew decides the station is too dangerous and escapes with minimal involvement.

This is valid, but it should leave unanswered questions and consequences.

# Core Reveals

The following reveals should appear during play, but not necessarily in this order.

## Reveal 1 — The Signal Comes from a Station

The final asteroid contains a damaged station built into a hollowed body.

## Reveal 2 — The Debris Comes from the Station

The debris field is not natural. It is wreckage from the station’s own damaged structures.

## Reveal 3 — The Station Is Not Dead

Systems are failing, but people are still present or recently active.

## Reveal 4 — The Conflict Was Internal

The disaster was caused by fighting between Arman’s Holdouts and Mira’s Breakaway Crew.

## Reveal 5 — The Freighter Is Hidden at the Secondary Spine

The freighter is docked at an offset berth and not obvious from the first approach.

## Reveal 6 — A Sealed Container Was Central to the Conflict

A valuable container was moved through the station before or during the fighting.

## Reveal 7 — The Container Is Missing

It is not in the expected place and not simply inside the freighter.

## Reveal 8 — The Artifact Is AI-Era, but Not Active AI

The object is valuable and dangerous because of what it is, not because it is a conscious machine.

## Reveal 9 — Leaving Is a Choice, Not a Failure

The crew can escape without solving everything. That is a valid outcome, but it leaves consequences behind.

# Tone and GM Direction

Ghost Station should feel grounded, tense, and practical.

The danger comes from damaged infrastructure, bad information, desperate people, and the cost of every decision.

Avoid making the station feel like:

- a haunted house
- a clean military facility
- a corporate black site
- an alien ruin
- a puzzle dungeon
- a pirate theme park

Instead, make it feel like:

- an old mining station reused too many times
- a hidden Belt refuge that barely passed safety margins
- a salvage site after a firefight
- a habitat where life support still works only because someone keeps forcing it to work
- a place where every door, corridor, and system has been modified by people under pressure

The players should never feel that the station was built for them to explore.

They should feel that they arrived too late, with a damaged ship, into a place already collapsing.
