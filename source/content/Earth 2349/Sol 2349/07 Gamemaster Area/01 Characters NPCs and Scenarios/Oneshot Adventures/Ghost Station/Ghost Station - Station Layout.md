# Ghost Station — Station Layout

## Purpose of This Page

This page describes the physical structure of the station for the GM.

It is not a fixed tactical map.  
It is a spatial framework for orientation, improvisation, hazards, clues, and faction movement.

## Core Layout Concept

Ghost Station is built inside a hollowed asteroid.

The asteroid was mined out long ago by independent miner crews. Most valuable material was removed, leaving a large irregular internal cavity, old access cuts, anchor points, stripped industrial shafts, and abandoned mining infrastructure.

Later crews reused that space. Over decades, the site became a hidden station: part salvage depot, part pirate refuge, part black-market transfer point, part failing habitat.

The station has three major structural elements:

1. **The Main Docking Spine / Central Spine**  
   A rigid industrial structure protruding from the asteroid and continuing into the hollowed interior. It connects external approach access to cargo handling, power, life support, maintenance, and spin-transfer systems.

2. **The Inner Rotating Habitat**  
   A damaged rotating living section mounted inside the asteroid cavity. It provides weak artificial gravity, but the rotation is uneven and unreliable.

3. **The Secondary Spine / Freighter Berth**  
   A smaller, offset docking structure connected deeper into the station. It is not clearly visible from the Asterion’s first approach vector. The docked freighter is attached here, partially hidden by the asteroid shell, station superstructure, shadow, and damaged scaffolding.

The asteroid itself is mostly protective shell and industrial history. The important play space is the station built inside and through it.

---

# Exterior View

From a distance, the asteroid appears as a dark, irregular rock body with old excavation scars. On closer approach, artificial structures become visible: a damaged Main Docking Spine protruding from the surface, broken antenna arrays, torn radiator panels, patched pressure modules, and limited drifting wreckage.

The station should not look like one clean construction project. It should look like decades of practical additions built onto an older mining site.

The **freighter is not immediately visible** from the first approach. It is docked to a secondary berth set at an angle from the main approach path, partly screened by rock and station infrastructure. Initial scans may detect unexplained mass, residual heat, or docking hardware deeper in the cavity, but the ship itself should not be obvious until the crew changes angle, restores better sensor data, reaches the Central Spine, or accesses station docking records.

## The Debris Field

The debris field around the station comes from the station itself.

It contains fragments of hull plating, docking hardware, cargo equipment, antenna structures, container parts, radiator panels, and wreckage from recent fighting.

The debris field is not background decoration. It is:

- the cause of the Asterion’s damage
- an obstacle to docking
- a clue to recent violence
- a possible source of salvageable parts

Keep the debris field limited and believable. It should make approach dangerous without visually burying the station in wreckage.

---
# Zone 1 — Approach and Debris Field

This zone establishes danger before the crew enters the station.

The crew can learn that the signal comes from station hardware rather than bare rock, that the communication array is damaged, and that the surrounding wreckage is recent and artificial.

The main hazards are navigation uncertainty, sensor clutter, unstable docking vectors, and fast-moving debris. This is where the station first proves dangerous without anyone attacking the crew.

The freighter should not be clearly visible here. If the players scan carefully, they may detect a secondary mass signature or heat shadow, but the data should be ambiguous until they gain a better angle or access station systems.

---

# Zone 2 — Main Docking Spine

The Main Docking Spine is the first major contact point with the station.

It protrudes from the asteroid like a broken industrial tool: pressure tubes, trusses, cable runs, airlock modules, docking collars, cargo rails, antenna remains, and patchwork plating. Some sections are torn open. Others have been welded shut.

The crew may enter through a damaged docking collar, an emergency airlock, a cargo intake, an EVA service hatch, or by cutting into a weakened panel.

This zone can reveal that the communication array was physically damaged, that cargo recently moved through the spine, and that both factions used or fought over station access. It may also show that the station was already badly patched before the recent conflict.

Hazards should be technical and physical: leaking seals, unstable clamps, damaged airlocks, loose cargo rails, decompressed side modules, shorting power couplings, and intermittent comm interference.

Both factions care about this area because it controls access to the Asterion and the most obvious route out.

---

# Zone 3 — Central Spine

The Central Spine is the station’s main internal axis and the most important navigation hub in the scenario.

It connects the Main Docking Spine to cargo handling, maintenance, power routing, spin-transfer access, old mining service spaces, and the Secondary Spine leading toward the freighter berth.

The players should reach it early in Act 2.

The Central Spine should feel like the station’s nervous system: damaged, overloaded, crowded with cable bundles, patched pipes, exposed conduit, old labels, temporary seals, faction markings, emergency lockers, and improvised repairs.

From here, the crew can choose routes toward:

- cargo and transfer areas
- maintenance, power, and life support
- the spin-transfer mechanism and rotating habitat
- old mining voids and service cuts
- the Secondary Spine and docked freighter

This is also the best place to seed the investigation. Damaged camera nodes, cargo logs, access records, pressure-door histories, mass discrepancies, faction markings, and sensor glitches can all point toward the missing container.

The Central Spine should not be safe, but it should be understandable. It gives the players enough orientation to make meaningful decisions.

---

# Zone 4 — Cargo and Transfer Areas

The cargo areas are where the container mystery becomes concrete.

They include freight bays, container storage, transfer rails, lift systems, sealed cargo pockets, black-market storage, and improvised loading zones.

The artifact is not automatically here. This zone provides evidence about where the container went.

The crew may find an empty cradle, cut restraints, altered manifests, scraped cargo rails, interrupted transfer logs, damaged camera footage, strange mass records, or sensor glitches along a route that no longer matches the official cargo plan.

The cargo areas also connect logically to the Secondary Spine. The container may have been intended to move from the freighter into station holding, or from station holding back toward the freighter, before the route was interrupted.

The area should feel like a warehouse after a firefight in weak gravity: loose freight, broken pallet locks, decompressed cells, smoke residue, poor lighting, and unstable stacks.

Factions may search this area, hide in it, guard access to the Secondary Spine, destroy records, or use cargo equipment as cover and leverage.

---

# Zone 5 — Maintenance, Power, and Life Support

This zone contains the systems keeping the station barely alive.

It gives technical characters practical problems that matter: scrubbers, pressure controls, battery racks, thermal loops, pumps, filters, emergency generators, old mining power trunks, and improvised repair cages.

Successful work here can change the scenario state. The crew might restore light, improve air in one section, unlock a route, restart a camera node, stabilize spin-transfer access, power a cargo lift, or identify useful repair options for the Asterion.

Failure, delay, or sabotage should make the station worse.

This zone should make the station’s age obvious: hot compartments next to freezing ones, overdue filters, layered sealant, rerouted cables, warning labels written over older warning labels, and systems forced to run outside design limits.

Factions may control life support, threaten to vent sections, trade access to spare parts, protect a technician, or use repair knowledge as bargaining leverage.

Maintenance also provides an indirect route toward the Secondary Spine. This can be useful if the main cargo route is barricaded, decompressed, or controlled by a faction.

---

# Zone 6 — Spin-Transfer and Rotating Habitat

The spin-transfer area connects the mostly non-rotating Central Spine to the rotating living section.

The transition should feel physical and uncomfortable: vibration, timing lights, uneven handholds, moving machinery, old safety markings, and a weak, imperfect handover into gravity.

The rotating habitat is the station’s living core.

It is a battered ring or drum mounted inside the asteroid cavity. It provides weak gravity, but the spin shudders, some corridors slope slightly, and several sections are sealed or overcrowded.

This is where the station feels most human.

There are bunks, mess areas, personal lockers, water bags, medical corners, repair notes, taped images, old entertainment screens, emergency shelters, and faction barricades. The habitat should contrast with the industrial Central Spine: still ugly and damaged, but personal.

The habitat can provide survivor testimony, faction pressure, medical problems, hidden access tags, map fragments, and partial truths about the container. It should not become just another combat zone. It should remind players that people lived here and are now trapped inside the consequences.

---

# Zone 7 — Secondary Spine and Freighter Berth

The Secondary Spine is a smaller, offset docking structure connected to the station’s cargo and service systems.

It does not protrude clearly along the Asterion’s initial approach vector. It sits deeper in the asteroid’s shadow, partly screened by rock, old mining structure, damaged scaffolding, and external station geometry.

This is where the freighter is docked.

## Function

The Secondary Spine serves as:

- a hidden or less obvious docking route
- the freighter’s berth
- a cargo-transfer corridor
- a possible escape route
- a contested objective
- a major source of logs, parts, and leverage

It is less prominent than the Main Docking Spine but potentially more important once the crew understands the station.

## Visual Description

The Secondary Spine is narrower and more improvised than the main spine. It may look like a later addition: reinforced pressure tubes, cargo umbilicals, docking clamps, service gantries, external braces, and shielded access trunks built into the asteroid’s inner curve.

It is damaged but not necessarily destroyed. Some clamps may still hold the freighter. Others may be bent, misaligned, or locked in emergency mode.

## Discovering the Freighter

The crew may discover the freighter through:

- Central Spine docking records
- cargo movement logs
- a secondary mass signature on sensors
- survivor testimony
- camera fragments
- access maps
- power draw from the berth
- a faction trying to reach or guard the route
- a later change in approach angle from the Asterion

The freighter should feel like a discovery, not something visually obvious from the first scene.

## The Docked Freighter

The freighter may have brought the sealed container, may hold useful cargo records, and may be more repairable than the Asterion. It is also a valuable prize.

It is not where the artifact automatically waits.

The freighter may be:

- damaged but flyable with work
- locked down by emergency protocols
- partially powered
- stripped by one faction
- trapped by unstable docking clamps
- decompressed in some sections
- useful as a source of parts
- usable as an alternate escape vessel

## Hazards

The Secondary Spine and freighter berth may involve unstable clamps, poor pressure seals, low power, damaged cargo umbilicals, decompressed access tubes, faction ambushes, or a dangerous manual undocking process.

## Faction Use

Both factions may want the freighter.

One side may see it as an escape route.  
The other may see it as proof, property, or leverage.  
Either side may try to seize it once they realize the Asterion crew can make it usable.

The route to the freighter should become more dangerous as Act 3 progresses.

---

# Zone 8 — Old Mining Voids and Deep Service Spaces

These areas are the station’s older bones.

They are rough, stripped, poorly mapped, and inconsistently sealed. Some are bare rock. Others contain old braces, abandoned equipment, obsolete conduits, sealed extraction shafts, and later station modifications.

Use these spaces sparingly.

They can provide dangerous shortcuts, hidden storage, faction escape routes, old records, abandoned bodies, or a possible final container location if the investigation supports it.

They may also provide an indirect route around the Central Spine toward the Secondary Spine or freighter berth, but this should require risk, technical work, or good navigation.

They should add risk and texture, not turn the scenario into cave exploration.

---

# Station Condition

The station should constantly communicate decay.

Use a few of these details in every scene:

- stale air, high CO₂, emergency masks, condensation, dust, smoke residue
- flickering lights, dead terminals, local battery packs, sparking panels
- welded plates, sealant foam, bent trusses, decompressed side rooms
- outdated maps, broken cameras, corrupted logs, manual labels
- abandoned meals, used medkits, blood traces, ration wrappers, repair notes

Do not repeat all of them everywhere. Pick details that match the zone.

The goal is to make the station feel like a failing machine people are still trying to survive inside.

---

# Pressure Through Layout

The layout should change during play.

As pressure escalates, the GM may:

- block the Main Docking Spine
- depressurize a cargo section
- seal a habitat corridor
- disable a camera node
- jam the spin-transfer mechanism
- reroute power away from a useful system
- expose an old mining shortcut
- make the Asterion harder to reach
- make the Secondary Spine harder to access
- destabilize the freighter berth
- force a manual undocking decision

The station is not a static map.

It is a failing machine.

---

# GM Layout Principles

## Keep the Central Spine Understandable

Players need one reliable spatial anchor.

Even if routes shift, they should understand that the Central Spine connects the major systems, including the route toward the Secondary Spine and freighter.

## Make the Freighter a Discovery

Do not make the freighter obvious from the first exterior image.

Let it emerge through scans, station records, changed viewing angles, survivor testimony, or exploration.

This preserves the first approach image and gives Act 2 or Act 3 a strong new objective.

## Make Every Route Cost Something

A route may cost time, air, repairs, faction attention, stress, injury risk, power, or information.

Avoid free movement once pressure rises.

## Let Technical Work Matter

Engineering, Operations, Science, Spacefaring, Survival, and Medical skills should all create real options.

A repaired camera node, restored pressure door, decoded cargo log, stabilized scrubber, or recovered docking record should change what the crew can do.

## Do Not Hide Everything Behind Combat

The station should be dangerous before anyone fires a weapon.

Bad air, bad maps, unstable spin, failing power, loose cargo, docking failure, and pressure loss should matter as much as armed enemies.

## The Artifact Is Found by Investigation

Do not place the artifact in the first obvious cargo room or simply aboard the freighter.

The crew should find it by connecting evidence: cargo route, video fragment, sensor glitch, survivor testimony, physical trace, system log, faction movement, or freighter transfer data.

The final location can be flexible, but the discovery should feel earned.

---

# Flexible Container Locations

Choose one during prep or decide based on player investigation.

## Jammed Cargo Lift

The container is trapped in a stalled lift between the cargo area and a service level.

## Sealed Maintenance Bay

A faction redirected the container into a maintenance bay and sealed it during the fighting.

## Old Mining Service Pocket

The container was pushed into an old mining void as an improvised hiding place.

## Spin-Transfer Buffer

The container never reached the habitat and became stuck near the spin-transfer machinery.

## Secondary Spine Transfer Corridor

The container was moved toward the freighter but abandoned before loading.

## Decompressed Cargo Cell

The container is in a sealed, decompressed section that requires EVA, pressure restoration, or remote manipulation.
