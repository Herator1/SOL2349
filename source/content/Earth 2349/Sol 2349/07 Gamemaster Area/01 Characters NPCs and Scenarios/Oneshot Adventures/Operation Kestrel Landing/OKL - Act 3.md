Act 3 begins when the players have enough information to understand that the station crisis is not simply a failed hostage situation. By this point, they should know or strongly suspect that the outpost was developing a neuro-adaptive system capable of subtly influencing behavior, emotional weighting, and decision bias. They should also understand that the **Purists** are not inventing the danger, even if their methods made the situation far worse.

This act is about **forced choice under pressure**.

The players are no longer just entering and investigating. They are now deciding what survives:

- the scientists
    
- the research
    
- the evidence
    
- the corporate version of events
    
- or some unstable combination of all four
    

Act 3 should feel tighter and more unstable than the earlier acts. The station is still physically dangerous, but by now the real threat is human intention:

- the scientists want extraction and control of the research
    
- the Purists want the project stopped and the truth preserved
    
- the corporate team wants containment, cleanup, and narrative control
    

The players are caught in the middle with partial authority, incomplete trust, and very little time.

---

## Scene Goal

Act 3 establishes:

- the real intentions of the surviving research staff
    
- the core demands of the Purists
    
- what evidence exists, and where
    
- the arrival and behavior of the corporate extraction team
    
- the final operational choice the players must make
    

Act 3 ends when the station incident is functionally decided:

- extraction
    
- destruction
    
- containment
    
- betrayal
    
- or collapse
    

---

## Act 3 and the Map

Act 3 is driven by the right-hand half of the station and the central approach to it.

The most important locations are:

- **Security Station**
    
- **Server Core**
    
- **Lab Sector**
    
- **Cryo Containment**
    
- **Research Vault**
    
- **Central Hub**
    

The basic logic of the act is simple:

- the **Purists** want to prevent movement from the research side toward any safe extraction route
    
- the **scientists** want to move themselves and the most important data away from the research side and toward a viable exit
    
- the **corporate team** wants to seize the same research side quickly, secure portable assets, and take control of the whole story before the players can define it
    

That makes **Central Hub** the key human chokepoint and the **Server Core / Research Vault / Lab Sector** triangle the key information zone.

---

## What the Players Can Find — and Where

By the beginning of Act 3, the players should have enough fragments to act. They do not need a perfect explanation. They need enough to understand what matters.

The easiest way to run this is to tie evidence to specific locations.

---

## 1. Security Station

This location should confirm that the incident was not a simple outside takeover.

### What the players can find

- corridor shutter logs showing internal compartment isolation
    
- manual lock overrides affecting routes into the research side
    
- camera feeds that cut out selectively rather than globally
    
- security dispatch fragments showing confusion rather than a coordinated defense
    
- one or more clipped internal messages ordering sectors sealed before the Purists fully reached them
    

### What this evidence means

- someone inside the station made the situation harder to observe and harder to interrupt
    
- station leadership chose control and isolation over transparency
    
- the first response to the crisis was not “call for help and hold,” but “seal and suppress”
    

### Strong reveal here

A restricted control log can show that the **jammer was activated internally**, not by the attackers.

This is one of the most important findings in the scenario.

### Why it matters

It breaks the clean extremist-takeover narrative and gives the Purists immediate credibility on at least one major point.

---

## 2. Server Core

This is the best place for hard digital evidence. If the players only extract one major information package in Act 3, it should probably come from here.

### What the players can find

- routing logs proving that external communications were suppressed from inside the station
    
- buried admin privileges or hidden access branches
    
- scrubbed or partially deleted system directories
    
- evidence that the interface system had undocumented modulation layers
    
- logs showing adaptive response tuning beyond declared parameters
    
- access traces linking restricted control functions to research leadership
    

### What this evidence means

- the system was not just assistive
    
- it was not just monitoring stress
    
- it had hidden control or biasing architecture beyond the declared project scope
    

### Strong reveal here

A hidden control layer or buried parameter branch makes it clear that the interface could be **externally tuned**.

Not direct mind control.  
Something worse: subtle, deniable behavioral shaping.

### Why it matters

This is the point where the players should understand that the real danger is not a lab accident. It is the existence of a system someone could plausibly use and plausibly deny.

---

## 3. Lab Sector

The Lab Sector should contain the most human evidence: notes, workstations, sample handling, and signs of how normal the project looked from the inside.

### What the players can find

- behavioral analysis workstations
    
- stress-adaptation trial records
    
- session reports full of euphemistic language
    
- partially interrupted experiments
    
- personal research notes showing disagreement within the team
    
- terminology that deliberately frames manipulation as stabilization, response shaping, or emotional regulation
    

### What this evidence means

- the project was wrapped in scientific and operational language that made it sound more acceptable than it was
    
- not everyone on the team understood the full implications the same way
    
- rationalization was built into the culture of the station
    

### Strong reveal here

A disagreement log, private memo, or internal comment chain can show that **some researchers objected to scaling the system before external modulation risks were understood**.

### Why it matters

This proves that the dangerous implications were not invisible. They were seen, debated, and pushed forward anyway.

---

## 4. Cryo Containment

Cryo Containment should feel like the coldest, least human room in the station. It is a good place to put the subtle “this is already affecting people” evidence.

### What the players can find

- long-term storage of neural substrate samples, frozen interface material, or trial-related biological support hardware
    
- controlled records of test exposure or adaptation sequences
    
- staff monitoring data showing flattened affect or unusual cognitive consistency
    
- preserved data on subjects or operators who were not supposed to display certain pattern stability under stress
    
- evidence that some researchers themselves were exposed beyond nominal safety thresholds
    

### What this evidence means

- this was not just theory
    
- the system was already interacting with human operators in non-trivial ways
    
- at least some personnel may no longer be entirely reliable witnesses to their own motives
    

### Strong reveal here

The players may find that **one or more current station staff already show signs of influence**.

Not obvious puppeting.  
Subtle flattening, unusually stable emotional response, repeated decision preferences, or too-perfect calm.

### Why it matters

It makes the scientists harder to trust even when they are telling the truth.

---

## 5. Research Vault

This is the single most important restricted room in the scenario.

If the **Server Core** proves the hidden architecture exists, the **Research Vault** proves how serious it is.

### What the players can find

- restricted archives
    
- portable high-value data packages
    
- command-level memos
    
- ethics review documents with missing sections
    
- emergency deletion tools
    
- contingency plans for extraction or project continuation after site compromise
    
- evidence of shell-project naming, compartmentalization, or future deployment intent
    

### What this evidence means

- this project was never meant to stay only here
    
- somebody was already thinking beyond station-level research
    
- the station is not the whole story, only the exposed part of it
    

### Strong reveal here

The strongest possible packet in the room is some version of this truth:

**the system was already being prepared for wider application under controlled external governance, even if under another label or project branch.**

### Why it matters

This is the piece that makes the Purists’ fear fully intelligible.

If this leaves the station intact under the wrong control, the incident becomes a beginning, not an ending.

---

## The Scientists

By Act 3, the players should have direct contact with the surviving research group. They should not be played as cackling villains or detached monsters. They are intelligent professionals under crisis pressure, and most of them still believe they are closer to a breakthrough than a crime.

### What the Scientists Want

Their immediate goals are:

- survive
    
- be extracted
    
- preserve the data
    
- keep the system from being destroyed
    
- keep the Purists from framing the entire incident uncontested
    
- keep the corporate team from simply taking everything away from them
    
- control who gets access to the truth
    

The key point is that they do **not** merely want rescue.

They want **rescue with retention**.

If the players offer safety but insist on destroying the project, exposing everything, or turning over all control to outside authorities, the scientists become more guarded and more manipulative.

### What the Scientists Want from the Players

They want the marines to:

- secure a route from the **research side** back through **Central Hub**
    
- neutralize or bypass Purist resistance
    
- keep the corporate team from simply taking custody of the project
    
- escort key personnel out alive
    
- allow selected data to leave the station
    
- treat the station as a crisis site, not a crime scene
    

In practical terms, they want the players to behave like disciplined extractors, not investigators.

### What Dr. Lyra Voss Wants

Dr. Voss is the clearest voice of research continuity.

She believes:

- the project is dangerous, but too important to lose
    
- the Purists have proven the need for stronger control, not abandonment
    
- if outsiders seize the system now, the worst actors will define it
    
- the players are most useful if they remain focused on extraction rather than judgment
    

If pressed, she should argue in terms of necessity, not innocence.

### How the Scientists Behave

They should:

- ask for immediate extraction
    
- downplay the Purists’ credibility
    
- avoid direct language about manipulation
    
- frame the system as adaptive, stabilizing, or protective
    
- insist the station cannot be left unsecured
    
- try to separate “dangerous misuse” from “valuable research continuity”
    

If the players have strong evidence, the scientists should not collapse instantly. They should pivot, contextualize, and rationalize.

### Read-Aloud: First Full Contact with the Scientists

> The sealed section opens only partway before stopping on internal override. Light spills through the gap — cleaner, brighter, more controlled than the corridors behind you.
> 
> Several figures wait inside. Research staff, exhausted but composed, some armed only with improvised tools, others simply watching with the stillness of people who have been holding themselves together by force.
> 
> One of them steps forward. Her voice is calm, precise, practiced even now.
> 
> “You’re League.”
> 
> She does not sound relieved for long.
> 
> “Good. Then we need to move quickly. The station is compromised, the attackers are still active, and this project cannot be left unsecured.”

## The Purists

The Purists should become clearer in Act 3, but not simpler.

They are under enormous pressure now. They know time is against them. If the players have not already sided openly with them, they assume the balance is slipping toward extraction and containment.

### What the Purists Want

Their immediate goals are:

- stop the project permanently
    
- keep the scientists from leaving with usable data
    
- preserve proof of what was being developed
    
- prevent the station incident from being buried
    

They are not trying to occupy the outpost as territory.  
They are trying to keep the truth from being erased.

### What the Purists Want from the Scientists

The Purists do **not** need the scientists dead as a first priority.

What they actually want is:

- the data stripped from them
    
- the system disabled or destroyed
    
- admissions, logs, or proof preserved
    
- the research leadership prevented from walking away under corporate protection
    

If possible, the Purists want the scientists exposed and detained, not turned into martyrs. They know dead scientists can be buried in the same narrative as destroyed evidence. Living scientists with seized archives are much more dangerous to the people who wanted silence.

### What Their Leader Wants

The Purist leader should believe two things at once:

- they have already lost the clean version of this operation
    
- they may still be able to stop the worst outcome
    

They do not need the players to agree with them morally.  
They need the players to stop behaving like a sterile extraction team and look at what is actually on the station.

### How the Purists Behave in Act 3

They should:

- become less patient
    
- become more direct
    
- push the players toward the strongest evidence
    
- warn them that external help will not be neutral
    
- oppose any attempt to let the scientists leave with full research control
    
- concentrate around chokepoints rather than waste themselves in random fights
    

On the map, this means they work best:

- around **Central Hub**
    
- on corridors between **Server Core** and **Research Vault**
    
- and on routes leading from the research side toward viable exits
    

### Read-Aloud: Purist Warning

> The message comes through on a local burst, rough and clipped, half-whispered through interference.
> 
> “If you take them out with the data, this happens again somewhere cleaner.”
> 
> A pause. Breathing. Then:
> 
> “You’re not the end of this operation. You’re just early.”

## The Corporate Extraction Team

This is the major escalation of Act 3.

The players should eventually realize that they are not the only outside force converging on the station. This team should not arrive with fanfare. No dramatic assault music. No obvious villain introduction. They are dangerous precisely because they are competent, calm, and structurally legible as “support.”

### What the Corporate Team Wants

Their goals are:

- secure or destroy the research as needed
    
- prevent uncontrolled disclosure
    
- recover key personnel if useful
    
- eliminate evidence and loose ends if necessary
    
- ensure the final story is manageable
    

They are not there to save the players.  
They are not there to arbitrate.  
They are there to end uncertainty in favor of corporate control.

### What the Corporate Team Wants from the Scientists

Ideally:

- living leadership
    
- recoverable data
    
- controllable witnesses
    
- a narrative that keeps the station incident narrow and deniable
    

If the scientists cannot be trusted to stay aligned, the corporate team may reduce them from “assets” to “containment liabilities” very quickly.

### How They Enter the Map

The most logical insertion points are:

- **Main Transfer Lock (Damaged)** if they force the obvious route
    
- **Surface Operations Lock** if they land cleanly and use a functional controlled access point
    
- **External Service Bay** if they prioritize rapid technical access and equipment movement
    

Whichever route you choose, their arrival should narrow options, not just add bodies.

### First Corporate Contact

> A clean voice cuts through the local channel with almost offensive clarity.
> 
> “This is corporate recovery element Aegis-3. We are now on site.”
> 
> A short pause.
> 
> “League personnel are instructed to maintain position and avoid interference with sensitive asset handling.”
> 
> Another pause, colder this time.
> 
> “We will take over from here.”

This should be the moment the players understand that “support” is not support.

## Map-Based Act 3 Set Pieces

Use one or more depending on pacing.

---

## 1. Security Station + Central Hub

This is the best early-Act-3 set piece if you want a confrontation that is still partly procedural.

### Good for

- restoring partial control of shutters or internal routing
    
- arguing over who controls movement through the station
    
- proving the jammer and lockdown were internal
    
- turning the station from “maze” into “decision space”
    

### Typical tension

- Purists want the players to use the station’s own control history as evidence
    
- scientists want the players to stop wasting time and move them out
    
- players must decide whether the hub is a secure transit node or a contested scene
    

This is the best place for a three-way standoff later.

---

## 2. Server Core

This is the best technical evidence room in the scenario.

### Good for

- proving hidden control architecture
    
- recovering portable evidence
    
- discovering scrubbed or compartmentalized files
    
- revealing that communication suppression came from inside
    

### Strong choice here

The players may have to decide whether to:

- copy the data
    
- lock it down
    
- destroy it
    
- leave it in place and trust nobody reaches it first
    

This is one of the strongest “mission changes here” scenes in the scenario.

---

## 3. Lab Sector + Cryo Containment

This is where the players should understand that the project was not only theoretical.

### Good for

- human evidence
    
- trial records
    
- adaptation reports
    
- subtle signs of already-affected staff
    
- the unsettling realization that some current testimony may itself be compromised
    

### Strong choice here

Do the players trust what the surviving researchers say about the system once they can see its effects more directly?

This area should feel colder, cleaner, and more controlled than the rest of the station. That contrast should be disturbing.

---

## 4. Research Vault

This is the high-value restricted archive and likely the single most contested room in the station.

### Good for

- the strongest evidence
    
- portable data packages
    
- proof of future deployment or hidden project continuity
    
- Voss’s last serious argument
    
- a choice between preservation and destruction
    

### Strong choice here

This room should force the players to decide what “mission success” now means.

A good Act-3 pressure triangle is:

- scientists trying to get selected vault data out
    
- Purists trying to stop that at any cost
    
- corporate recovery trying to seize the vault intact

## Running the Final Choice

By the late part of Act 3, the players should no longer be able to remain passive.

They do not need to pick an ideology.  
They do need to choose an outcome.

### Common choices

**Support the scientists**  
Escort them and the data toward extraction.  
Consequence: the Purists resist, the corporate team may try to take over, and the players become responsible for what survives.

**Support the Purists**  
Help destroy, disable, or permanently compromise the system while preserving evidence.  
Consequence: the scientists resist, the corporate team becomes openly hostile, and extraction becomes far less clean.

**Try to split the difference**  
Preserve proof, deny full control, and prevent either side from walking away with everything.  
Consequence: hardest path, most unstable, highest chance of everyone turning on the players.

**Stand down and defer**  
Accept the corporate or formal chain and let stronger institutions take over.  
Consequence: easiest in the moment, morally darkest in retrospect.

## Extraction to the Pilgrim

If the players secure surviving scientists and decide to remove them from the station, extraction should feel tense, procedural, and fragile rather than heroic.

The **Pilgrim** is not parked at a boarding gate. It is waiting on the surface at the team’s chosen landing site, or at a revised pickup point if the tactical situation has changed. That means extraction requires three distinct steps:

- move the survivors out of the station
- get them across the surface in EVA or emergency transfer gear
- board and strap them into the Pilgrim for immediate liftoff

In most cases, the hardest part is not the takeoff itself. It is getting frightened, injured, compromised, or uncooperative civilians from a damaged research station to a military lander under pressure.

### Getting the scientists out

If the scientists are mobile and cooperative, the players can move them in a controlled evacuation line: armed marines forward and rear, survivors in the middle, pace dictated by suit integrity, injuries, and surface conditions.

If they are injured, sedated, unstable, or contaminated, the players may need:

- emergency soft stretchers
- suit assist frames
- improvised tow lines
- one marine fully committed to casualty movement rather than security

Keep this practical. The scene should reinforce that rescue is slow, awkward, and exposed.

### Boarding the Pilgrim

Once at the lander, boarding is fast and procedural.

The hatch cycles, the marines push the survivors inside, and the interior immediately becomes cramped and ugly in a different way than during insertion. Empty restraint points, crash couches, fold-down web seats, and cargo tie-downs now become evacuation hardware.

Scientists are not expected to board well. Some may freeze, argue, panic, or demand priority for data, samples, or equipment. The marines should be forced to decide quickly what matters:

- living people
- wounded personnel
- critical data packages
- sealed research containers
- or speed of departure

Once inside, survivors are strapped down as hard as the marines were on descent. Injured evacuees may be clipped into med restraints or cargo-rated crash webbing if proper seats are limited. This should not feel comfortable or humane. It should feel necessary.

### Liftoff

Takeoff itself is simple once the hatch is sealed.

The Pilgrim lifts under controlled thrust, clears the surface, and begins ascent to rendezvous with the **Kestrel**. Unless you want one last complication, do not overplay this part. The emotional weight is in who made it aboard, what was left behind, and whether the team believes they actually extracted victims, witnesses, liabilities, or evidence.

### GM guidance

Do not spend too long here unless extraction becomes the crisis of the finale.

In most runs, this sequence works best as a **tight closing procedure**:

- secure survivors
- withdraw to the surface
- load the Pilgrim
- strap in
- seal hatch
- lift off

The important questions are not technical. They are human and political:

- Who gets out?
- In what condition?
- With what information?
- And who now controls the story of what happened on the station?

## GM Guidance for Intentions

Do not flatten the factions in the finale.

### The scientists are not trying to conquer the world

They are trying to survive, preserve their work, and avoid losing control of something they still believe matters.

### The Purists are not bloodthirsty fanatics

They are trying to stop the scientists from walking out under protection with a deniable system and a portable archive.

### The corporate team is not theatrical evil

They are trying to seize the same archive, the same people, and the same story before anyone else can define what happened.

The players should feel that every side has a real argument and a real blind spot.

## Tone of Act 3

Act 3 should feel:

- tighter
    
- clearer
    
- morally heavier
    
- still physically grounded
    
- more about decisions than body count
    

The best version of this act is not a giant final firefight.  
It is a sequence of pressure scenes where combat remains possible, often likely, but never the only thing happening.

---

## Ending the Act

Act 3 ends when the crisis is operationally resolved.

That may mean:

- the scientists leave
    
- the system is destroyed
    
- the evidence is preserved or lost
    
- the corporate team takes control
    
- the players break with orders
    
- the station becomes unstable enough to force withdrawal
    
- or some combination of the above
    

The important thing is that by the end of the act, the players have made a real choice about what kind of truth survives the incident.

After Act 3, the scenario moves into **Outcomes and Rewards**.

That section answers:

- who survived
    
- what data remains
    
- how the League interprets the mission
    
- what the corporate response is
    
- and whether the players are seen as professionals, liabilities, or something in between
