## Overview

Act 1 establishes the mission, the physical transition into the Europa system, and the first contact with personnel inside the operational chain. The players are framed as routine inspectors. No real investigation happens yet. The purpose of this act is tone, pressure, and orientation.

## Scene 1 – Briefing

The players receive their briefing before departure.

### Read-Aloud (optional)

> The discrepancies are minor.  
> Within tolerance in isolation.  
> Outside tolerance in aggregate.
> 
> This is a routine audit.  
> You are there to confirm that.

The official task is simple: review procedures, validate records, and confirm that the system remains within acceptable parameters. The players are instructed to keep the true scope of the concern concealed for as long as possible. No contamination event has been declared, no shutdown has been authorized, and no wider alert has been issued.

If the players ask questions, answers remain controlled and procedural. The discrepancy is described as small but persistent. Escalation is not desired. Only one on-site report triggered the audit.

### GM Notes

Keep the tone neutral and controlled. This is not a crisis briefing. It should feel like an institution trying very hard not to call something a problem yet.

## Scene 2 – Transit

The team departs from an outer station beyond the radiation belt and begins the burn toward [[Europa]].

There is little to do during transit. The crew is strapped in, systems are checked repeatedly, and the vehicle remains under sustained acceleration for most of the approach. Visibility is limited, conversation is minimal, and the flight is uncomfortable rather than dramatic.

### Read-Aloud (optional)

> There is no view.  
> Only force, pressure, and time.

An optional physical or endurance check can be used here to underline the strain of the burn. Failure can mean nausea, disorientation, or a brief loss of composure, but should not carry any serious mechanical penalty. The point is atmosphere.

### GM Notes

Keep this short. It is a transition scene, not a challenge scene.

## Scene 3 – Approach and Entry

As the shuttle approaches Europa, communication becomes tighter and more formal. The flight window is narrow and the descent profile is aggressive. The players do not get a scenic arrival. They get a controlled, high-stress insertion designed to minimize time in the worst of the radiation environment.

### Read-Aloud (optional)

> The approach window is narrow.  
> There is no room for delay.

The landing is hard. Braking is immediate and forceful. The hull complains just enough for everyone to notice it.

## Scene 4 – Surface Transfer

The shuttle docks at a surface facility and the players are processed immediately. Identity verification, routing assignment, transfer authorization, and movement orders are all handled efficiently. There is no tour, no waiting area, and no informal welcome. Everything is already scheduled.

### GM Notes

This section should feel impersonal and efficient. The system is used to moving people safely below the ice, not to making them comfortable.

## Scene 5 – Descent Below the Ice

The players are transferred into a sealed descent module and sent through the ice shaft system.

The shift in atmosphere matters more than the mechanics. The last external light disappears quickly. From this point on, the environment is artificial, enclosed, and procedural. The players are no longer arriving at a place. They are entering a controlled interior.

### Read-Aloud (optional)

> Light disappears quickly.  
> What remains is structure and signal.

This is a good point to slow down slightly and let the feeling of confinement settle in.

## Scene 6 – Transfer Dock

At the base of the descent shaft, the players arrive at a small docking chamber. Waiting there is the transfer sub that handles movement between the station and the research vessel below. The chamber is functional, compact, and quiet. Personnel presence is minimal.

This is the first point where the players are clearly inside the operational chain rather than just passing through infrastructure.

## Scene 7 – First Contact

Here the players meet **Tomas Verik**, pilot of the transfer sub.

Tomas should come across as relaxed, competent, and easy to talk to. He helps with boarding, handles equipment naturally, and speaks like someone who does this every day. He is not evasive, not tense, and not trying to impress anyone. If the players speak to him, he answers simply and practically.

A few sample lines are enough:

> “First time down here?”  
> “Ride’s a bit rough, but short.”  
> “Once you’re inside, it’s all routine.”

### GM Notes

This is important. Tomas should feel like the most normal person the players have met so far. He is friendly, grounded, and useful. Nothing about this scene should suggest guilt.

## Scene 8 – Boarding

The players board the transfer sub. Equipment is secured, seating is assigned, and the vehicle is already in a state of near-readiness. The space is smaller and more intimate than the ships and modules they have used so far. At this point, the insertion phase is over. The next step will carry them directly into the closed system where the rest of the scenario takes place.

## End of Act 1

By the end of this act, the players have received their mission, endured the physical stress of insertion, passed through the layered infrastructure of Europa, and made first contact with the internal transport chain. They have not uncovered anything yet, but they are now inside the system where the discrepancies originate.
