## Overview

Act 3 begins once the players stop collecting information and start applying pressure.

This can happen when they confront a suspect, restrict movement, demand access, or openly signal that the audit is no longer routine. From this point onward, the station reacts. Crew members become cautious, guilty parties adjust their behavior, and the next transfer cycle becomes critical.

This act is not built around one fixed scene. It is a reaction phase shaped by how the players proceed.

---

## General Use

When the players confront someone, use three questions:

**How strong is their evidence?**  
A weak accusation produces denial and defensiveness. A strong accusation produces mistakes, confessions, or escalation.

**Is the confrontation private or public?**  
A private confrontation may break a suspect. A public one changes the whole station.

**Do the players move immediately or leave time afterward?**  
If they confront someone and then step away, guilty people start adapting.

## Correct Accusation

### Dr. Ilya Soren, Senior Biochemist

If confronted early, Soren does not panic. He stays calm, technical, and controlled. He avoids direct lies where possible and tries to reframe the issue as a misunderstanding of incomplete research practice. He argues interpretation, not innocence.

He may say that the players are mistaking procedural variance for criminal intent, or that they are trying to freeze a line of inquiry before it is even properly understood. He will admit to unusual priorities before he admits to smuggling. He prefers ambiguity.

If the players can connect the cartridge overuse, the encapsulation equipment, and the suspicious return flow, Soren loses his technical cover. At that point he stops arguing procedure and starts arguing motive. He does not see himself as a thief. He sees himself as someone preventing an important discovery from being buried inside institutional control.

### How Soren defends himself

He narrows the argument.  
He attacks the interpretation.  
He insists the players are drawing conclusions too early.

### How Soren breaks

He breaks once the players understand both the method and the route out of the system. When that happens, he becomes more open, but not apologetic. He may admit that material left the station, yet still defend the decision as necessary.

### GM Note

Soren should not collapse easily. He is strongest when the players are technically correct but morally uncertain.

---

### Tomas Verik, External Transfer Technician

If confronted with only partial evidence, Tomas reacts with surprise and mild irritation. He stays personable for as long as possible and tries to normalize his behavior. He frames his departures from the cockpit as habit, maintenance checks, or practical shortcuts.

He can defend himself reasonably well if the players only have the camera recordings. Once they also have evidence from material routing or a clear link to Soren’s work, his position weakens.

Tomas will usually try to protect Soren first. He is more likely to admit to “moving things that should not have gone where they went” than to explain the full purpose.

### How Tomas defends himself

He makes it sound routine.  
He keeps his tone relaxed.  
He avoids big explanations.

### How Tomas breaks

He breaks when the confrontation becomes personal and immediate. If he believes the players already understand the route and are about to act, he loses confidence quickly. He is not a disciplined operative. He is a practical man with a cause he believes in.

### GM Note

Tomas works best if the players genuinely like him before they turn on him.

---

### Dr. Marek Chen, Senior Analyst for Sequencing and Classification

Chen is the most fragile of the guilty parties.

If confronted with only weak evidence, he hides behind workload, messy documentation, and procedural backlog. He tries to explain every inconsistency as stress, delayed cleanup, or incomplete data handling. None of it sounds entirely convincing, but it sounds plausible enough at first.

If the players connect the documentation errors, the shore leave pattern, and the implant irregularities, Chen starts to unravel. He does not have a coherent justification because he does not believe in what he is doing. He is scared, compromised, and trying to survive it.

### How Chen defends himself

He blames stress.  
He blames backlog.  
He blames poor documentation.  
He tries to explain small things in too much detail.

### How Chen breaks

He breaks fast once he believes the players really understand the pattern. He may not deliver a clean confession, but he stops maintaining the lie. He is also the most likely to cooperate if he thinks it will help him.

### GM Note

Chen should feel dangerous not because he is competent, but because panic makes him unpredictable.

## Partial Accusation

This is the most useful confrontation outcome.

If the players correctly identify **Soren and Tomas Verik**, the case should appear almost solved. Their route is real, their motive is coherent, and the evidence is strong. The players may believe they have found the answer.

That should hold for a moment.

Then the discrepancy should still remain. Either the players notice that the numbers still do not close, or **Dr. Sia Renn, Biosecurity Officer**, or **Dr. Jonas Krell, Sample Custody and Chain-of-Handling Officer**, points out that the missing categories do not fully match the biochemistry route.

If the players identify **Marek Chen** first, the opposite happens. He clearly did something, but it is too small, too chaotic, and too incoherent to explain the full discrepancy. That pushes the players back toward the transfer chain and the biochemistry lab.

### GM Note

The key feeling here is:  
**this explanation is true, but not enough**

That is the core of the double-perpetrator structure.

## Wrong Accusation

Wrong accusations should not break the scenario. They should cost trust, time, and access.

### Dr. Talia Neves, Senior Researcher for Cultivation Systems

If accused, Talia reacts with anger, embarrassment, and professional defensiveness. She knows her lab looks bad. She knows her records are sloppy. She also knows she has unrelated things to hide.

She attacks the accusation as lazy and superficial. She may accuse the players of targeting cultivation simply because it is the obvious messiest department.

If pushed hard enough, her stimulant misuse or her hidden relationship with **Nika Venn, Internal Service Technician**, may surface. This creates a real scene, but not a solution.

### Dr. Jonas Krell, Sample Custody and Chain-of-Handling Officer

If accused, Krell becomes rigid and procedural. He overexplains recordkeeping, produces too much paperwork, and withdraws into process language. He is frightened of being blamed for missing the discrepancy, which makes him look worse than he is.

### Dr. Sia Renn, Biosecurity Officer

If accused, Renn turns colder and more formal. She may begin demanding written grounds for access requests or route the audit through stricter channels. This makes the station harder to work in, but it does not mean she is guilty.

### Nika Venn, Internal Service Technician

If accused, Nika first reacts with humor and irritation, then closes off. If the players continue pressing, her small supply diversion and her relationship with Talia may come into view. This can easily consume time and create plausible but wrong suspicion.

### GM Note

Wrong accusations should make the station feel more brittle. People stop volunteering information. Access becomes harder. The players feel the cost of being wrong.

## Evidence Destruction

Evidence destruction should only begin once the players apply visible pressure and then leave time for the guilty to react.

Good triggers include:  
a public accusation, broad shutdown attempts, aggressive access demands, or confronting someone without securing records and movement first.

The guilty parties react differently.

### Soren’s reaction

Soren does not smash evidence. He cleans it.

He normalizes suspicious workflows, adjusts side records, shuts down marginal procedures, and removes anything that makes his biochemistry setup look too unusual. His goal is not to erase the event, but to make it easier to explain.

This weakens interpretive clarity, but does not erase the lead entirely.

### Tomas Verik’s reaction

Tomas changes behavior immediately. He stops leaving the cockpit, becomes more formal during transfers, and avoids carrying anything risky. He makes future observation less useful.

This does not erase the past, but it reduces the chance of catching him in the act.

### Marek Chen’s reaction

Chen panics.

He patches records badly, deletes things he should not delete, and makes new inconsistencies while trying to hide the old ones. If the players pressure Chen and then leave him unsupervised, he can actually make himself easier to expose.

### GM Note

Evidence destruction is not a fail state. It is escalation. Some leads become weaker, others become stronger, and the station becomes more unstable.

---

## Public Accusation

If the players accuse someone publicly, the station changes.

Crew members become guarded. Gossip spreads. Work continues, but not naturally. The guilty react. The innocent become wary. **Prof. Dr. Lena Orlov, Scientific Lead**, may start protecting the integrity of the mission rather than the convenience of the audit. **Captain Daren Vale** may move the process into more formal channels.

This can cause:  
Soren to suspend risky work, Tomas to become harder to read, and Chen to spiral.

### GM Note

Public accusation should make Act 3 harder, but also more intense. The audit is no longer routine.

## Breaking Points

Use this section quickly at the table.

**Soren breaks through understanding.**  
Once the players understand both his method and the transfer route, he stops hiding behind procedure and starts defending principle.

**Tomas breaks through personal pressure.**  
He holds until he believes the case is already turning and he has to choose between loyalty and damage control.

**Chen breaks through fear.**  
Once he believes the players have the pattern, he starts to collapse.

---

## Practical Thresholds

Use these as a simple guide.

With **one clue**, suspects deny and deflect.  
With **two linked clues**, suspects become defensive.  
With **three linked clues plus timing or routing**, guilty suspects begin to break.  
If the accusation is public and the players do not secure the next step immediately, evidence starts shifting.  
If the accusation is wrong, trust drops and side secrets surface.
