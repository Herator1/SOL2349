# ACT 1 — THE JOB

## Scene Frame

The crew of the **MCRV Dustbreaker** is en route to a clustered recovery site flagged by a low-confidence orbital anomaly review. The contract is routine, low-priority, and not expected to pay well. That is exactly why this crew got it.

Act 1 should establish three things clearly:

- this is a **working salvage crew**, not an expedition team
- the **Dustbreaker** is a lifeline, workplace, and financial dependency all at once
- the site feels slightly wrong before it feels important

The tone should stay grounded, procedural, and believable. The scenario works best if the crew begins this job expecting dust, dead hardware, and a modest payout — not revelation.

---

## Read Aloud — Opening

> The Dustbreaker crawls across the Martian surface, its mass carried on wide pressure wheels that grind through dust and stone alike.
> 
> Inside, everything hums. Air systems. Thermal loops. Drive control.
> 
> Nothing here is optional.
> 
> Outside, the world is silent, thin, and lethal.
> 
> Inside, five people and a machine keep each other alive.
> 
> The contract is simple.
> 
> Three small losses. Low value. No competition.
> 
> Get in. Strip what matters. Get out.

---

# The Player Characters

The crew of the Dustbreaker are not soldiers, explorers, or treasure hunters. They are a **contract salvage crew** working the Martian surface under a practical, low-margin recovery arrangement.

They know how to:

- keep a rover working in bad conditions
- evaluate whether an asset is worth the time
- strip useful parts from dead systems
- manage risk against contract payout
- leave when a site stops being worth it

They are used to discomfort, delay, weak support, and imperfect information. They take jobs because the vehicle must stay running, the lease must be justified, and routine work is still better than no work.

### What They Want

At the start of Act 1, the crew’s priorities are simple:

- complete the contract cleanly
- avoid damaging the Dustbreaker
- recover enough value to make the trip worthwhile
- avoid getting trapped in an off-scope problem
- maybe find a little more than the paperwork promised

### What They Do Not Want

- vehicle failure far from support
- injury or suit damage
- contract disputes with Tharsis
- a site that turns into a claims problem
- anything that requires expensive outside attention

---

# The Dustbreaker

**Type:** Medium Pressurized Salvage Rover  
**Owner:** Tharsis Recovery & Leasing  
**Crew Capacity:** 5  
**Operational Endurance:** ~10–14 days active field operation

The Dustbreaker is the crew’s transport, shelter, workshop, and recovery platform. It is not elegant, not spacious, and not new. But it works — because crews like this keep it working.

It should feel like a machine that is depended on every day:

- cramped but competent
- worn but maintained
- practical in every detail
- trustworthy only as long as the crew keeps it that way

---

## Read Aloud — Inside the Dustbreaker

> The interior of the Dustbreaker is all close angles, strapped-down equipment, worn surfaces, and systems noise.
> 
> There is no wasted space. Tools sit where they have to. Emergency gear sits where it must.
> 
> The air smells faintly of metal, filtered dust, machine heat, and five people who have been living in the same sealed volume for too long.
> 
> Every compartment has a purpose. Every loose item is a future problem.

---

## Purpose

The Dustbreaker is not built to haul intact wrecks back to civilization. It is a **mobile recovery platform** used to locate, open, strip, secure, and transport salvageable components from small to medium-value sites.

Built for:

- locating lost assets
- field stripping and partial recovery
- short-term remote salvage work
- operation far from major support infrastructure

It is not built for:

- combat
- heavy lifting
- extended excavation
- carrying full wrecks
- prolonged scientific exploration

---

## What It Carries

### Core Systems

- pressurized crew compartment
- life support with recycled air and water loop
- thermal regulation for Mars surface operations
- navigation and terrain mapping systems
- short-range comms and burst transmission package
- external suit lock / work access

### Salvage Equipment

- external manipulator arm
- thermal and mechanical cutting tools
- portable winch system
- modular tool racks
- patch kits and field access gear
- interface package for routine hardware recovery
- optional scout drone interface, if the GM wants to use one

### Sensor Suite

- ground-penetrating radar
- short-range lidar
- thermal imaging
- passive signal analysis / comm scan
- close-range structural read capability

### Storage

- limited internal cargo space
- external clamp mounts for bulky salvage
- manual securing required for fragile items
- no comfortable surplus capacity

**GM Note:** The Dustbreaker can recover **parts**, modules, and cut sections. It cannot casually remove large buried structures.

---

## Limitations

- not fast
- not armored
- not designed for combat
- cannot lift large structures
- interior space is tight and functional
- field repairs are possible, but never free
- any major breakdown turns the mission into a survival problem

---

## GM Note — Using the Dustbreaker Well

In Act 1, the Dustbreaker is not just transport. It is the crew’s normal world.

Use it to reinforce:

- routine competence
- work culture
- physical dependence on infrastructure
- constant low-level pressure

The players should understand early that leaving the Dustbreaker is always a meaningful step. Outside is exposure, effort, time, and risk.

---

# Employer and Contract Context

## Tharsis Recovery & Leasing

A mid-tier Mars surface contractor specializing in:

- refurbished salvage vehicles
- low-margin recovery contracts
- distributed crew operations
- contract fulfillment in marginal terrain

Tharsis is not a glamorous company. It makes money by sending durable crews in durable machines to do unattractive work that larger operators do not prioritize.

The company does not need to be villainous. It simply operates according to narrow margins and limited patience.

### What Tharsis Feels Like in Practice

- vehicles are kept in service as long as economically possible
- crews are expected to solve most problems themselves
- support exists, but often at delay and at distance
- contract language is deliberately narrow
- anything outside scope becomes a cost question very quickly
- discover something big enough, and the company will absolutely care — later

### What They Care About

- contract completion
- recoverable value
- preventing avoidable equipment loss
- keeping operations moving
- clean reporting when required

### What They Do Not Care About

- crew comfort
- curiosity
- long exploratory detours
- nuanced historical significance
- anything that does not improve recoverable value or reduce liability

---

## Why This Crew Got the Job

This is a **low-priority clustered recovery contract** built around weak confidence data and modest expected return.

Why Tharsis assigned the Dustbreaker:

- the anomaly looked real enough to justify a small recovery crew
- the likely asset value did not justify a larger operation
- the wrecks were unmanned and low-claim
- the location was inconvenient but manageable
- this kind of job is exactly what a rover like the Dustbreaker is for

At the start of the scenario, nobody at Tharsis believes this site is a major discovery.

---

## Why the Job Matters to the Crew

The crew takes this job because routine work still matters.

Possible motivations include:

- standard contract pay
- salvage percentage or performance bonus
- maintaining good standing with Tharsis
- keeping the Dustbreaker active and justified
- covering lease, maintenance, or debt pressure
- hoping the paperwork undersold the site a little

The crew does not need to be desperate. But they should be grounded enough to care about a small contract and alert enough to notice when a small contract stops behaving like one.

---

## Contract Summary

**Objective:**  
Recover, identify, and strip three unmanned assets.

**Source of Site Data:**  
Low-confidence orbital anomaly / secondary terrain review

**Site Condition:**  
Clustered debris signatures over roughly 2 km radius

**Expected Value:**  
Low to moderate

**Expected Difficulty:**  
Routine surface recovery in rough terrain

**Official Scope:**  
Locate, assess, recover viable components, report extraction-relevant anomalies

**Not Included:**  
Deep survey, excavation of major structures, extended off-contract exploration

---

## Optional Company Contact

**Field Coordinator:** _R. Halden_  
**Availability:** delayed comms / burst transmission only

Halden is useful if the crew wants:

- clarification on scope
- authorization for minor deviation
- confirmation on reporting obligations
- a voice from the contract side

Halden should sound dry, competent, and efficient. Not cruel. Not invested. Just professionally narrow.

### Example Response

> “Dustbreaker, confirm visual on assets.  
> No extended survey authorized.  
> Recover what’s viable and move on.  
> Report anomalies if they affect extraction.”

### Other Example Lines

> “If the site is stable and the assets are recoverable, proceed.”
> 
> “Historical relevance is not part of the contract.”
> 
> “Do not expand scope unless recovery value justifies it.”
> 
> “If you think you found something larger, document first. Do not damage anything expensive by guessing.”

---

# Starting the Scene

## Suggested Opening Crew Beats

Before the first scan, give the crew a few minutes to sound like a real working team. Useful prompts:

- Who is monitoring route and terrain load?
- Who is already complaining that the contract probably barely pays for the trip?
- Who checks suit seals and outside readiness out of habit?
- Who is most likely to want the easiest wreck first?
- Who is already suspicious of the orbital scan quality?
- Does anyone want to ping Halden before starting, or only if the site becomes inconvenient?

These do not need full scenes. They simply help establish tone before the mystery begins.

---

# Approach to Site

The route to the clustered site should not be dramatic, but it should feel like real Mars surface work:

- long sightlines
- bad ground hidden by dust
- uneven stress on the rover
- constant vehicle awareness
- small decisions that matter more than they would on Earth

This is a good place for the Driver / Vehicle Operator and Operations Specialist to contribute early.

---

## Read Aloud — Arrival

> The terrain opens slightly as you approach the coordinates.
> 
> Not flat. Not safe.
> 
> But open enough to see further than before.
> 
> Your systems begin to pick up returns.
> 
> Faint. Scattered.
> 
> Metal signatures under dust.
> 
> More than one.

# Gameplay — Scan Phase

The Dustbreaker’s first task is not excavation. It is interpretation.

The crew knows there are supposed to be three small unmanned losses in the area. The first useful question is whether the site matches the contract summary.

At first glance, it does.

On second look, not quite.

---

## Initial Scan

The Dustbreaker detects:

- 3 separate objects
- spread across roughly 2 km
- partially buried
- all inactive
- no strong active transmissions
- no obvious large debris field

The signatures differ in size, depth, and apparent condition.

---

## What the Crew Can Realistically Conclude Early

Even before anything becomes truly strange, competent operators may notice:

- these assets do not look like one clean cluster event
- they are not all from the same technological generation
- burial depth and exposure differ
- at least one seems to have stopped in place rather than crashed violently

This should read as **professional irregularity**, not immediate alarm.

---

## Suggested Skill Checks

Use as needed, not all at once.

### Plot Safe Approach Routes

**Piloting / Operations**

**Success:**

- identify safest route for the Dustbreaker
- reduce travel time between targets
- avoid unstable ground or wheel stress
- determine which object is easiest to approach first

**Strong Success:**

- identify a route order that saves time later
- notice that all three objects sit in positions that feel unrelated to one crash sequence

**Failure:**

- longer route
- awkward repositioning
- minor terrain complication
- extra time spent maneuvering

---

### Characterize the Site

**Operations / Science**

**Success:**

- confirm artificial origin of all three signatures
- estimate size, burial depth, and orientation
- identify inconsistent age / generation differences

**Strong Success:**

- determine that the three objects are from noticeably different periods or design families
- conclude the site is a cluster only in geography, not in origin

**Failure:**

- unclear classification
- one object misread until approached more closely
- incomplete estimate of object condition

---

### Read the Buried Geometry

**Engineering / Operations / Tech**

**Success:**

- identify whether each object is intact, broken, or partly accessible
- estimate what tools are needed for access
- detect that at least one object is not lying in a normal impact orientation

**Strong Success:**

- flag one object as a likely “best first recovery”
- notice inconsistent shallow subsurface returns in the wider area without yet fully identifying the buried facility

**Failure:**

- object orientation remains uncertain
- excavation or physical access required before good assessment
- wasted time and minor repositioning

---

## Early Hint — What Feels Off

On good results, tell the players some version of the following:

- the objects are **not impact clustered**
- spacing feels **too coincidental or too unrelated**
- there is no clean crash logic
- they may all have ended up here for different reasons
- one or more appear to have been **trying to do something** before shutdown

Do not oversell this. In Act 1, the site should feel wrong in a professional sense, not eerie in a theatrical sense.

---

# Moving Between Objects

Travel between targets is part of the job. Use it to reinforce that:

- the site is spread out
- time passes
- terrain matters
- nothing happens instantly on Mars

Good minor complications:

- uneven rock shelves under dust
- wheel placement issues
- sensor clutter from mineral reflections
- line-of-sight problems for local drone use
- a minor delay while choosing whether to expose crew or use the manipulator first

These are not meant as threats. They are meant as texture.

---

# The First Objective

The crew selects one of the three returns and approaches for inspection.

For best pacing, the first wreck should usually be the **Old Relay Drone**. It is the least dramatic object, which makes it the best opening reveal.

---

> The signal sharpens as you close in.
> 
> At first, there is nothing but dust and stone.
>

> 
> A hard line. A shape.
> 
> Metal under red dust.
>

# First Wreck — Old Relay Drone

## Description

A small relay drone or remote comm support unit, partially buried and heavily weathered.

Visible features:

- broken mast or comm assembly
- surface wear from long exposure
- partial burial in dust and compacted regolith
- little or no surrounding debris
- no clear crater
- no obvious impact scatter

It looks dead, old, and unimportant.

That is exactly why it works well as the first find.

---

## First Impression

The relay drone does **not** read like a dramatic wreck.

It reads like something that:

- stopped working
- stayed where it was
- got buried slowly
- was forgotten

This is more unsettling to professionals than a clean crash would be.

---

## Interaction

### Expose and Access the Drone

**Engineering / Physical**

**Success:**

- clear access point without damaging key internals
- open casing or maintenance seam cleanly
- preserve internal board stacks and memory fragments

**Failure:**

- casing damage
- dust contamination of exposed internals
- slower access
- possible minor escalation seed for later pacing, if desired

---

### Evaluate the Interior

**Operations / Tech / Engineering**

**Success:**

- confirm long-term inactivity
- determine power loss or controlled shutdown rather than catastrophic destruction
- identify partial memory/log retention

**Strong Success:**

- recover a fragment showing repeated route or scan persistence
- determine the drone remained operational long enough to repeatedly interact with the same nearby point

**Failure:**

- corrupted logs
- damaged memory recovery
- only broad confirmation of age and inactivity

---

### Strip Useful Components

**Engineering / Operations**

**Success:**

- recover minor salvage value
- secure a few reusable parts
- leave with enough to justify the stop materially

**Failure:**

- limited recoverable value
- more wear than expected
- the object is mostly useful as information, not payout

---

## What’s Wrong

Keep this subtle in Act 1.

Useful findings:

- orientation does not match a violent crash
- no impact signature
- no broad debris fan
- shutdown sequence is unclear
- final behavior suggests task persistence rather than random failure

The key impression:

**It does not look destroyed.**  
**It looks like it stopped.**

---

## What the First Wreck Should Give the Crew

The first wreck should reward investigation with **just enough** information to carry momentum into Act 2.

Possible outputs:

- a partial route log
- repeated scan priority behavior
- one fixed location reference
- evidence that the drone remained focused on local survey rather than travel
- confirmation that it was here on purpose, at least at the end

Do not reveal the buried facility yet. But the crew should begin to suspect that the site is more coherent than the contract implied.

---

## Example Fragment

You do not need to present this as clean data. It can be a broken line, recovered pattern, or technician interpretation.

Examples:

- repeated local scan priority
- path correction toward fixed coordinate
- survey loop persistence after primary relay failure
- unresolved subsurface target tag
- incomplete local task repetition

These are better than a neat explanatory message. The scenario is stronger when the crew has to interpret pattern rather than receive briefing.

---

# Roleplay Opportunity After the First Wreck

This is a good point to let the crew talk.

Questions that may come up naturally:

- Is this cluster really just “three unmanned losses”?
- Why are the ages different?
- Why was this drone still focused on one local point?
- Do they continue by easiest recovery order, or now by anomaly value?
- Does anyone ping Halden with a “this site is a little off” message?
- Does anyone already start thinking there might be something under the dust worth more than the wrecks?

This should still feel like working-crew judgment, not instant conspiracy thinking.

---

# End of Act 1

By the end of Act 1, the crew should have:

- arrived at the site
- established the Dustbreaker as their working environment
- performed initial scans
- identified the three objects as inconsistent with one simple debris event
- inspected and partially recovered the first wreck
- found evidence that the site is older, less random, and less worthless than expected

Nothing is openly impossible yet.  
Nothing clearly supernatural is happening.  
But the crew should now understand one important thing:

**The cluster is real. The explanation on paper probably isn’t.**

---

## Transition to Act 2

The next object — usually the **Survey Probe** — should move the scenario from “professional irregularity” to “active anomaly.”

Act 2 introduces:

- stronger evidence of intentional subsurface interest
- the first real signal
- the realization that the wrecks are not the prize
- the first choice to go beyond routine recovery logic

---

## GM Note — Desired Feel of Act 1

Act 1 should feel:

- grounded
- procedural
- occupational
- slightly underpaid
- technically believable
- quietly interesting

It should not yet feel like full horror.

That restraint is important. The deeper unease of Act 2 and the machine-identity pressure of Act 3 work much better if the scenario begins as a completely plausible salvage job.

---

## Optional Additions the GM Can Use

### If You Want More Crew Texture

Add one or two small moments before first contact:

- someone checking a worn seal or tool latch
- a short argument about route efficiency
- a complaint about Tharsis underpaying another long drive
- a reminder to log all recovered parts cleanly

### If You Want More World Texture

Mention one or two practical realities:

- burst comm delays with company control
- surface recovery claims are often bought cheap from weak scan data
- half the business is deciding what is not worth digging out
- old Martian losses stay buried until chance, erosion, or economics make them visible again

### If You Want a Slightly Stronger Hook

Let the first wreck contain a corrupted but repeated task behavior that clearly points toward one nearby subsurface location. Not enough to explain anything — just enough to make professionals say:  
**That is not random.**
