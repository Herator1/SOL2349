# CT 2 — THE SIGNAL

## Scene Frame

Act 2 turns the clustered wreck site from a routine recovery contract into a real opportunity.

The second and third assets confirm that the site is not random. All three objects were drawn toward the same buried point. None of them appears to have simply crashed there. None of them resolves what they found. Together, they suggest a hidden subsurface system that remained unnoticed long enough for multiple unrelated machines to encounter it across different periods.

At this stage, the crew should not yet feel trapped or under attack.

They should feel:

- intrigued
- professionally alert
- increasingly suspicious
- tempted by the possibility of an unregistered buried asset
- aware that this could become far more valuable than the original contract

Act 2 works best if the crew begins making a very practical calculation:

**Do we finish the job as contracted — or do we go after the real find?**

---

# Second Wreck — Survey Probe

## Read Aloud

> The second signal sits lower.
> 
> Buried deeper.
> 
> You do not see it at first — only the scan confirms that something is there.
> 
> When enough dust is cleared away, the shape emerges.
> 
> Cleaner than the first.  
> Newer.
> 
> And mostly intact.

---

## Description

A compact autonomous survey probe, partially buried but in much better condition than the relay drone.

Visible features:

- compact sealed chassis
- partially deployed sensor array
- minimal external abrasion
- no obvious impact crater
- one side sunken deeper into old dust layering
- preserved access seams and service points

Unlike the relay drone, this unit looks as if it reached the site in working condition and remained there long enough to conduct repeated scan operations.

---

## Why It Matters

The survey probe is the first strong indication that the cluster is not just coincidence.

It suggests:

- deliberate survey activity
- repeated local investigation
- unresolved target priority
- a site interesting enough to hold a probe in place beyond normal mission logic

This is where the crew should begin to think less like scrap cutters and more like professionals who may have stumbled across a hidden claim.

---

## Interaction

### Access the Probe

**Engineering / Operations**

**Success:**

- casing opened cleanly
- internal systems preserved
- memory core and sensor bus accessible

**Failure:**

- slower access
- one subsystem damaged during entry
- partial data loss
- more time spent exposed outside

---

### Recover Probe Data

**Tech / Operations / Science**

**Success:**

- partial survey logs recovered
- local mapping cycles visible
- repeated scan passes centered on one subsurface point

**Strong Success:**

- identifies that the probe repeatedly changed scan profile to penetrate the same buried geometry
- shows the probe cycling through multiple sensor modes and failing to classify the target
- reveals the target was flagged internally as **artificial but unresolved**

**Failure:**

- fragmented logs only
- enough to show repeated survey behavior, but not enough to explain why

---

### Evaluate Condition and Salvage

**Engineering**

**Success:**

- recover moderate-value components
- secure sensor head, control board, or intact scan package
- confirm the probe remained functional for a significant period on site

**Failure:**

- useful salvage present, but more worn or contaminated than expected
- data value exceeds parts value

---

## What the Probe Reveals

The probe should provide more than “same point below.”

Useful findings include:

- repeated scan focus on a fixed subsurface coordinate
- multiple failed attempts at classification
- inconsistent return geometry
- false depth resolution
- evidence of a buried void or layered artificial structure
- persistent task behavior after scan ambiguity should have triggered disengagement

The key discovery:

**The probe did not just notice something below.**  
**It stayed to understand it — and failed.**

That alone makes the site more interesting.

---

## Suggested Data Fragments

Do not present these as full clean logs. Better as interpreted fragments, broken machine summaries, or clipped technical lines.

Examples:

- unresolved subsurface geometry
- confidence collapse after repeated scan passes
- local target retained despite classification failure
- artificial return / topology mismatch
- scan recursion exceeded
- access vector unavailable
- no registered site beacon detected

That last one matters. It suggests the buried system is **not properly registered**, or no longer visible in known surface infrastructure.

---

# First Clear Signal Detection

The signal is usually detected after deeper analysis of the probe or through active local scanning.

This is the first moment when the buried site stops being an abstract anomaly and becomes a potentially valuable target.

---

## Read Aloud

> Your systems pick it up clean.
> 
> Not noise. Not interference.
> 
> A signal.
> 
> Fixed position. Below the surface.
> 
> Stable. Repeating.
> 
> Structured — but wrong.
> 
> It does not answer.  
> It does not drift.  
> It does not behave like a beacon.
> 
> It just runs.

---

## What Makes the Signal Interesting

The signal should feel specific enough to matter.

It is:

- fixed to one subsurface point
- stable over time
- clearly artificial
- not part of any familiar local network
- low-power but persistent
- packetized in repeated bursts
- internally structured, but inconsistent from cycle to cycle

This is not a normal emergency ping, locator beacon, survey handshake, or salvage tag.

It looks more like a machine process leaking outward through damaged or degraded transmission pathways.

---

## What the Crew Can Notice

With competent analysis, the crew may conclude:

- this is not random machine noise
- the signal is being generated intentionally by a functioning system
- it may be old, degraded, or translated through damaged infrastructure
- parts of it resemble communication framing, indexing, or synchronization behavior
- but the internal payload does not resolve into a coherent known protocol

The important tone:

**This is not “a ghost signal.”**  
It is **an active system doing something real**, in a way the crew cannot easily classify.

---

## Signal Hooks for Salvagers

This is where the buried site starts to look like money.

To a professional salvage crew, this signal suggests:

- active buried hardware
- residual power
- preserved subsurface structure
- possibly sealed internal systems
- maybe a site no one fully logged or reclaimed
- maybe a forgotten old installation with surviving technical value

That is enough to justify risk.

---

## Suggested Skill Checks

### Signal Analysis

**Science / Operations / Tech**

**Success:**

- confirms artificial origin
- confirms repeated packet structure
- confirms non-standard or obsolete protocol behavior

**Strong Success:**

- identifies nested timing layers, suggesting a complex internal system rather than a simple beacon
- detects internal formatting consistency with corrupted or incomplete payload logic
- concludes the signal may be a byproduct of active processing rather than a deliberate external transmission

**Failure:**

- can confirm only that it is artificial and unusual
- no clean classification

---

### Interpret Salvage Value

**Operations / Engineering / Commerce-style judgment if applicable**

**Success:**

- recognizes that an active buried system is worth far more than the surface wrecks
- identifies likely value categories: data, sealed hardware, interface components, rare legacy tech

**Strong Success:**

- realizes that the best value may not be bulk salvage but **portable internal fragments**
- concludes that if the site is unregistered, reporting it too early could cost the crew control of the find

**Failure:**

- can tell it is unusual, but not what kind of value it implies

---

### Subsurface Resolution

**Tech / Engineering / Science**

**Success:**

- detects buried non-natural geometry
- confirms void space or structural layering below
- identifies vertical elements consistent with shafting, conduits, or reinforced access structures

**Strong Success:**

- picks up multiple geometric returns, suggesting this is not a small buried box or single drone nest
- flags a larger structure than expected
- suggests internal segmentation or stacked subsurface construction

**Failure:**

- returns remain partial and distorted
- enough to confirm artificial structure, but not enough to size it confidently

# Third Wreck — Autonomous Crawler

## Read Aloud

> The third signal is easier to see.
> 
> Something small sits canted against a rock shelf, half exposed by wind and shifting dust.
> 
> Smaller than the probe.  
> More recent.
> 
> One side is crushed.  
> The rest looks like it kept trying to work anyway.

---

## Description

A small autonomous crawler or utility scout unit, visibly newer than the other two assets.

Visible features:

- compact tracked or multi-wheel chassis
- structural crush damage along one side
- external tool or sample arm partly extended
- sensor mast bent or snapped
- drive section jammed
- partial exposure above dustline

This unit feels active in a way the relay drone did not. It looks like it physically tried to reach something and failed in the process.

---

## Interaction

### Access the Crawler

**Engineering / Physical**

**Success:**

- cracked housing opened
- internal routing and memory access possible
- movement logs recoverable

**Failure:**

- access is awkward
- damaged internals complicate retrieval
- more time and manual work required

---

### Recover Movement Logs

**Tech / Operations**

**Success:**

- pathing behavior visible
- repeated route correction toward the same subsurface point
- evidence of obstacle avoidance and renewed approach attempts

**Strong Success:**

- confirms the crawler attempted to physically reach or enter a local surface feature near the signal origin
- shows it repeatedly rerouted after terrain collapse, blockage, or inaccessible descent geometry
- suggests it was trying to find an access path, not just scan from range

**Failure:**

- incomplete route history
- enough to show repeated local focus, but not intent

---

### Evaluate Recent Activity

**Operations / Engineering / Science**

**Success:**

- confirms the crawler is more recent than the other two finds
- shows that the site continued attracting automated attention across a long time gap

**Strong Success:**

- identifies that the crawler likely had no reason to remain here unless it detected something worth investigating
- implies that different generations of systems independently converged on the same buried target

**Failure:**

- age difference obvious, but behavioral conclusion remains weak

---

## What the Crawler Adds

The crawler makes the pattern harder to dismiss.

The first wreck suggests local persistence.  
The second suggests unresolved buried structure.  
The third suggests attempted physical access.

That matters.

The crew can now reasonably conclude:

- three different unmanned systems from different periods interacted with the same buried point
- at least one tried to scan it
- at least one tried to reach it
- none resolved it
- none clearly failed due to a simple crash event

At this point, the buried structure becomes the real objective whether the crew admits it out loud or not.

---

# The Pattern

By now the crew should be able to piece together the following:

- this is not a random debris field
- the three wrecks are a history of failed contact with the same buried target
- the target remained hidden long enough to outlast multiple machine generations
- no registered claim marker, access beacon, or active surface infrastructure explains it
- whatever is below is still running at least enough to produce a signal

This is the moment when Act 2 should shift from **recovery job** to **possible hidden score**.

---

# The Hook — Why the Crew Might Go Off-Contract

A buried active structure on Mars can imply several forms of value:

- intact old hardware
- sealed subsurface systems
- portable technical data
- legacy processing components
- rare salvage outside normal circulation
- claimable information no one else has yet logged

The crew does not need to know what the structure is.  
They only need enough evidence to believe it is:

- real
- unregistered
- accessible
- potentially worth more than the contract they were sent to fulfill

That is the hook.

# Locating the Entry Point

Once the crew decides to investigate further, the next step is practical:

Where is the easiest way in?

The signal origin and wreck behavior should lead them toward a specific area of irregular terrain where buried structure approaches the surface.

---

## Suggested Skill Checks

### Terrain and Structural Read

**Operations / Science / Engineering**

**Success:**

- identify a disturbed subsurface pattern
- isolate likely access-related geometry
- distinguish natural rock layering from buried artificial structure

**Strong Success:**

- identify a collapsed shaft or service penetration
- determine likely original direction of descent
- find the safest approach point

**Failure:**

- more manual searching required
- false leads among surface irregularities
- more time lost before the right spot is found

---

### Physical Search and Exposure

**Survival / Engineering / Physical**

**Success:**

- uncover part of a structural rim, support frame, or access collar
- confirm the buried point includes a vertical artificial element

**Failure:**

- unstable dust collapse
- awkward footing
- slower progress
- possible first escalation seed if you want to start pressure early

---

# The Access Shaft

## Read Aloud

> The ground gives way slowly.
> 
> Not a crater.  
> Not a sinkhole.
> 
> A structure.
> 
> Straight edges under dust.  
> Uniform material.  
> Reinforced internal geometry.
> 
> Something vertical.
> 
> Something built to go down.

---

## Description

The access point is not a neat door or hatch. It is a partially collapsed vertical shaft, likely a service, cooling, or maintenance penetration associated with the buried complex below.

Features:

- partly choked with regolith
- structural frame still partly intact
- no visible external markings
- no readable ownership tag
- no human-friendly signage
- materials older and stranger than routine surface infrastructure

This should feel less like “we found a bunker entrance” and more like “we found part of a buried machine.”

---

## Why This Matters

The shaft confirms three important things at once:

- the signal comes from a real buried installation
- the site is larger than a single object
- there may be physical access without heavy excavation equipment

That is enough to push most salvage crews into a serious internal debate.

---

# Clearing the Entrance

Reaching the shaft does not mean the crew can immediately descend. They must first make it usable.

Possible tasks:

- clearing loose regolith
- cutting away collapsed braces
- securing anchor points
- checking structural stability
- deciding what gear to bring down
- deciding what stays with the Dustbreaker

This is a good practical moment to emphasize:

- they are not equipped for a full underground expedition
- every extra tool, light, battery, and carry container matters
- if they go down, they are choosing improvisation

---

## Suggested Checks

### Clear and Stabilize Access

**Engineering / Physical**

**Success:**

- access route cleared
- descent path identified
- anchor points or movement plan secured

**Strong Success:**

- find a safer partial descent route
- identify structural elements useful for climbing
- reduce early movement risk

**Failure:**

- unstable edges
- loose dust
- poor descent angle
- delayed access
- possible first formal Escalation +1 if the GM wants Act 3 pressure to begin immediately after entry

---

# Tone Shift

This is the pivot point.

Up to now, the crew has been working a surface salvage problem.

Once the shaft is open, the scenario changes.

Not because the site suddenly attacks them.  
Because they now have evidence of:

- a buried active installation
- unknown age and purpose
- no obvious registration
- possible intact value below
- a way in

The emotional shift should be:  
**interest first, unease second**

---

# First Descent

## Read Aloud

> The shaft drops into darkness.
> 
> Not wide. Not narrow.  
> Just wrong for you.
> 
> Structural members line the interior — conduits, supports, recessed channels, mounting points.
> 
> No ladder. No steps. No clean human path.
> 
> Just depth.
> 
> And the signal, somewhere below.

---

## Descent Feel

The first descent should establish the core architectural truth of the buried facility:

**This place was not built around human movement.**

Make that clear through:

- awkward spacing
- strange support placement
- poor handholds
- irregular interior orientation
- surfaces meant for service machinery, not people
- the sense that humans can pass through here, but were never the design priority

---

## Hazards

Keep these practical and not yet severe:

- fall risk
- loose regolith from the upper breach
- awkward movement in suits or field gear
- poor line of sight
- uncertain footing
- limited ability to move bulky tools

This is not yet the point for overt hostility. The site is difficult before it becomes dangerous.

---

# First Unsettling Elements

These should stay light and deniable.

Good early signs:

- interior surfaces are too regular to be natural but not arranged in familiar human ways
- dust distribution is uneven, as if parts of the shaft remained protected or periodically disturbed
- faint temperature gradients appear where they should not
- small vibrations travel through the structure at irregular intervals
- the signal reads stronger here, but not more understandable

These details should create unease through **machine persistence**, not menace.

---

# First Light Cognitive Effect

Keep the first effect small enough that the crew can rationalize it.

Trigger examples:

- open comm channel during descent
- helmet audio bleed
- local signal amplification
- passing a sensor node or structural choke point

Possible manifestations:

- a clipped fragment of one crew member’s voice repeated with wrong timing
- a half-second echo that is not a normal comm reflection
- a broken phoneme cluster that sounds familiar but resolves into nothing
- a tag-like burst that briefly resembles a name and then collapses into noise

The important point:  
it should feel strange, but not yet like proof of anything impossible.

---

## Optional Read Aloud

> Something flickers across the comms.
> 
> Not static.  
> Not a full transmission.
> 
> Just a fragment.
> 
> A voice shape more than a voice.
> 
> Familiar for half a second.
> 
> Then gone.

---

# Act 2 Endpoint

By the end of Act 2, the crew should be:

- committed to the buried site
- physically inside the shaft
- aware that the structure is real and potentially valuable
- aware that the signal is active, artificial, and deeply unusual
- curious enough to continue
- unsettled enough to know this is no longer a normal recovery job

They should **not** yet fully understand the danger.

That comes later.

---

## Final State of the Crew’s Knowledge

They now know:

- all three wrecks interacted with the same buried target
- the target is artificial and still active
- the structure below is real
- there is at least one accessible descent path
- the site may be worth far more than the original contract
- whatever is generating the signal does not behave like any normal salvage beacon or local system

That is enough to carry the scenario cleanly into Act 3.

---

## Transition to Act 3

Act 3 begins when the crew leaves the shaft and enters the facility proper.

That is where:

- the architecture becomes fully non-human
- the facility starts responding more clearly
- the truth/profit/survival trade-off takes over
- escalation becomes the central pressure mechanic

---

## GM Note — Desired Feel of Act 2

Act 2 is the pivot.

- **Act 1:** routine
- **Act 2:** discovery
- **Act 3:** pressure

Do not rush into fear too early.  
The strongest version of this act makes the crew want to keep going.

Because it looks valuable.  
Because it looks rare.  
Because it looks like no one else has found it yet.

And only then, because it starts to feel wrong.
