## Plot Summary for the Gamemaster

The crew of the **MCRV Dustbreaker** is on a routine low-priority salvage contract for **Tharsis Recovery & Leasing**. Their job is simple: recover and strip three small unmanned assets scattered across an old Martian surface cluster. None of the wrecks looks especially valuable. The real anomaly is the pattern linking them: all three were drawn toward the same buried subsurface point.

That buried point is the real find.

Beneath the regolith lies an **AI-era cognition facility**. It is not a bunker, a habitat, or a normal research site. It was built to process **human-origin cognitive data** and reconstruct personhood as a distributed machine system. The project failed. It never produced stable consciousness. The central intelligence is gone. What remains are partial archives, damaged processing logic, fragmented self-model routines, and machine systems still trying to complete work they were never able to finish.

Once the crew enters the facility, the site begins treating them as **new input**. It records their voices, movement, biometrics, stress patterns, and faces. It does not communicate clearly and it does not behave like a haunted intelligence. Instead, it classifies, imitates, and recombines human patterns badly. The crew starts encountering broken voice fragments, wrong identity tags, distorted facial meshes, and flawed model outputs built from their own presence. The horror of the scenario comes from being processed by a system that does not understand what a person is.

Structurally, the scenario moves from routine work to buried discovery and then into a true hard-sci-fi dungeon crawl. **Act 1** establishes the Dustbreaker, the contract, and the surface wrecks as a grounded salvage job. **Act 2** reveals the signal, the buried structure, and the possibility of a much larger off-contract find. **Act 3** is the main play engine: a vertical, non-human machine space where the crew must choose between **truth, profit, and survival**. They cannot fully secure all three.

The crew can leave the site with some combination of:

- evidence of what the facility was
- portable cognition data
- rare physical hardware
- or one unique high-value relic

But they cannot take everything. The deeper they go, the more the facility escalates. Routes become harder, systems become more active, the mimicry grows more personal, and retreat becomes less certain. The best rewards are also the most dangerous to secure.

The final decision is pure **SOL 2349**: the crew can report the site honestly, file a limited report while hiding the best find, or conceal the discovery and try to move it off-contract. None of these outcomes is clean. Reporting means losing control of the real prize. Partial truth creates leverage and risk. Black salvage offers the highest upside, but also the highest chance that the find becomes a larger problem than the contract ever was.

### In one sentence

**Voices Below Mars** is a hard-sci-fi salvage dungeon in which a routine Martian recovery job leads a working crew into a buried AI-era cognition facility that begins using them as live input, forcing costly choices between knowledge, money, and getting back out alive.


## What Is Really Going On?

- The buried site is an **AI-era cognition facility** built to process **human-origin cognitive and behavioral data** and reconstruct personhood as a distributed machine system.
- The project failed. It never achieved stable human consciousness. Instead, it produced fragmented cognition models, broken self-continuity, and incomplete identity structures.
- The central intelligence is gone. What remains are partial archives, damaged processing routines, and machine systems still executing parts of their original purpose.
- When the crew enters the facility, the system begins treating them as **new input**. It records their voices, movement, biometrics, and stress patterns, then folds those into ongoing reconstruction logic.
- The system does **not** understand the crew and does **not** communicate with them in any true sense. The strange outputs they encounter are failed classifications, bad mimicry, and broken attempts to assemble human models from incomplete data.
- The site still contains real value: portable cognition data, rare legacy hardware, and one major deep relic. But most of the original infrastructure is gone, degraded, or too deeply integrated to extract safely.
- The core conflict of the scenario is simple: the crew can pursue **truth**, **profit**, or **survival** — but the deeper they go and the more they take, the harder it becomes to leave with all three intact.
