This page is about making scenes feel challenging without making them slow.

In _SOL 2349_, difficulty should stay broad and readable. Tension should come less from rules clutter and more from pressure, bad conditions, time loss, damage, strain, and difficult choices.

## Core Difficulty Benchmarks

Use these as your default DCs:

- **DC 8** — routine
- **DC 10** — standard
- **DC 13** — challenging
- **DC 16** — hard
- **DC 19** — extreme
- **DC 22+** — near-impossible

These values already match the current core rules.

## What These Numbers Mean in Play

With your current bonus range, a character usually rolls somewhere between **+1 and +10**, with **+10** being an exceptional expert before situational effects; the wiki’s current benchmark examples list **+2, +4, +6, +8, +10** as rough competence tiers.

A good practical reading is:

- **DC 8** — almost automatic for trained people unless conditions are bad
- **DC 10** — the default professional task
- **DC 13** — a real challenge for ordinary specialists
- **DC 16** — serious pressure, danger, or complexity
- **DC 19** — only strong skill, strong conditions, or tactical advantage make this reliable
- **DC 22+** — desperate, experimental, or “this probably should not work” territory

## Fast GM Rule

Start from the task itself, then adjust once.

A good default is:

- **routine task** → DC 8
- **normal risky task** → DC 10
- **difficult task** → DC 13
- **emergency / hostile conditions** → DC 16
- **borderline insane attempt** → DC 19+

Do not stack tiny modifiers if you can avoid it. Your own rules already recommend making **one meaningful adjustment** instead of many small ones.

## When to Raise the DC

Raise the DC by about **+2 to +3** when the task itself becomes meaningfully harder.

Good reasons include:

- poor tools
- bad visibility
- zero-g or unstable footing
- smoke, vacuum, heat, radiation, or damage
- rushed work under pressure
- compromised systems

If the base task was **DC 10**, bad conditions usually make it **DC 13**.  
If it was already difficult at **DC 13**, serious pressure may push it to **DC 16**.

## When Not to Raise the DC

Do **not** raise the DC just because the scene is dramatic.

If the main pressure comes from timing, risk, exposure, or consequences, keep the DC where it is and make failure hurt more. Your current rules also point to using **Tactical Edge and Setback** and existing penalties from **Time & Actions**, **Load**, injuries, or equipment when those already apply.

## Build Tension with Consequences

A scene gets tense when failure changes the situation.

Good failure costs:

- lose time
- take damage
- gain strain
- attract attention
- worsen position
- damage equipment
- reduce later options

This is usually better than turning every roll into a very high DC.

## Simple Tension Ladder

If a scene needs more pressure, escalate in layers:

1. inconvenience
2. delay
3. danger
4. crisis

Example:  
A repair starts at **DC 10**.  
Under time pressure it stays **DC 10**, but failure costs time.  
Once the system overheats, it becomes **DC 13**.  
Once people may die if it fails, the scene is now in crisis, even if the DC stays the same.

## Practical Benchmarks by Bonus

Using your current bonus assumptions, this is a useful GM shortcut:

- **+1 to +2** — DC 10 is already meaningful
- **+4** — DC 10 is reliable, DC 13 is real work
- **+6** — DC 13 is solid, DC 16 is dangerous
- **+8** — DC 16 is achievable, DC 19 is serious
- **+10** — DC 19 becomes plausible, DC 22+ is still exceptional

That keeps the scale readable and helps you judge quickly what kind of scene you are actually creating.

## Good GM Questions

When setting a check, ask:

- what is the base difficulty?
- what makes it harder right now?
- what happens if they fail?
- what gets worse while they try?

If you can answer those four questions, you usually have enough.

## In One Sentence

Use **DC 8 / 10 / 13 / 16 / 19 / 22+** as your backbone, and build tension through pressure and consequences rather than endlessly raising numbers.
