Conflict in _SOL 2349_ is rarely simple, rarely clean, and only sometimes openly violent.

The Solar System is too interconnected, too infrastructure-dependent, and too politically fragmented for conflict to take the form of constant total war. Most actors are tied into systems they cannot afford to destroy lightly: trade corridors, station networks, transport chains, legal regimes, labor structures, data infrastructure, financing, and access to life support, spare parts, or fuel. That does not make the setting peaceful. It makes conflict more constrained, more layered, and often more indirect. The broader lore of the setting reflects exactly this kind of distributed, faction-rich political structure rather than a single dominant empire or clean two-sided front.

## Why Large Wars Are Rare

Large-scale war is possible in theory, but usually irrational in practice.

Major powers in _SOL 2349_ are deeply dependent on vulnerable systems that are difficult to replace once damaged. Habitats can fail. Corridors can collapse. Industrial nodes can be isolated. Shipping delays can cascade into shortages. Even a successful campaign may leave the victor economically weaker, politically exposed, and dependent on the same damaged infrastructure it helped disrupt. The setting’s existing political and faction material already points toward a world of overlapping power centers, arbitration structures, megacorporate influence, transit authorities, and regional blocs rather than stable war blocs built for open systemic conquest.

For that reason, conflict usually stops short of full mobilization. Pressure builds, incidents multiply, people disappear, systems fail, contracts break, and violence happens — but often below the level of official war.

## What Conflict Usually Looks Like

Most conflicts in _SOL 2349_ take one of a few familiar forms.

Sometimes conflict appears as **economic pressure**: a route is restricted, a shipment is delayed, a contract is revoked, a refinery is starved of inputs, a labor force is squeezed, or a station is pushed into dependency.

Sometimes it appears as **political pressure**: regulation, investigation, denial of docking access, manipulated arbitration, selective enforcement, quiet intelligence work, or carefully limited security operations.

Sometimes it becomes **local violence**: raids, sabotage, covert recovery missions, assassinations, labor crackdowns, piracy, station fights, or deniable armed interventions.

And sometimes it looks like **systems under strain**: a maintenance shortfall, a suspicious accident, a data leak, an unexpected shortage, a failed relay, or an emergency that may or may not be accidental.

This is one of the key things a gamemaster should preserve: conflict in _SOL 2349_ is often real long before it becomes openly military.

## Human Motives Matter More Than Battle Lines

A good conflict in _SOL 2349_ usually begins with motives, not sides.

Who wants control?  
Who is under pressure?  
Who cannot afford delay?  
Who is hiding risk, cutting corners, enforcing loyalty, or trying to survive inside a structure that is already failing?

Because the setting is politically fragmented, most conflicts should not feel like one clean faction opposing another. They should feel like intersecting interests. A corporation may be divided internally. A local official may be acting against formal policy. A station authority may need outside help while publicly denying weakness. A labor action may be justified, manipulated, or both. A pirate attack may also be a proxy operation. A security response may be real, deniable, or opportunistic.

This usually creates more believable and more useful scenarios than a simple “enemy faction attacks” structure.

## Violence Is Real, But Not Neutral

Violence is a valid part of the setting, but it should not feel frictionless.

Gunfights on stations, boarding actions, assassinations, raids, and armed confrontations absolutely belong in _SOL 2349_. The rules support combat directly through the Core Mechanics and Combat sections, as well as dedicated Space Combat rules.

But violence should usually create consequences beyond the scene itself. It damages infrastructure, creates fear, changes legal exposure, escalates factional response, and leaves social traces. In a tightly connected and resource-sensitive world, even a small firefight may matter for much longer than the fight itself.

## Good Sources of Conflict for the GM

When building conflict in _SOL 2349_, it often helps to begin with one of these pressures:

- a contract that benefits one side and endangers another
- a failing system someone is trying to hide
- a shortage, delay, or missing shipment
- a relic, facility, or piece of data multiple actors want
- a labor, jurisdictional, or access dispute

These are strong starting points because they naturally produce layered decisions instead of immediate binary opposition.

## Conflict Scales Well When It Stays Specific

The best conflicts in the setting usually remain tied to a concrete location and a practical problem.

A transfer station under pressure is better than “regional instability” as an opening. A compromised cargo route is better than “trade war.” A strike, sabotage case, or sealed habitat section gives players something they can see, investigate, negotiate, and change.

The wider structures still matter, but they work best when they are visible through local effects.

## Using Existing Scenarios as Models

The material already present in the GM area points in the right direction. The wiki’s one-shot section includes **Cloudfall** and **Ghost Station**, both of which are structured around contained situations, local stakes, and layered pressures rather than open battlefield escalation. That makes them good models for how conflict in _SOL 2349_ is usually strongest: specific, motivated, dangerous, and embedded in place.

## Questions for the GM

When preparing a conflict, it helps to ask:

- What does each side actually want?
- Why is open war not the preferred option?
- What form is the conflict taking instead?
- Who is caught in the middle?
- What changes if the players intervene, escalate, or walk away?

Those questions usually produce conflicts that feel much more like _SOL 2349_ than a simple two-sided showdown.

## In One Sentence

Conflict in _SOL 2349_ is usually not a clean war between clear sides, but a layered struggle shaped by dependency, pressure, limited violence, and competing interests inside a fragile system.
