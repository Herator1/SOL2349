Power in _SOL 2349_ is rarely clean, centralized, or absolute.

The Solar System is not ruled by a single empire, one dominant ideology, or two clearly opposed blocs. Instead, power is distributed across megacorporations, planetary governments, station federations, corridor authorities, research bodies, labor institutions, criminal networks, and local actors who control access to something others need. The faction structure already visible in the wiki reflects exactly this kind of fragmented order: corporate, political, legal, criminal, and infrastructural power all coexist and overlap.

For the gamemaster, this matters because factions in _SOL 2349_ should usually be treated less as “teams” and more as systems of pressure, dependency, and competing priorities.

## Power Is Usually Functional

In this setting, power often comes less from formal sovereignty than from practical control.

A faction is powerful if it can move cargo, maintain habitats, enforce contracts, control data, mediate disputes, secure transit, supply fuel, manage labor, or protect infrastructure that others cannot afford to lose. That is why megacorporations, corridor authorities, station federations, and political blocs all matter at once. They do not dominate the setting in the same way, but each can shape events by controlling a different part of what keeps human civilization running. The wiki’s faction structure makes this especially visible by separating corporate actors, planetary power centers, transit authorities, regional blocs, labor and ethics bodies, and criminal networks into different but connected categories.

## Factions Should Rarely Feel Monolithic

A faction in _SOL 2349_ should almost never behave like a single unified mind.

Even powerful organizations should contain:

- internal disagreements
- competing departments
- regional priorities
- self-interested individuals
- inconsistent enforcement

A megacorporation may contain both ruthless extraction logic and local managers trying to prevent disaster. A station federation may publicly present unity while quietly splitting over resources, law, or outside influence. A labor organization may defend workers in one situation and become rigid or political in another. Criminal networks may be more reliable than legal actors in one corridor and more destructive in the next.

This kind of internal variation is important. It makes factions feel like institutions rather than alignment labels.

## Most Conflicts Are About Access

A very useful GM principle is this: most faction conflict in _SOL 2349_ is really conflict over access.

Access to what depends on the story:

- docking rights
- transport routes
- reactor fuel
- legal protection
- labor
- salvage claims
- research findings
- communication infrastructure
- station security
- political legitimacy

This is one reason the setting works so well at the scale of stations, ports, corridors, and industrial sites. Factions become interesting when their power is visible in concrete bottlenecks rather than abstract speeches.

## Megacorps Are Not Just Villains

The wiki already gives the megacorps a large and varied presence, including actors such as Archeron, Atlas, Helios, Kaiten, NovaChem, Nyx Data Systems, Shukra, and Solaris.

When running them at the table, it helps to avoid treating them as cartoon empires. Megacorporations should often be dangerous not because they are simplistically evil, but because they are indispensable, profit-driven, politically entangled, and structurally insulated from the human cost of their decisions. They run systems people rely on. They employ people who are not villains. They solve real problems. And they still cut corners, manipulate rules, bury failures, exploit dependence, and protect their own strategic advantage.

That complexity makes them more useful than a generic “bad corporation” antagonist.

## Political Factions Are Usually Constrained

The political side of the setting is also visibly diverse: Earth governance blocs, the Mars Planetary Consensus, the Venus Administrative Compact, the Jovian League, habitat federations, the Belt Arbitration Tribunal, the Interplanetary Science Authority, transit and corridor authorities, and more all appear as distinct entries in the wiki.

These factions should usually feel constrained rather than omnipotent. They govern through treaties, deterrence, mediation, procedure, scarcity, and selective enforcement, not through unlimited reach. They often depend on corporate infrastructure, local cooperation, and fragile compliance. That makes them useful sources of pressure, delay, and procedural conflict even when they are not openly hostile.

## Criminal Networks Fill the Gaps

The setting also contains organized crime, piracy, smuggling, shadow networks, and region-specific syndicates. Those are not side elements. They are part of how a fragmented system actually works. The faction tree explicitly includes criminal networks, space piracy, smuggling rings, corporate shadow networks, organized crime on Earth, and Venus syndicates.

At the table, criminal actors work best when they are treated as parallel infrastructure. They move goods, provide off-book access, solve problems legal systems cannot solve quickly, and exploit every gap left by formal power. In some stories they are predators. In others they are intermediaries. Often they are both.

## Local Power Often Matters Most

For actual play, the most important faction may not be a major bloc at all.

A dockmaster, station board, local fixer, security chief, labor organizer, convoy operator, maintenance supervisor, or corridor official may matter more to the scenario than a distant megacorp executive or planetary council. That is because power in _SOL 2349_ is often exercised locally, through control over immediate systems and decisions. The larger factions matter because their interests press downward into local life. But the people the players actually deal with are often much closer to the ground.

## Good Questions for the GM

When using factions in a scenario, it usually helps to ask:

- What does this faction actually control?
- What does it depend on?
- What can it not afford to lose?
- Who inside it wants something different?
- How does its power become visible in this specific place?

Those questions usually lead to better faction play than asking which faction is “the enemy.”

## In One Sentence

In _SOL 2349_, power belongs less to whoever claims the most authority and more to whoever controls the systems, access points, and dependencies that other people cannot live without.
