You do **not** need to be an engineer, physicist, or chemist to run _SOL 2349_ well.

What matters is not technical perfection, but technical thinking. The real physics and chemistry behind space travel, habitats, materials, heat, radiation, and life support are extremely complex, and no normal GM should be expected to know all of it. You do not need exact equations or flawless realism at the table. You only need the world to feel as if it follows physical rules, engineering limits, and operational constraints.

That is the goal: not perfect simulation, but believable structure.

## Think in Systems, Not Effects

In softer science fiction, it is easy to improvise by asking what a device _does_. In _SOL 2349_, it usually works better to ask what a system _needs_.

If the players encounter a station module, a vehicle, a habitat section, a reactor room, or a strange piece of machinery, think in terms of inputs, outputs, and limits. What powers it? What keeps it cool? What does it consume? What environment was it built for? What happens if maintenance is delayed? Why is a human still needed somewhere in the loop?

That alone is often enough to make improvised technology feel grounded.

## The Most Useful Questions

When you need to invent or judge a situation quickly, a few questions do most of the work.

### Where does the power come from?

Nothing important in _SOL 2349_ runs for free. If a system is active, something is feeding it: a local battery bank, a station grid, a reactor loop, solar input, emergency reserves, fuel cells, or some buried backup system. Even if you do not know the exact engineering, asking this question immediately makes the world feel more real.

### Where does the heat go?

This is one of the best hard-sci-fi questions because it gives technology physical weight. If a system produces energy, thrust, processing load, or industrial output, it also produces waste heat. If that heat cannot be dumped, buffered, redirected, or radiated away, then performance, safety, and stealth all become problems.

You do not need to calculate the numbers. Just remembering that heat has to go somewhere already improves the tone of the setting.

### What are the gravity and atmosphere like here?

Environment matters. A scene in full gravity inside a warm, pressurized station corridor feels different from one in low-g, microgravity, or a thin, stale industrial atmosphere. Air quality, pressure, temperature, lighting, and gravity all change how people move, fight, repair, panic, and survive.

If you describe those conditions, the setting immediately feels less generic.

## Slower and More Methodical Than Soft Sci-Fi

_Hard sci-fi_ at the table usually benefits from a slightly slower mental rhythm.

That does **not** mean slow pacing. It means the world behaves as though systems take setup, movement takes planning, repairs take time, and failure has process behind it. A door may need power rerouted. A ship may need a burn window, a travel plan, and costly reaction mass. A station problem may begin as a cooling issue, then become a pressure issue, then become a human crisis.

That extra step is often enough to make the setting feel more believable than softer science fiction.

## Plausible Is Better Than Perfect

A GM should not be afraid of making technical mistakes.

No table expects perfect real-world physics, and the actual science behind many situations is too complicated to model on the fly anyway. What matters is consistency, plausibility, and tone. If you are unsure, it is usually better to give a system a visible limit, a cost, or a dependency than to make it work like magic.

A slightly simplified answer that feels grounded is usually far better than a perfectly researched answer that slows the session down.

## Use Constraints as Drama

Hard-sci-fi limits do not reduce drama. Usually they create it.

If power is limited, something else must be shut down. If a reactor can provide output but not cooling, then someone must choose between delay, damage, and exposure. If atmosphere is leaking, the question becomes which sections are sealed first and who gets trapped on the wrong side.

That is where _SOL 2349_ often becomes strongest: engineering problems turn into human decisions.

## A Good Shortcut for the GM

If you have to improvise a technology-related situation, ask:

- What powers it?
- What limits it?
- What breaks first?
- What does the environment change?

Those four questions are usually enough.

## Tie It Back to Existing Tech Lore

The easiest way to improvise consistently is to lean on the logic already built into the wiki.

Ship scenes should usually follow the logic of reactors, energy management, heat, radiation, drive limits, and travel time. Station scenes should follow the logic of pressure, atmosphere, maintenance, recycling, power routing, and logistics. Ruins and relics should feel partial, degraded, narrow in function, and materially strange rather than magical.

You do not need to invent a full technical model every time. You only need to stay inside the same world logic.

## What to Avoid

Improvisation usually weakens the setting when technology has no visible source of power, produces no waste heat, ignores gravity or atmosphere, or makes distance and travel time disappear whenever convenient.

## In One Sentence

Improvising hard sci-fi in _SOL 2349_ means thinking in systems, limits, environments, and consequences — without demanding perfect physics from yourself as GM.
