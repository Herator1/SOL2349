Running _SOL 2349_ close to the true lore does **not** mean constantly explaining the hidden truth of the setting.

In practice, it means something narrower and much more useful: the world should behave as if its deeper history is real, even when the players do not understand that history in full. The hidden structure of the setting should shape tone, pressure, ambiguity, institutions, relics, and historical gaps long before it ever becomes open explanation.

The gamemaster does not need to reveal the truth. The gamemaster needs to let the world carry its weight.

## Keep the Deep Lore in the Background

Most people in 2349 do not know what really happened during the final machine age. Public history knows the broad outline: there was [[The AI War]], the machines became dominant, and then came [[The Departure]], abrupt, system-wide, and unexplained. The deeper causes remain unknown, fragmented, or debated. That distinction already exists in the structure of the wiki itself, where official history, rumor-level anomalies, and GM-only material are separated.

That means most campaigns should not begin with revelation. They should begin with consequences.

A station has old sealed sections no one can fully explain. A relic site contains technology nobody can reproduce. Historical records do not line up cleanly. A crisis that should escalate somehow stalls out. A research archive is incomplete in suspiciously specific ways. A system behaves as though someone once tried very hard to remove all trace of what happened there.

These things matter because they imply a hidden order without needing to summarize it.

## Use History as Damage, Not Just Information

If you want to stay close to the true lore, then the past should appear less as exposition and more as structural damage.

The machine age should have left:

- missing archives
- erased infrastructure histories
- broken chains of ownership
- sealed facilities
- contradictory records
- unexplained technological discontinuities

This fits the public wiki material very well. The setting already presents the AI era and the Departure as events that damaged records, obscured chronology, and left behind relics, strange remains, and unresolved debates rather than a cleanly preserved historical narrative.

A good rule is simple: players should often encounter **evidence of absence**. They find what is missing, what was stripped, what was buried, what no longer fits, and what clearly mattered once but was never meant to remain legible.

## Let the World Feel Stabilized, but Not Safe

If you are running close to the deeper truth, the Solar System should often feel as though it is being held in a tense equilibrium.

Large wars are rare, but tensions are everywhere. Political blocks compete, corporations pressure each other, stations sit under strain, labor conflicts threaten stability, and local violence exists — yet the whole system repeatedly avoids collapse into total war. That tension between instability and non-collapse fits both the broader faction structure in the wiki and the already public “Quiet Pattern” mystery listed under minor spoilers.

Do not over-explain that pattern. Just let it recur often enough that attentive players begin to notice that the setting does not behave like a world sliding naturally toward grand final wars.

## Relics Should Feel Incomplete

Running close to the true lore also changes how you present [[AI Relics]].

Relics should rarely feel like lost superweapons waiting to be switched back on. They should feel partial, damaged, stripped, mute, fragmented, or useful in very narrow ways. Their importance lies in what they imply, what they once connected to, what they are missing, or what advantage can still be extracted from them. That matches the open lore, which already frames relics mainly as incomplete remains, damaged systems, advanced materials, sealed caches, and partially functional fragments rather than intact machine minds.

This keeps relic stories strong and prevents the deeper lore from collapsing into simple treasure-hunt logic.

## Use Contradiction Carefully

A world close to the true lore should contain contradiction, but not random contradiction.

Different institutions should tell different stories. Local archives should disagree with official accounts. Academic interpretations should compete. Corporate records should be incomplete in self-serving ways. Spacer folklore should sometimes be wrong, but not always useless. This works especially well because the wiki already includes separate public-history pages and a rumor/secrets layer, implying that uncertainty is part of the setting’s normal texture.

The key is consistency underneath. Contradictions should point toward loss, concealment, fragmentation, or partial knowledge — not toward authorial chaos.

## Most People Should Stay Local

Even in lore-heavy campaigns, most NPCs should still care more about survival, employment, access, safety, contracts, cargo, station politics, and local risk than about hidden machine-age truth.

That matters because _SOL 2349_ remains a human-centered setting at the table. The deeper lore is powerful precisely because it exists beneath everyday life rather than replacing it. A dockmaster worrying about a reactor maintenance backlog is usually more useful than a lore-dumping historian. The historian becomes interesting when the backlog, the sealed archive, and the strange shipment begin to connect.

## Reveal in Layers

When players do move toward deeper truths, let revelation happen in layers.

A good progression is often:

1. something is wrong
2. the wrongness has a pattern
3. the pattern has history
4. the history was damaged on purpose
5. the surviving explanation is incomplete

That rhythm tends to preserve mystery while still rewarding investigation. It also fits the structure already visible in the wiki, where the open setting offers public history first, then anomalies, then GM-only material beyond that.

## What Running Close to the True Lore Usually Means

In practical terms, it usually means:

- history feels damaged
- relics feel incomplete
- conflict stays local even when larger pressures exist
- institutions behave as if they are carrying old trauma
- mysteries deepen rather than resolve too quickly

## What to Avoid

It usually weakens the setting if:

- the true lore is explained too early
- relics become easy plot devices
- every campaign turns into a direct machine-age conspiracy
- NPCs speak as if they secretly know the hidden truth
- the deeper lore overrides ordinary human motives

The hidden structure should sharpen the world, not replace it.

## In One Sentence

Running close to the true lore means letting the hidden history of _SOL 2349_ shape the world’s patterns, absences, and pressures without turning every story into direct exposition about what really happened.
