_SOL 2349_ works best when stories remain close to specific people, places, and pressures.

The setting is not primarily built around saving the entire Solar System. Its natural scale is lower: crews, stations, contracts, breakdowns, investigations, salvage jobs, local disputes, and decisions whose consequences are immediate enough to matter. That does not make the stories smaller in an emotional sense. It makes them clearer, sharper, and easier to feel.

## Start Small, Stay Concrete

A strong _SOL 2349_ scenario usually begins with a contained problem. A station is under pressure. A shipment goes missing. A job has hidden conditions. A habitat section is failing. Someone vanishes, lies, sabotages, steals, or asks for help too late.

These stories work because the stakes are concrete. The players know who is affected, what may be lost, and why the situation matters now. A story does not need to threaten all of human space to feel important. In this setting, it is often stronger when it does not.

## The Default Scale Is Local

Most stories in _SOL 2349_ should focus on a limited area of impact.

That might mean:

- a ship and its crew
- a station or habitat section
- a contract zone
- a local political or labor conflict

The wider Solar System is always present in the background, but usually as pressure rather than as the direct stage of play. Large systems, distant institutions, and historical forces matter most when they shape the local situation the characters are actually dealing with.

## Existing One-Shots Show the Right Scale

The current one-shot material already points toward the intended default.

[[Ghost Station]] works because it is built around a contained environment, immediate danger, secrecy, salvage, and competing interests. [[Cloudfall]] likewise suggests a scenario rooted in a specific place, a specific situation, and a specific problem under pressure. Both are good examples of how _SOL 2349_ is usually strongest: not at the scale of grand history, but at the scale where players can still see the people and systems their choices affect.

## Campaigns Can Expand — But Should Earn It

Longer campaigns can absolutely grow beyond the default scale.

A player group may eventually influence a trade corridor, destabilize or protect a regional network, expose a major secret, reshape the fate of a station cluster, or brush against the deeper hidden history of the setting. In rare cases, a campaign may significantly alter the players’ corner of the Solar System.

But that should usually emerge over time. It works best when a campaign begins with concrete stakes and grows outward through play, rather than starting with the assumption that the characters are meant to reshape civilization from the beginning.

Large-scale impact should feel possible. It should not feel automatic.

## Change the Players’ World First

Before the players change the wider setting, they should change their own immediate world.

That usually means their actions affect:

- their crew
- their reputation
- their employers and enemies
- their home port, station, or route
- their future access to safety, work, and trust

This is often where campaign consequences become most meaningful. Not in abstract headlines, but in changed relationships, altered routines, new risks, lost opportunities, and places that now feel different because of what the players did.

## Small Stories Can Still Reveal Big Truths

Keeping the scale local does not mean cutting the setting off from larger themes.

A labor dispute can expose deeper economic dependence. A salvage mission can uncover fragments of machine-age history. A habitat accident can reveal political neglect. A courier job can touch factional pressure far beyond the players’ full understanding.

This is usually the strongest way to handle larger structures in _SOL 2349_: let them appear through local consequences, not through constant epic framing.

## Questions for the GM

When planning a scenario, it helps to ask:

- What is the immediate problem?
- Who is directly affected?
- What concrete place is under pressure?
- What changes if the players succeed or fail?

These questions usually lead to stories that fit the setting better than starting from abstract large-scale stakes.

## In One Sentence

The default scale of _SOL 2349_ is not the fate of the entire Solar System, but the fate of specific people, places, crews, stations, and communities within it.
