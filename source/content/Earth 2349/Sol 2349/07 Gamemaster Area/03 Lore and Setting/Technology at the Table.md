Technology in _SOL 2349_ should feel useful, powerful, and advanced — but never effortless.

At the table, technology is not there to erase problems. It is there to shape them. Good technology in _SOL 2349_ creates options, but also limits, dependencies, risks, and costs. It can extend human capability, improve survival, and make extraordinary things possible, but it should still feel embedded in systems of power, maintenance, logistics, training, and material reality.

That is one of the key differences between _SOL 2349_ and softer science-fiction settings: technology does not float above the world. It is part of the world.

## Technology Should Solve One Problem and Create Another

A useful rule for the GM is simple: when technology helps, it should usually complicate something else.

A better suit improves survival, but increases maintenance burden. A stronger implant expands capability, but adds load, strain, or dependency. A high-performance ship can make difficult routes possible, but demands fuel, cooling, repairs, and trained crew. A secure system protects information, but slows access in a crisis. A relic fragment may provide an advantage, but create legal, political, or technical danger.

This does not make technology weaker. It makes it part of play.

## Keep It Material

Technology in _SOL 2349_ should feel physical.

Ships have mass, thrust limits, heat signatures, and vulnerable systems. Habitats need atmosphere, power, pressure integrity, repair access, and waste management. Weapons, tools, implants, drones, and vehicles all exist inside material constraints. Even very advanced systems should feel built, serviced, repaired, patched, upgraded, and sometimes misused.

If technology starts feeling weightless, invisible, or consequence-free, the tone usually weakens.

## Human Skill Still Matters

Technology does not remove the importance of people.

Even advanced systems need operators, technicians, medics, navigators, engineers, handlers, security staff, pilots, analysts, and people willing to make decisions under pressure. A good GM should let technology matter without turning it into an automatic replacement for competence.

A ship is only as good as the people running it.  
A station only works because someone maintains it.  
A powerful device in the wrong hands may be useless, dangerous, or both.

This is especially important in _SOL 2349_, where human judgment remains central to the setting.

## Avoid Miracle Devices

Technology should not become an all-purpose plot solvent.

If a device can bypass every obstacle, solve every investigation, neutralize every danger, and remove every cost, then it usually does not belong in the foreground of play. Even powerful tools should be narrow, conditional, or difficult to use well. They may be expensive, unstable, politically restricted, hazardous, slow, incomplete, or dependent on supporting infrastructure.

This is especially true for advanced or unusual equipment. The more impressive something is, the more it should feel like it comes with strings attached.

## Keep Relics Narrow and Strange

When using older or unusual technology, especially [[AI Relic]], avoid treating it like a clean upgrade.

Relic technology should usually feel incomplete, difficult to interpret, narrow in purpose, and potentially dangerous to study or deploy. It may do something impressive, but rarely in a convenient or fully understood way. That keeps the tone intact and prevents relics from becoming generic treasure items.

Their value often lies as much in implication, leverage, or scientific insight as in direct field use.

## Let Infrastructure Matter

One of the best ways to make technology feel like _SOL 2349_ is to remember that most important technology exists inside infrastructure.

A ship depends on docks, fuel, spares, maintenance, certifications, crews, navigation support, and supply chains. A habitat depends on recycling, heat control, pressure management, logistics, software, inspection, and labor. A weapon depends on ammunition, authority, context, and consequences. An implant depends on surgery, upkeep, compatibility, and the user’s own body.

This keeps technology connected to the setting’s larger systems instead of turning it into isolated gadgets.

## Technology Should Change the Shape of a Scene

At the table, technology works best when it changes _how_ a problem is approached, not when it removes the problem entirely.

A drone changes how players scout.  
A suit changes how they survive.  
A ship sensor package changes how they detect risk.  
A medical system changes what happens after injury.  
A data tool changes what kind of evidence can be extracted.

The problem remains. The method changes.

That is usually much more interesting than simple technological override.

## Use Limits to Make Technology Feel Real

Technology often feels more believable when its limits are visible.

Batteries run down. Systems overheat. Interfaces fail. Spare parts are wrong. Cheap components break first. Military-grade gear is hard to acquire. Civilian systems are safer but weaker. Corporate tools are locked behind permissions. Station infrastructure is old. Frontier infrastructure is improvised. High-end systems exist, but not everywhere.

You do not need to model every detail mechanically. You only need the players to feel that technology lives inside a real world.

## A Good GM Question

When introducing a piece of technology, ask:

- What is it good at?
- What does it depend on?
- What does it make harder?
- Why is it not used everywhere?

Those questions are often enough to keep the answer grounded.

## In One Sentence

Technology in _SOL 2349_ should expand what people can do without ever making the world frictionless, effortless, or free of consequences.
