**SOL 2349** was created with **massive use of AI tools**.

That is not something I want to hide, downplay, or dress up in vague language. AI was used throughout the project in a very extensive way, and without it, this setting would simply not exist in its current size, structure, and level of detail — at least not within the time I had available.

At the same time, I also want to be very clear about what AI **did not** do.

The actual core of the project — the worldbuilding direction, the major plot ideas, the technological assumptions, the political structure, the rules concepts, the tone, the setting logic, and the creative decisions behind all of that — comes from the author.

AI was primarily used as an amplifier, editor, consistency checker, research assistant, naming helper, and production tool.

## What AI was used for

AI was used heavily in the following areas:

- generating **all images** used for the project
- editing and refining **final text versions**
- checking text for **consistency, clarity, and internal contradictions**
- improving structure, readability, and flow
- helping with **crosslinking** between wiki pages
- helping generate or refine some **names**, including some faction names
- assisting with technical questions, rough calculations, and plausibility checks
- speeding up iteration on ideas that would otherwise have taken much longer to develop and document

To put it very clearly:

**All images in the project are 100% AI-generated.**  
The final versions of most texts were also heavily revised with AI support.

The main AI used for this project is **ChatGPT (GPT-5.4 Thinking)**.

## What still comes from the author

Even with all that AI support, the actual creative foundation of **SOL 2349** is still mine.

That includes, among other things:

- the overall concept of the setting
- the major worldbuilding decisions
- the factions, conflicts, and themes
- the technological direction of the setting
- the rules ideas and gameplay philosophy
- the plot hooks, story potential, and larger lore structure
- the decision of what fits the setting and what does not

AI helped me **develop, test, structure, and express** those ideas much faster.  
It did not replace the underlying creative intent.

A useful way to put it would be this:

**AI helped me build and polish the project. It did not decide what the project is.**

## Why I used AI so heavily

The simple answer is: **time**.

I do not have the free time to build a world like this completely by hand at this speed. Without AI, I could still have developed many of the same ideas, but not at this scale, not with this level of written-out detail, and definitely not in such a short time.

AI made it possible to turn notes, concepts, half-formed ideas, technical questions, and worldbuilding fragments into an actual structured setting with readable pages, internal links, and a much higher level of consistency than I could realistically manage on my own in my spare time.

So for this project, AI is not a gimmick. It is a core production tool.

## Typical workflow

A lot of pages in this wiki were created through a process that looked roughly like this:

### 1. Initial idea

It usually starts with an idea, a question, or a problem in the setting. Sometimes that idea sits in my head for days or weeks before I write anything down.

### 2. Background inspiration and research

Then I usually do some mix of background research and inspiration gathering. That can include reading, listening to audiobooks, playing games, revisiting other science-fiction settings, or looking into real scientific and technical topics.

Sometimes the inspiration comes first.  
Sometimes I first think about a setting problem and only later find a work that helps me think it through.

### 3. Asking targeted questions

After that, I often use AI to ask very specific questions that help me understand the scale of something better.

For example:

> How much power is needed to accelerate a 20,000 ton spaceship to 1% of light speed?

This is exactly the kind of thing I _could_ calculate myself, but usually do not want to spend my limited spare time doing in full detail. AI is extremely useful here as a fast technical thinking aid.

### 4. Writing the rough concept

Then I usually feed the AI a rough concept in my own words.

For example:

> I want to describe how fusion power and drive systems work in SOL 2349. Fusion is common, fusion torch ships are common, but the reactors are not cheap to build or operate, and other drive systems are still relevant.

That kind of rough input is often the real starting point of a page.

### 5. Iteration

From there, the text usually goes through several rounds of iteration.

That can include things like:

- expand this idea
- make this more realistic
- remove that part
- this is too soft-SF, make it harder
- this sounds too much like another franchise
- shorten this
- make the tone cleaner
- make this easier to read
- connect this to other pages
- check whether this still fits the rest of the setting

### 6. Review passes

After that, I often do one or more focused review passes, for example:

**Scientific / plausibility review**  
Does this still fit current scientific understanding?  
Is this at least compatible with real physics, engineering logic, or realistic extrapolation?

**Editorial review**  
Remove redundancies.  
Shorten the text.  
Keep the information, but make the language simpler and easier to read.

**Consistency / crosslink review**  
Does this match the rest of the setting?  
Does it contradict another page?  
What pages should link to this one, and which existing pages should be referenced here?

### 7. Final author decision

At the end, I decide what stays and what goes.

That part matters a lot. AI can generate options, rewrites, alternatives, and checks — but the final version still depends on my judgment of whether something actually belongs in **SOL 2349**.

## Strengths and limits of AI use

AI is incredibly useful for this project, but it is not magic.

Its strengths are obvious:

- speed
- iteration
- restructuring
- wording help
- consistency checking
- brainstorming support
- technical back-and-forth
- helping turn vague concepts into usable text

But it also has limits:

- it can make things sound more convincing than they really are
- it can introduce hidden contradictions
- it can drift toward generic genre patterns
- it can over-explain things
- it can flatten tone if not corrected
- and it absolutely needs human direction, correction, and filtering

So while AI is deeply embedded in the production of this project, it still works best when treated as a tool rather than an authority.

## Final note

I do not see AI use as something that makes the project “less real.”  
For me, it is closer to using a very powerful combination of editor, idea sparring partner, writing assistant, and production accelerator.

Without AI, **SOL 2349** would be smaller, less polished, less interconnected, and much slower to grow.

With AI, I can actually build the kind of setting I want to build.
