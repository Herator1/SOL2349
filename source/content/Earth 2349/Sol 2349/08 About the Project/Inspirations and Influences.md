**SOL 2349** did not come out of nowhere. Like most science-fiction settings, it grew out of a mix of books, games, films, and other ideas that stuck with me over time.

This page is **not a ranking of quality**. Some of the works listed under **Avoid** are franchises I really like. The categories simply show how well something fits the kind of setting I want **SOL 2349** to be.

I roughly sort influences into three groups:

- **Major Influences**  
    These had a big impact on the core identity of the setting.
- **Other Influences**  
    These contributed smaller ideas, specific themes, bits of tone, or useful concepts.
- **Avoid / Negative Reference**  
    These are works that helped define **SOL 2349** by showing me what I _do not_ want the setting to become.

In general, **SOL 2349** aims for a more grounded and system-driven kind of science fiction. I am usually more interested in logistics, politics, material limits, and messy human systems than in miracle technology, clear good-and-evil setups, or overly simple answers to complex problems.

This list is also **very much a work in progress**. I will almost certainly remember additional influences later, especially smaller ones, half-forgotten ones, or things that mainly shaped the deeper lore, future plans, or the rules side of the project. So this page should be read as an evolving overview, not as a final or complete catalog.

## Major Influences

These are the works that had the strongest influence on the overall shape of **SOL 2349**.

### **The Expanse**

**Authors:** James S. A. Corey  
**Adaptation:** TV adaptation by Alcon Television / Syfy / Amazon

This is probably the most obvious major influence on **SOL 2349**. Its fragmented Solar System, political tensions between space-based populations, and more grounded treatment of space travel helped shape the overall direction of the setting. At the same time, **SOL 2349** deliberately moves away from the alien mystery plot, the protomolecule escalation, and especially the ring-gate or portal side of things.

### **Delta-v** and sequels

**Author:** Daniel Suarez

These books strongly influenced the industrial and economic side of **SOL 2349**, especially asteroid mining, space resource extraction, and the idea that large-scale expansion into space is driven just as much by profit and logistics as by exploration. They also reinforced the feeling that space development is risky, expensive, and shaped by powerful interests.

### **Bobiverse**

**Author:** Dennis E. Taylor

This series influenced the way **SOL 2349** thinks about space combat, long-range problem-solving, physical constraints, and large-scale systems thinking. It is not a tonal blueprint for the setting, but it helped shape some of the more technical and strategic ways in which space can be approached.

### **The Martian**

**Author:** Andy Weir  
**Adaptation:** Film adaptation by 20th Century Fox / Scott Free Productions

This was a major influence on the survivalist and engineering-driven side of **SOL 2349**, especially in relation to Mars. It reinforced the idea that hostile environments are not overcome by miracle technology, but by improvisation, technical skill, and constant struggle against material limits.

### **Terra Invicta**

**Studio:** Pavonis Interactive

This game strongly influenced the overall feel of strategic expansion and conflict within the Solar System. Its treatment of fleet movement, orbital warfare, political escalation, and large-scale infrastructure resonated strongly with the design direction of **SOL 2349**. Even where the details differ, it helped shape the sense that space is a strategic environment defined by distance, commitment, and logistics.

### **Kerbal Space Program**

**Studio:** Squad

This was a major influence on the intuitive physics culture behind the setting. More than almost anything else, it helped reinforce the importance of orbital mechanics, transfer windows, delta-v logic, and the fact that space travel is governed by physics rather than cinematic convenience.

### **Remembrance of Earth’s Past**

**Author:** Liu Cixin

This trilogy influenced the larger civilizational scale of thinking behind **SOL 2349**. Its impact is less about direct copying and more about seriousness of scope, long-term perspective, and the sense that humanity’s place in the universe may be shaped by forces far beyond immediate everyday life. That said, **SOL 2349** does **not** adopt the **Dark Forest** idea as one of its core assumptions.

### **Deus Ex**

**Developers:** Ion Storm / Eidos Montréal  
**Publishers:** Eidos Interactive / Square Enix Europe

Taken together, the **Deus Ex** games are a major influence on the setting’s treatment of transhumanism, augmentation, corporate power, and technologically mediated inequality. They helped shape the idea that implants are not just tools, but social and political fault lines that affect access, status, and even the meaning of being human.

### **Cyberpunk 2077**

**Developer:** CD Projekt Red

This is another major influence on the augmentation and corporate side of the setting, even though **SOL 2349** is not a cyberpunk setting in the classic sense. Its strongest contribution lies in the depiction of implants as normalized but unevenly distributed power, and in the idea that bodies, identities, and opportunities can become entangled with corporate systems and technological modification.

### **Dungeons & Dragons**

**Publisher:** Wizards of the Coast

This is a major influence on the **rules side** of the project rather than the setting itself. It helped shape the idea that the game should remain playable, structured, and accessible even when the setting is dense and technical.

### **The Dark Eye / Das Schwarze Auge**

**Publisher:** Ulisses Spiele  
**Earlier editions:** Schmidt Spiele / FanPro

This is also a major **rules influence** rather than a setting influence. Its strongest impact lies in character progression, restrained power growth, and the idea that player characters should not feel like superheroes by default. That fits **SOL 2349** very well, where competence matters, but advancement should still feel grounded.

## Other Influences

These works influenced specific aspects of **SOL 2349**, but not as strongly or as broadly as the major inspirations. In some cases, only certain ideas, tones, or structural elements were important, while other aspects were deliberately left aside.

### **Project Hail Mary**

**Author:** Andy Weir

This influenced some of the broader conceptual side of **SOL 2349**, especially science as a practical tool for survival and problem-solving. At the same time, parts of it go too far into speculative imagination to serve as a direct model. So the influence is limited to selected concepts rather than the overall tone.

### **The Swarm**

**Author:** Frank Schätzing

This work influenced the way **SOL 2349** thinks about ecological complexity, interconnected systems, and the possibility that humanity only partly understands the larger structures it is embedded in. Its value lies less in direct setting material and more in its sense of scale and systemic entanglement.

### **Am Abgrund der Ewigkeit**

**Author:** Philip P. Peterson

Only the earlier part of this work had a meaningful influence on **SOL 2349**. What mattered here was the initial sense of scale, exploration, and speculative possibility, while later parts moved too far away from the harder science-fiction direction this project aims for.

### **Surviving Mars**

**Developer:** Haemimont Games  
**Publisher:** Paradox Interactive

This game influenced parts of the settlement-building and early colonization imagination behind **SOL 2349**. Its strongest contribution lies in the practical feeling of building infrastructure in a hostile environment and thinking in terms of survival systems, resource chains, and expansion under pressure.

### **Terraforming Mars**

**Designer:** Jacob Fryxelius  
**Publisher:** FryxGames

This is a useful influence in terms of long-term planetary development, environmental transformation, and the idea that large-scale human activity can reshape entire worlds over time. It helped reinforce the notion that settlement and development are gradual, material, and infrastructural processes.

### **Schleichfahrt / Aquanox series**

**Developers:** Massive Development and related studios

These games influenced the feeling of enclosed technical settlements in hostile environments. Their underwater cities, industrial isolation, and pressure-bound infrastructures translate surprisingly well into thinking about space stations, sealed habitats, and life in dangerous environments where engineering is the precondition for survival.

### **Demolition Man**

**Studio:** Warner Bros.

This is a small but useful influence in terms of alternate social futures and the idea that future societies may evolve in ways that feel functional, strange, and culturally disorienting at the same time.

### **Judge Dredd**

**Creators:** John Wagner and Carlos Ezquerra

This is a limited but relevant influence on the portrayal of extreme urban concentration, institutional harshness, and megacity logic. It helped inform the sense that some future population centers may feel oppressive, overbuilt, and politically brutal in ways very different from present-day cities.

### **Half-Life**

**Developer:** Valve

This series contributed to the idea of hidden research complexes, secret infrastructure, and institutions operating behind sealed doors. Its influence can be felt in the way **SOL 2349** imagines restricted facilities, black-site science, and systems whose official purpose only partly reflects what is really happening inside them.

### **Subnautica**

**Developer:** Unknown Worlds Entertainment

This is an influence in terms of environmental hostility, exploration under constraint, and the feeling of being surrounded by an ecosystem that is beautiful, dangerous, and only partly understood. It also reinforces the logic of fragile survival within sealed technical systems.

### **S.T.A.L.K.E.R. series**

**Developer:** GSC Game World

This series influenced the tone of exclusion zones, unstable frontier spaces, and regions where normal rules have partly broken down. Its importance for **SOL 2349** lies less in supernatural anomaly logic and more in atmosphere: dangerous margins, abandoned infrastructure, unofficial economies, and the uneasy mix of fear, opportunity, and exploitation.

### **Metro series**

**Author:** Dmitry Glukhovsky  
**Developers:** 4A Games

The **Metro** series influenced the tone of confined survival, compromised societies, and worlds shaped by disaster and long-term pressure. For **SOL 2349**, that influence matters most in how isolated populations adapt psychologically, socially, and politically to environments defined by danger and constraint.

### **Avatar**

**Director:** James Cameron

This is only a limited influence, and specifically not in its spiritual or “space indigenous” side. What mattered instead was the theme of corporate extraction, asymmetrical exploitation, and the way powerful institutions interact with valuable and contested environments.

### **Fallout**

**Creators / Rights:** Interplay / Bethesda Softworks

This series contributed to the imagination of exclusion zones, civilizational scars, and landscapes still marked by the memory of large-scale war. **SOL 2349** does not adopt its retrofuturistic style, but some of its influence can be felt in the treatment of damaged regions, old infrastructures, and places where past catastrophe still shapes present life.

### **BioShock**

**Developer:** Irrational Games  
**Publisher:** 2K Games

This is a useful influence in terms of secret research, isolated experimental environments, and technologically ambitious systems cut off from the wider world. Its underwater setting also overlaps conceptually with the enclosed habitat logic that matters elsewhere in **SOL 2349**.

### **XCOM**

**Developers:** Firaxis Games and earlier creators in the franchise

This is only a partial influence, and explicitly not because of its alien invasion plot. What matters instead is the structure of covert programs, layered threats, strategic response systems, and the feeling that institutions may be forced to manage dangers that remain mostly invisible to the wider public.

### **The Witcher games**

**Developer:** CD Projekt Red

These games are not science fiction, but they influenced the tonal design of **SOL 2349** in a meaningful way. In particular, they reinforced the idea that conflicts should often resist simple moral categorization, and that many situations are defined less by clear good and evil than by competing interests, compromised actors, and difficult trade-offs.

### **Jurassic Park**

**Author:** Michael Crichton  
**Adaptations:** Universal Pictures

This belongs less to the visible surface of **SOL 2349** than to its deeper conceptual background. Its influence is not about dinosaurs, but about scientific ambition, containment failure, complex systems escaping control, and the gap between technical capability and actual mastery.

### **Stellaris**

**Developer:** Paradox Development Studio  
**Publisher:** Paradox Interactive

This is not a core influence on the 2349-era setting, but it becomes more relevant when thinking about deeper future developments and larger civilizational possibilities. Its contribution lies in broadening the imagination toward long-term trajectories, unusual political forms, and future layers of human expansion beyond the immediate scope of the main setting.

### **Cthulhu Mythos**

**Associated with:** H. P. Lovecraft and later authors

This is relevant mainly as a deep-lore influence. What matters here is not horror in the usual monster sense, but the idea of incomprehensible cosmic structures, entities, or truths that may exceed human frames of understanding.

### **Adrian Tchaikovsky**

**Relevant works:** _Alien Clay_, _Children of Time_, and others

Tchaikovsky’s work matters mainly in relation to nonhuman intelligence, evolutionary difference, and the difficulty of understanding minds, systems, or ecologies unlike our own. This influence is not central to the everyday feel of **SOL 2349**, but it matters in the deeper background.

### **Vacuum**

**Author:** Philip P. Peterson

This work contributed to the broader speculative horizon of the setting, particularly where mystery, cosmic scale, and scientific uncertainty meet. Its influence is not foundational, but it helped open certain directions of thought relevant to deep background material.

### **Other works by Dennis E. Taylor**

**Author:** Dennis E. Taylor  
**Relevant works:** including _The Singularity Trap_ and others

Outside the **Bobiverse**, Taylor’s work still influenced some of the wider speculative thinking around technology, discovery, and future trajectories. These influences are not as central as the main series, but they helped reinforce interest in large conceptual shifts, hidden dangers, and unusual long-term developments.

### **Quantum series / trilogy**

**Author:** Douglas Phillips

This is another influence that matters more in the background than in the everyday foreground of the setting. Its contribution lies in speculative escalation, unusual technologies, and the sense that reality may contain layers not immediately visible within ordinary political or material life.

### **A Fire Upon the Deep**

**Author:** Vernor Vinge

This work is especially relevant as a future-oriented and deep-lore influence. Its importance lies in scale, civilizational layering, and the idea that intelligence, development, and power may take radically different forms at different levels of reality or history.

### **Warframe**

**Developer:** Digital Extremes

This is not a direct tonal or structural model for **SOL 2349**, but it contributes to the setting’s more distant imaginative horizon. Its influence is most relevant when thinking about strange far-future possibilities, fragmented legacies, and societies shaped by the remains of older technological ages.

## Avoid / Negative Reference

This is probably the easiest section to misunderstand, so one thing up front: **Avoid** does not mean “bad.” In some cases it means the opposite — these are great franchises, sometimes even personal favorites. They are just not the direction I want for **SOL 2349**.

I do not want to avoid horror, mystery, or dark material in general. I just do not want the **simple version** of them. “Thing from space is evil” is usually too flat for what I want. The same goes for “AI turns evil and wants to kill everyone,” “aliens are either clearly good or clearly bad,” or super-clean miracle tech that can do basically whatever the plot needs.

I want the setting to stay messier than that. Dark things should come from uncertainty, bad incentives, hidden agendas, systems getting out of control, and people pushing into areas they do not fully understand — not just from obvious monsters or clear-cut villains.

### **Star Wars**

**Creator:** George Lucas  
**Rights / Studio:** Lucasfilm / Disney

A personal favorite, especially Episodes I–VI, but still much too close to fantasy in space for the goals of **SOL 2349**. Its mythic structure, spiritual powers, soft treatment of physics, and cinematic logic are all pretty far removed from the harder and more industrial approach of this setting.

### **Star Trek**

**Creator:** Gene Roddenberry  
**Rights / Studio:** Paramount

This stands for exactly the kind of clean, polished, miracle-tech future that **SOL 2349** does not want to emulate. Its highly flexible technology, human-looking alien species, and general smoothness lead in a very different direction from the harsher, more materially limited, infrastructure-heavy future imagined here.

### **Warhammer 40,000**

**Rights / Studio:** Games Workshop

Despite my affection for the setting — including a significant Death Guard army — **Warhammer 40,000** is basically everything **SOL 2349** is not. It is mythic, excessive, gothic, and proudly unconcerned with realism, while this project aims for plausibility, restraint, and systemic coherence.

### **Terminator**

**Creator:** James Cameron and Gale Anne Hurd

This franchise represents the classic machine-rebellion model that does not fit **SOL 2349**. In this setting, AI is not built around a simple “machines rise up and decide to kill humanity” premise. I am much more interested in containment, limits, fear of uncontrolled systems, and institutional reactions to AI than in open extermination war.

### **The Matrix**

**Creators:** The Wachowskis

Like **Terminator**, this is built around a very clear and direct AI-versus-humanity conflict that does not match the logic of **SOL 2349**. The setting avoids sharply moralized “evil machine overlord” structures and is more interested in blurred boundaries, constrained systems, and technological dependence without straightforward villainy.

### **Alien**

**Creator:** Dan O’Bannon and Ronald Shusett

A classic and highly effective franchise, but not a model for this setting. **SOL 2349** does not want aliens to function mainly as monsters, horror engines, or pure existential predators. “Alien = bad and scary” is exactly the kind of simplified pattern I want to avoid.

### **The Thing**

**Source:** John W. Campbell Jr. / adaptation by John Carpenter

This is a strong negative reference in tonal terms. Paranoia and hidden danger absolutely can exist in **SOL 2349**, but the setting is not built around body horror, monstrous imitation, or “the unknown is evil by default.” It is useful mainly as a reminder of what kind of horror logic not to drift into too easily.

### **Classic space horror in general**

This includes settings where space mainly functions as a backdrop for monsters, infection, and survival horror. **SOL 2349** can absolutely be dark, strange, and unsettling, but I do not want it to fall back on the easy version where the unknown is just there to be a monster.

### **Event Horizon**

**Director:** Paul W. S. Anderson

Interesting concept, very memorable atmosphere, but completely outside the intended tone of **SOL 2349**. Its supernatural and psychological horror side is exactly the kind of direction I do not want the setting to take as a core identity.

### **E.T.**

**Director:** Steven Spielberg

An obvious non-model for the project. Friendly, emotionally legible alien contact stories do not fit the grounded, human-centered, and deeply ambiguous way **SOL 2349** approaches nonhuman intelligence and the unknown.

### **Mad Max**

**Creator:** George Miller

Although scarcity, violence, and collapse exist in parts of **SOL 2349**, it is not a post-apocalyptic wasteland setting and does not use that visual or social logic as a core model. Its future is damaged and unequal, but still deeply industrial, infrastructural, and system-bound.

### **Dune**

**Author:** Frank Herbert

This is obviously one of the giants of science fiction, but not a model for **SOL 2349**. Its feudal structure, mysticism, prophecy-heavy framing, and stylized civilizational logic lead in a very different direction from the more materialist, technological, and hard-science approach I want here.

### **Mass Effect**

**Developer:** BioWare

This is a very useful anti-model because it represents several things **SOL 2349** deliberately avoids. Its strongly legible alien factions, clearer moral framing, space-opera heroics, and smoother high-tech assumptions push toward a type of galaxy-scale adventure setting that is fundamentally different from what I want to build.

### **Dead Space**

**Developer:** EA Redwood Shores / Visceral Games

This is another clear negative reference because it turns space into a stage for grotesque body horror, madness, and monstrous infection. None of that fits the intended tone of **SOL 2349** as a core approach.

### **Halo series**

**Developers:** Bungie / 343 Industries / Halo Studios

This franchise is too strongly built around super-soldier heroics, stylized military spectacle, and clean factional conflict to work as a meaningful model for **SOL 2349**. It also leans toward exactly the kind of larger-than-life protagonist logic I want to avoid.

### **Wing Commander series**

**Creator:** Chris Roberts

This stands for a style of space combat that **SOL 2349** explicitly rejects: naval aviation and World War II-style dogfights translated straight into space. That cinematic approach may be fun, but it is not the kind of physically grounded warfare this project wants.

### **Any “World War II dogfights in space” style setting**

More broadly, **SOL 2349** avoids settings where spacecraft behave like atmospheric fighter planes just because it looks cool. Space combat here is meant to come out of physics, heat, detection, distance, missiles, and weapon logic — not from reskinned air combat.

### **MechWarrior**

**Rights / Studio:** BattleTech and related rights holders

Giant humanoid war machines are far outside the design language of **SOL 2349**. They belong to a different kind of science fiction, one driven by stylization, spectacle, and franchise identity rather than plausible military or engineering logic.

### **Starship Troopers**

**Author:** Robert A. Heinlein  
**Adaptations / Rights:** various

Whether one looks at the novel, the film, or the broader franchise legacy, this is not the kind of military science fiction **SOL 2349** is trying to emulate. It leans too strongly toward clearly defined external enemies, militarized identity, and simplified us-versus-them conflict structures.
