Let’s address the obvious comparison.

Yes, **SOL 2349** and **The Expanse** have things in common. A lot of things, actually.

Both are harder science-fiction settings.  
Both are set in our Solar System.  
Both care about infrastructure, space travel, political tension, and the fact that living beyond Earth changes societies in major ways.  
Both take Mars, the Belt, stations, and interplanetary logistics seriously.

So yes — if someone looks at **SOL 2349** and immediately thinks of **The Expanse**, that is completely fair.

It would actually be stranger if they did not.

But similarity on that level does **not** mean the two settings are the same thing.

## The short version

If I had to put it simply:

**The Expanse** is one of the best and most influential hard-science-fiction franchises set in the Solar System, so of course it overlaps with what I am doing.

But **SOL 2349** is built with different priorities, different assumptions, a different visual identity, a different historical logic, and a different long-term direction.

It is not “The Expanse with different names.”  
It is a different setting that happens to share some of the same broad territory.

## Similar surface, different foundation

A lot of the overlap is structural.

If you want to make a believable Solar System setting, you will probably end up with at least some of the following:

- Earth still matters
- Mars matters
- the Belt matters
- space travel is difficult and expensive
- infrastructure matters more than style
- distance and logistics shape politics
- stations, trade routes, mining, and industrial power matter a lot

That is not copying. That is just what happens when you take the Solar System seriously as a setting.

The reason the overlap feels strong is mostly because **The Expanse** did that really well. It became the most visible modern benchmark for this kind of science fiction. So anything else in the same general space will naturally be compared to it.

That is fine.

## Technology: similar category, different details

This is probably the most obvious area of comparison.

Yes, both settings use relatively grounded space technology compared to softer space opera. But the actual technological feel is different.

**SOL 2349** is generally a bit more **industrial**, a bit rougher, and in some areas slightly **lower-tech**. It is not a polished future with one miracle propulsion breakthrough carrying the whole system on its back. There is no equivalent to the Epstein Drive solving everything in a neat package.

Fusion exists. Fusion torch ships exist. Advanced space infrastructure exists. But that does **not** mean every ship is cheap to run, easy to maintain, or magically flexible.

Reactors are expensive.  
Torch drives are expensive.  
Heat matters.  
Maintenance matters.  
Different drive systems still matter.  
Industrial support matters.

So while both settings live in the broad zone of “harder spaceflight,” **SOL 2349** leans more into the idea of a civilization that has built a huge, messy, maintenance-heavy industrial network across the Solar System.

Less sleek torch-age elegance.  
More welded trusses, radiator farms, propellant chains, orbital yards, ugly utility ships, and infrastructure everywhere.

## The world is politically more fragmented

One of the biggest differences is political structure.

In **The Expanse**, the world is shaped to a large degree by a relatively clean major-power triangle:  
**Earth, Mars, and the Belt**.

That works very well for the kind of story it wants to tell.

**SOL 2349** is much more fragmented.

There are major powers, yes, but not in that same clean three-sided way. The setting has more actors, more overlapping interests, more regional complexity, more corporations, more semi-independent systems, and more messy in-between layers. Power is distributed in a less elegant and often more chaotic way.

That changes everything.

It changes diplomacy.  
It changes alliances.  
It changes trade.  
It changes conflict.  
It changes how history unfolds.

My Mars is not the Mars of **The Expanse**.  
My Belt is not the Belt of **The Expanse**.  
My Earth is also different.

And Venus is full of cloud cities instead of turning into a weird alien gate project.

## History works differently

This is a big one.

Even if two settings share planets and similar kinds of technologies, they can still feel completely different if their **history** works differently.

**SOL 2349** is not built by taking the political map of **The Expanse** and tweaking it a bit. Its background history, power development, industrial expansion, and social evolution follow a different path.

The result is a setting where present-day conditions emerge from different pressures and different long-term developments. The factions are shaped differently. The infrastructure developed differently. The institutional logic is different. The social balance between planets, habitats, corporations, and political blocs is different.

So even where the geography overlaps, the **historical DNA** does not.

## The role of aliens is completely different

This may be the single most important difference in long-term setting identity.

**The Expanse** eventually becomes deeply shaped by its alien plotline: the protomolecule, the builders, the gates, and the massive shift from Solar System politics into something much bigger and stranger.

That is one of its defining features.

**SOL 2349** does not go that route.

This setting is not built around an alien artifact rewriting the human future from the outside. The unknown may exist. Strange things may exist. Deep lore may exist. But the setting does not pivot into “alien technology changes everything and opens the map.”

No ring gates.  
No portal network.  
No protomolecule-equivalent taking over the core structure of the world.

That alone pushes the entire setting in a very different direction.

Humanity remains much more trapped inside its own systems, its own Solar System, and its own consequences.

## Transhumanism matters much more in SOL 2349

This is another major difference.

In **SOL 2349**, implants, augmentation, human modification, and the social consequences of transhumanism are much more central to the setting. Not as shiny cyberpunk flavor, but as a real structural part of how power, labor, identity, inequality, and capability work.

That theme is present much more strongly here than in **The Expanse**.

The body itself becomes part of the setting’s politics and economics.

Who gets enhanced?  
Who can afford it?  
Who depends on it?  
Who regulates it?  
Who profits from it?  
What does it do to military force, industrial labor, and social class?

That is a very different emphasis.

## The scale of stories is different

**The Expanse** often operates through very large, system-shaping storylines. Even when it begins with smaller events, it tends to escalate toward massive political and civilizational consequences.

That is part of its strength.

**SOL 2349** is much more interested in the smaller layer.

Small crews.  
Local crises.  
Industrial disputes.  
Station politics.  
Salvage jobs.  
Security failures.  
Research incidents.  
Mining conflicts.  
Personal stakes inside larger systems.

The big setting absolutely exists, but the focus is often lower down and closer in. The goal is less “save the Solar System” and more “survive inside it,” “work within it,” “understand one part of it,” or “get through a situation that larger powers barely notice.”

That gives the world a different narrative feel.

## Infrastructure matters even more

Both settings care about infrastructure, but **SOL 2349** pushes that even harder.

This is a world of:

- orbital construction
- shipyards
- mining chains
- industrial stations
- transport bottlenecks
- fuel and propellant logistics
- cloud cities
- maintenance burdens
- ugly but essential support systems
- long-lived technical ecosystems

The setting is, in many places, less cinematic and more infrastructural.

It is meant to feel like a civilization that has really built itself into the Solar System, piece by piece, over a long time. Not cleanly. Not elegantly. But massively.

## Diplomacy is less neat

Another difference is that **SOL 2349** is generally less clean in diplomatic structure.

There are fewer tidy lines. More overlapping interests. More partial alignments. More situations where nobody is fully in control and nobody is fully innocent. Political action is often shaped by economics, logistics, corporate interests, local autonomy, and institutional inertia rather than by clearly readable bloc logic.

That does not make it more realistic in some objective sense. It just makes it different in tone and structure.

Messier.  
More crowded.  
Less triangular.  
Less narratively elegant on purpose.

## A different visual identity

This matters more than people sometimes think.

When I picture **SOL 2349**, I do not see a sleek version of **The Expanse**. I see something more industrial, more layered, more maintenance-heavy, and often less visually elegant.

More scaffolding.  
More tanks.  
More radiators.  
More patched infrastructure.  
More working hardware.  
More orbital clutter.  
More evidence that millions of people and thousands of organizations have been building things for a long time.

That visual identity matters because it reflects a deeper setting philosophy.

This is not just a dramatic stage for characters.  
It is a built environment.  
A used environment.  
A worked-in environment.

## So why the comparison still makes sense

Because **The Expanse** is really, really good.

That is honestly a big part of it.

If someone creates a serious Solar System setting with harder science-fiction leanings, comparisons to **The Expanse** are unavoidable because it is the strongest modern benchmark in exactly that space.

I do not see that as a problem. If anything, it means the comparison is being made for a good reason.

But comparison is not identity.

## Final answer

So: is **SOL 2349** influenced by **The Expanse**?

Obviously yes.

Is it trying to be **The Expanse**?

No.

It shares some broad territory with it because both settings take the Solar System and harder science fiction seriously. But the deeper you look, the more the differences start to matter:

- different tech assumptions
- different political fragmentation
- different historical development
- different role of aliens
- different emphasis on transhumanism
- different story scale
- different diplomatic texture
- different visual identity
- different long-term direction

Or in very simple terms:

**SOL 2349** is not “The Expanse with the serial numbers filed off.”  
It is my own industrial, transhuman, Solar System setting — one that happens to live in a neighborhood where **The Expanse** is the most famous house on the street.
