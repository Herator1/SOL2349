# Overview

Space piracy in 2349 is not an organized empire, nor a unified threat. It exists as a **fragmented, opportunistic ecosystem** operating primarily in the Asteroid Belt and other lightly controlled regions of the solar system.

Pirates are not conquerors. They do not hold territory, build fleets, or wage open war. Instead, they operate in small groups or independent cells, adapting to opportunity, risk, and local conditions. Many are former security personnel, transport crews, or freelancers who turned away from corporate structures—sometimes out of necessity, sometimes by choice.

What connects them is not ideology, but pragmatism.

---
# How Piracy Works

Space piracy is defined by restraint as much as by action.

Most pirate operations focus on **low-risk, high-return targets**: unmanned cargo transports, poorly protected mining shipments, or industrial barges operating far from immediate support. These assets can be intercepted, redirected, or quietly stripped of valuable materials without triggering large-scale retaliation.

Direct attacks on heavily defended convoys are rare. Suicide operations do not exist. The balance between gain and consequence is always calculated, and survival is more important than profit.

In many cases, piracy overlaps with smuggling. Goods are diverted, rerouted, or resold through informal networks that extend across stations, colonies, and transport routes. The line between pirate and trader is often unclear.

---
# What Pirates Do Not Do

There are limits—clearly understood, even if not formally agreed upon.

Pirates do not destroy ships without reason. Blowing vessels apart and salvaging debris is inefficient and dangerous, but more importantly, it invites overwhelming retaliation from affected factions. Escalation is avoided wherever possible.

Full ship hijackings are also uncommon. Taking control of a vessel, its crew, and its transponder identity is complex and difficult to conceal. Such actions attract attention that most pirate groups cannot afford.

The objective is not dominance.  
It is **continuity**.

---
# Networks and Safe Havens

Pirate groups are loosely connected through shared contacts, informal agreements, and overlapping supply chains. These networks are fluid, often temporary, and rarely centralized.

Bases of operation vary. Some groups operate from hidden installations—abandoned stations, deep Belt structures, or repurposed infrastructure. Others live semi-openly in remote colonies, blending into local populations or maintaining arrangements with sympathetic communities.

In certain regions, piracy is not fully suppressed because it is not always inconvenient.

---
# Privateers and Proxy Conflict

In some cases, pirate groups are indirectly supported by larger actors. Corporations and factions may tolerate—or quietly encourage—operations that disrupt competitors without requiring formal engagement.

These **modern privateers** operate in a grey zone between legality and deniability. Their actions are unofficial, but not entirely unsupported. As long as escalation remains controlled, they serve a purpose.

This further blurs the line between crime and strategy.

---
# Enforcement and Consequences

When piracy becomes too visible or too disruptive, responses follow. These may take the form of targeted corporate raids, bounty contracts, or limited security operations aimed at restoring stability.

However, full-scale military intervention is rare. The cost of such action often outweighs the losses incurred. In many cases, it is more efficient to absorb a degree of piracy than to eliminate it entirely.

As with everything in the Belt, enforcement is **pragmatic**.

---
# Reputation and Perception

Perception of pirates varies widely across the solar system.

In the inner system, they are seen as criminals—unreliable, disruptive, and dangerous to economic stability. On Earth, Luna, and Venus, piracy is associated with risk and loss.

In the Belt, the picture is more complex. Some pirates are viewed with a degree of respect, particularly those who operate with restraint, avoid unnecessary harm, and maintain informal codes of conduct. Independence from corporate control can carry a certain legitimacy in regions where such control is deeply resented.

Not all pirates are admired.  
But not all are condemned.

---
# A Necessary Friction

Space piracy persists not because it is dominant, but because it is **difficult to eliminate without disrupting the system itself**. It exists in the gaps—between routes, between authorities, and between economic calculations. It adapts, contracts, expands, and shifts with changing conditions.

Pirates do not control space. But they are part of how it works.
