## Overview

The **Interplanetary Labor Assembly** is the largest recognized umbrella organization for organized labor across human space. It links worker associations, maintenance guilds, dock unions, freight crews, habitat service labor, refinery workers, station technicians, and long-haul infrastructure personnel into a common political framework able to negotiate beyond the scale of any single station or employer.

Despite its name, the Assembly is **not** the dominant labor voice of all humanity. Its core strength lies in the orbital and off-world economy: the worlds of docks, habitats, shipyards, traffic control, life-support maintenance, cargo handling, industrial support, and long-distance transport. It matters most wherever labor and infrastructure are tightly bound together and where stoppages can turn quickly into systemic disruption.

The Assembly has far less influence in the great population centers of **[[Earth]]**, in the corporate-political machinery of **[[Venus]]**, or inside the more controlled institutional structures of **[[Mars]]**. Its real domain is the working architecture of human space.

## Historical Role

The Assembly emerged gradually out of the hard realities of off-world labor. Orbital habitats, docks, shipyards, and industrial stations could not function without highly trained workers whose labor was difficult to replace and whose mistakes could kill entire populations. Life-support crews, hull technicians, docking personnel, maintenance workers, tug operators, refinery staff, and long-haul support workers all occupied positions too essential to be treated as disposable indefinitely.

Early labor associations were mostly local and defensive. They formed to resist contract abuse, unsafe staffing levels, predatory residency terms, withheld emergency provisions, and the routine tendency of powerful operators to treat habitat workers as extensions of infrastructure rather than as populations with rights. Over time, these local groups found common cause. As traffic expanded and stations became more interconnected, labor politics also became interplanetary.

The result was the **Interplanetary Labor Assembly**: not a single union, but a federated labor structure capable of setting minimum standards, coordinating industrial action, and turning local abuses into wider political issues.

## Sphere of Influence

The Assembly is strongest in sectors directly tied to the operation of human space. These include:

- orbital habitats and mixed-use stations  
- docking and cargo handling systems  
- long-haul freight support  
- shipyards and construction frames  
- life-support maintenance  
- pressure-shell inspection and repair  
- refinery and industrial processing environments  
- emergency technical services  
- cislunar and Belt-side support infrastructure  

In these environments, labor has unusual leverage because systems cannot simply be paused without consequence. Even short disruptions in maintenance, docking, repair, or environmental operations can create cascading risk. That gives the Assembly real bargaining power.

Its influence weakens sharply once one moves into the major planetary population centers. On **[[Earth]]**, labor politics are far too large, diverse, and regionally fragmented for the Assembly to dominate. On **[[Venus]]**, corporate control and syndicate-entangled labor systems often blunt or bypass its reach. On **[[Mars]]**, highly institutional and technocratic structures leave less room for the Assembly to set the overall framework, though it may still matter in orbital and transport-linked sectors such as **[[Phobos]]**.

## Core Functions

### Minimum Standards

One of the Assembly’s most important roles is the definition and defense of baseline labor standards for space-linked work. These include staffing levels, emergency access, hazard compensation, rotation lengths, maintenance crew ratios, exposure limits, habitat residency protections tied to employment, and the minimum conditions under which dangerous or infrastructure-critical work may be legally assigned.

These standards are not universally enforced, but they are widely recognized. Even actors that ignore them often feel the need to justify doing so.

### Collective Bargaining

The Assembly gives otherwise fragmented workers the ability to negotiate at scales large enough to matter. A single dock union or habitat service bloc may be vulnerable on its own. Linked through the Assembly, such groups can bargain across multiple stations, contractors, or logistics corridors.

This is especially important in sectors where employers attempt to fragment responsibility across subsidiaries, shell operators, subcontractors, and jurisdictional boundaries.

### Strike Coordination

The Assembly is one of the few legal civic actors in human space capable of organizing strikes or coordinated slowdowns across multiple off-world sites. It does not do so lightly. In environments where air, pressure, food, and docking reliability matter, labor action can carry immense risk. For that reason, most Assembly actions are highly targeted: work-to-rule campaigns, maintenance slowdowns, selective handling refusals, corridor coordination stoppages, or synchronized labor withdrawals in non-critical sectors.

When full strikes do occur, they are politically significant. They signal that workers believe normal negotiation has already failed.

### Representation and Exposure

The Assembly also serves as a public-facing body able to document abuses and turn hidden labor conditions into recognized political issues. In a civilization where companies often hide behind contracts, remoteness, and infrastructure complexity, simply making abuse legible can be a form of power.

This is one of the reasons the Assembly often works in uneasy parallel with bodies such as the **[[Habitat Rights Commission]]**.

## Structure

The **Interplanetary Labor Assembly** is not a centralized union with a single rigid command structure. It is better understood as a layered federation of affiliated labor bodies. Some are traditional unions. Some are station worker associations. Some are technical guilds, docking organizations, maintenance councils, or long-haul crew blocs. Different regions and industries join under different historical conditions.

This federated structure is one of its strengths. It allows the Assembly to coordinate across huge distances and varied legal systems without requiring total ideological unity. It is also one of its weaknesses. Local priorities differ. Dock labor, long-haul freight crews, refinery maintenance personnel, and habitat service workers do not always want the same thing at the same time.

As a result, the Assembly is often most effective when it acts on clearly recognizable shared issues: unsafe staffing, exploitative contract residency, withheld emergency support, catastrophic maintenance cuts, or attempts to break labor power through fragmentation.

## Relationship with the Megacorporations

The Assembly exists in permanent tension with the **[[Megacorporations]]**, especially those whose operations depend heavily on disciplined labor and uninterrupted throughput.

- **[[Atlas]]** often clashes with the Assembly over habitat maintenance staffing, structural certification pressure, construction schedules, and lease-linked labor dependency.  
- **[[Helios]]** tends to collide with it over refinery labor, extraction-side support crews, industrial casualties, and cost discipline in harsh operational environments.  
- **[[NovaChem]]** enters conflict where environmental systems workers, habitat chemistry technicians, or emergency support staff are treated as replaceable inputs.  
- **[[Archeron]]** tends to appear wherever labor disputes are securitized, especially in high-value industrial or contested frontier environments.  

The Assembly can win concessions, expose abuse, and coordinate meaningful disruption. But it cannot always force compliance. Powerful actors can delay, isolate, reroute, outsource, intimidate, or simply absorb scandal if the strategic stakes are high enough.

## Tension with [[Kaiten]]

The Assembly’s relationship with [[Kaiten]] is unusually complex. In theory, the two should be natural allies. Both claim to defend workers against exploitative contract systems, unsafe operating conditions, and the tendency of larger powers to treat labor as expendable. In practice, however, they often collide.

Part of the reason is structural. [[Kaiten]] does not see itself merely as a transport organization. It sees itself as a worker-rooted power in its own right: one built by crews, docks, route culture, and long-haul mutual dependence. From Kaiten’s perspective, the **Interplanetary Labor Assembly** can look redundant, distant, or naive—an outside labor body trying to speak for sectors that Kaiten believes it already represents better through its own institutions.

The Assembly, by contrast, views Kaiten as deeply limited. However worker-oriented its self-image may be, Kaiten ultimately protects **its own people, its own routes, and its own internal structures first**. It is not a general labor movement for human space. It is a transport collective with its own interests, hierarchies, exclusions, and power logic. Where non-Kaiten workers, independent station crews, contract maintenance personnel, or rival labor bodies are concerned, Kaiten can be as territorial and unsentimental as any other major system actor.

This produces recurring friction. Membership or dual affiliation in both structures is often viewed with suspicion. Kaiten tends to distrust workers and organizers who look to the Assembly for broader solidarity beyond route culture, while the Assembly is wary of labor politics being absorbed into Kaiten’s narrower freight-centered worldview. In some regions, especially around docks, convoy systems, and Belt logistics corridors, the two compete directly for legitimacy, representation, and the right to define what labor protection is supposed to mean.

As a result, their relationship shifts constantly between tactical cooperation and open rivalry. They may align during major safety disputes, anti-contractor campaigns, or conflicts with powerful corporate operators. But just as often, they accuse one another of fragmentation, opportunism, territorialism, or betrayal. In the politics of human space, [[Kaiten]] may be closer to labor than most major powers—but it is not the same thing as the **Interplanetary Labor Assembly**.

## Legitimacy and Limits

The Assembly is legal, official, and widely recognized across much of human space. That matters. It is not merely an underground resistance network or a fringe labor cult. In many sectors, its representatives are accepted participants in negotiations over staffing, hazard compensation, emergency standards, and infrastructure-linked labor rights.

But legality is not the same as power. The Assembly’s greatest weakness is enforcement. It can define standards, organize labor, and make conflict visible. It cannot always compel compliance where employers control docking access, contract housing, emergency support, debt structures, or critical route timing.

This gives the Assembly a distinctive political position: too legitimate to ignore completely, too weak to dominate the system, and too necessary to disappear.

## Reputation

To workers in stations, docks, shipyards, and long-haul infrastructure, the Assembly is often seen as one of the few institutions willing to insist that life-support economies still contain human beings. To executives and corridor operators, it can look like a disruptive force that treats fragile infrastructure as leverage. Both perceptions contain truth.

The Assembly is not revolutionary in the sense of trying to overthrow the entire order of human space. Its politics are usually more practical than ideological. It wants safer systems, livable rotations, enforceable minimums, and fewer ways for powerful actors to make survival conditional on obedience.

That is already more than enough to make it dangerous in the eyes of many employers.

## Role in the Solar System

The **Interplanetary Labor Assembly** is one of the most important civic counterweights to corporate and infrastructural power in **SOL 2349**. It does not rule worlds, own stations, or command fleets. What it does command, at least sometimes, is the ability to remind civilization that docks do not function by themselves, habitats do not maintain themselves, and air does not keep flowing because a contract says it should.

In a Solar System built on fragile systems and hard labor, that gives the Assembly lasting political weight.
