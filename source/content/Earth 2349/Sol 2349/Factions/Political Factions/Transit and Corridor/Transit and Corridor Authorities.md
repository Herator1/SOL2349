# Transit and Corridor Authorities

In a civilization built on slow movement and fragile logistics, the regulation of access, docking, routing, priority, and emergency passage becomes a form of political power in its own right. Some important institutions derive their authority not from territory or population, but from their role in governing the corridors between worlds.

These authorities are rarely governments in the traditional sense. Most function instead as transit regimes, arbitration bodies, technical compacts, or safety frameworks that exist because interplanetary civilization would become dangerously unstable without them. They help regulate the movement of cargo, passengers, and strategic mass across the spaces between worlds.

---

## Important Factions

### [[Cislunar Traffic Coordination Board]]

One of the most important transit authorities in human space, responsible for managing the dense and politically sensitive movement environment of the **[[Earth]]–[[Luna]]** corridor. The Board regulates critical approach windows, mass-driver traffic interfaces, docking priority, emergency rerouting, and the practical sequencing of some of the busiest orbital flows in the Solar System.

It is not a sovereign government, but its decisions carry enormous weight because cislunar traffic is too dense and too strategically important to function without constant coordination. This places it in frequent tension with actors such as the **[[Earth Deterrence Forces]]**, **[[Luna]]**, **[[Atlas]]**, and **[[Kaiten]]**, all of whom have their own interests in how movement through the corridor is managed.

---

### [[Inner System Freight Arbitration Council]]

A formally neutral but heavily contested arbitration body dealing with commercial and logistical disputes across the inner Solar System. Its jurisdiction typically includes freight claims, delivery failures, salvage conflicts, damaged cargo, route liability, and disputes over responsibility for delays, losses, or contract breakdowns in transit.

Although strongly influenced by megacorporate pressure and legal maneuvering, the Council remains important because the economics of slow interplanetary transport depend on a widely recognized framework for resolving disputes without constant escalation. In a system where cargo can take weeks or months to move, reliable arbitration is not an administrative luxury. It is part of what makes trade possible at all.

---

### [[Docking Reciprocity Compact]]

A widely recognized inter-station agreement that standardizes emergency docking rights, minimum rescue obligations, repair access, distress protocols, and certain categories of temporary harbor protection. It applies across many major stations, docks, and long-haul service nodes, especially where refusing assistance too casually could turn a local crisis into a broader systemic failure.

The Compact is part technical standard, part political agreement. It does not eliminate local discretion or power politics, but it establishes a shared baseline of conduct in a civilization where denying access to air, repair, or safe docking can easily become a death sentence. For that reason, it is one of the clearest examples of how infrastructure law and survival ethics overlap in human space.

---

### [[Outer Corridor Safety Accord]]

A long-range safety and continuity regime covering the major outbound routes toward the outer Solar System. The Accord defines standards for rescue coordination, beacon maintenance, emergency depots, convoy reporting, hazard notification, and the minimum infrastructure expectations required to keep long-haul movement viable beyond the inner system.

It is not a government and does not command the outer routes directly. Its influence comes instead from the fact that too many actors depend on its standards to ignore them. The farther one moves toward **[[Jupiter]]**, **[[Saturn]]**, and beyond, the more fragile movement becomes—and the more politically important corridor safety turns out to be.
