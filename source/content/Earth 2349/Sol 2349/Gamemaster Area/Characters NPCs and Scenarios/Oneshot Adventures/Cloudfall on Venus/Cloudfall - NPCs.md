## 🧑‍💼 **Elena Varga**

_Infrastructure Optimization Director_

Elena Varga is the human face of Aphrodite Prime’s system logic. She is not a villain in the traditional sense, and she should never be played like one. She does not enjoy suffering, and she does not posture as cruel. What makes her dangerous is that she has accepted the city’s logic completely. To her, instability is not a moral outrage but a systems problem, and systems problems are solved through prioritization, containment, and controlled sacrifice.

She does not hide information once the players reach her. In fact, she is strongest when she is honest. She explains clearly, calmly, and without apology. She does not argue emotionally. She reframes. When the players talk about failure, she talks about allocation. When they talk about injustice, she talks about consequence. When they talk about saving one district, she talks about preserving an entire city.

Varga’s real power is not physical. It is administrative, procedural, and narrative. She can decide how the players’ actions are recorded, who carries legal exposure, whether their employer is protected or destroyed, whether Node 3 access is treated as emergency diagnostics or sabotage, and whether the crew leaves Aphrodite Prime as useful contractors or convenient culprits. She is not threatening because she can kill them personally. She is threatening because she can decide which version of events becomes official.

### **How to play her**

- calm, precise, controlled
- never raises her voice
- never reacts emotionally
- answers directly, then reframes the meaning
- treats the players as capable adults, not subordinates
- never sounds theatrical, only certain

### **Example tone**

> “You’re looking for failure. There isn’t one.”  
> “There is only allocation.”

> “You have already made yourselves visible. I can still decide what kind of visibility that becomes.”

> “You can be right. Or you can survive being right. Those are not always the same thing.”

### **Scenario Use**

Use Varga as:

- the final moral and political pressure point of the scenario
- the voice of system continuity
- the person who makes cooperation tempting and resistance dangerous

👉 No combat stats needed. Varga should never be fought.

## 🧑‍🏭 **Darian Velasco**

_Local Infrastructure Coordinator_

Velasco is the voice of AP-LC-47. He knows the lower cluster well enough to understand that something is wrong, but lacks the authority, reach, or leverage to make the system care. He is not a revolutionary, not a heroic leader, and not a grand political thinker. He is simply the kind of person people come to when they need practical answers in a place that has stopped getting them from above.

He has already tried the official route. Reports were filed, inspections happened, numbers improved, and the real situation got worse. That experience defines him. He is not naive anymore, but he has not fully given up either.

Velasco matters because he grounds the scenario. Before it becomes a story about system logic, filtered truth, and administrative violence, it is a story about a real district with real people in it. Velasco keeps that visible. He is the character who reminds the GM and the players that AP-LC-47 is not an abstract data point.

He can provide context, introductions, local trust, and practical ways through the lower cluster. He can point the players toward routes upward, explain what happened after the last inspection, and make clear that staying local will not solve the problem. What he cannot do is protect them once the issue moves into the middle and upper layers.

### **How to play him**

- direct, grounded, practical
- slightly exhausted, but not defeated
- distrusts official reassurance
- respects competence quickly
- speaks like someone with no time left for politeness

### **Example tone**

> “They already know. That’s the problem.”

> “If you stop now, nothing changes.”

> “Whatever this is, it didn’t start down here.”

### **Light Stat Profile (optional)**

- **HP:** Low
- **Combat:** Minimal, improvised tools only
- **Strengths:** Local knowledge, trust network, credibility
- **Use in combat:** Avoid; he is not a fighter

### **Scenario Use**

Use Velasco as:

- the local anchor of the scenario
- a source of practical direction and pressure
- the reminder that the stakes are human before they are political

## 🔧 **Yara N’Doye**

_Local Technician_

Yara is the first person in the scenario who openly says what the players are beginning to suspect: the numbers do not match reality. She has already watched the system change its presentation without changing the problem, and she has learned the right lesson from that — not that the city is failing, but that someone has decided what failure is allowed to look like.

She is cautious because she understands the risk of being noticed. She is not passive, but she has adapted to a world where direct confrontation with the system is rarely survivable. She watches first, trusts slowly, and speaks carefully. Once she decides the crew is not just another layer of denial, she becomes one of the most important emotional anchors in the scenario.

Yara matters because she makes the stakes personal before they become systemic. Velasco explains the district. Yara embodies what it feels like to live inside the lie.

### **How to play her**

- alert, quiet, careful
- watches surroundings constantly
- speaks in short, deliberate statements
- trust is earned, not assumed
- clearly frightened of official attention, without becoming helpless

### **Example tone**

> “The numbers changed. Not the system.”

> “They didn’t fix it. They fixed what we see.”

> “That’s new normal.”

### **Light Stat Profile (optional)**

- **HP:** Low
- **Skills:** Systems, Analysis, Local Infrastructure Knowledge
- **Combat:** None
- **Use:** Information source, witness, emotional anchor

### **Scenario Use**

Use Yara as:

- the first credible crack in the official story
- a witness to manipulated stability
- the person who makes the players care before the abstraction sets in

## 🛡️ **Mid-Level Security Personnel**

_Corporate Security Units_

Security in Cloudfall should never feel like generic action guards. These personnel are trained for containment, not bravado. They are calm, efficient, procedurally confident, and unsettling precisely because they do not behave like people expecting a fight. They are there to narrow possibilities, not create spectacle.

Their job is to ask questions, classify behavior, isolate disturbances, and restore procedural control. Violence is a tool of last resort, not their default mode. When they do escalate, it is measured and coordinated. They do not shout, posture, or threaten dramatically. They simply make the environment less permissive.

This is important. If played correctly, they feel like the city growing less patient.

### **How to play them**

- patient, procedural, detached
- controlled posture, minimal wasted movement
- polite, but never warm
- escalate only when control is genuinely threatened
- more interested in classification and isolation than punishment

### **Example tone**

> “You are outside your assigned service band.”

> “State your task.”

> “This area is under review.”

### **Statblock (Standard Unit)**

- **HP:** Medium (≈ 12–15)
- **Guard:** Medium
- **Armor:** Light Tactical

### **Attributes**

- **Combat:** +2
- **Physical:** +1
- **Systems:** 0
- **Presence:** +1

### **Actions (3-Action Economy)**

**Controlled Shot (1 Action)**  
Ranged attack. Medium damage. Can be used non-lethally.

**Suppression (2 Actions)**  
Area denial. Targets must reposition or suffer penalty. May reduce enemy actions next turn.

**Restrain (1 Action)**  
Close range control action. On success, target is slowed or immobilized.

**Tactical Reposition (1 Action)**  
Move and gain Guard until next turn.

### **Behavior**

- focus on the most disruptive target
- work in coordinated pairs or trios
- prioritize immobilizing, isolating, and containing
- withdraw or wait for support if a fight becomes inefficient

### **Scenario Use**

Use security to:

- increase pressure
- make the system feel intelligent
- punish loud or reckless play without turning the scenario into a firefight

## 🤖 **Security Drone Units**

_Automated Enforcement Systems_

Drones are the cleanest combat tool in Cloudfall. They are ideal for early or mid-level confrontation because they create pressure without immediately turning the scene into human bloodshed. They are first-response containment systems: fast, reactive, highly trackable, and designed to mark, slow, disorient, and hold people until the city decides what to do next.

They should almost always be deployed non-lethally by default. Their job is not to kill intruders. Their job is to preserve control.

### **How to play them**

- quick, responsive, impersonal
- little or no spoken output
- focus on tracking, stunning, slowing, and isolating
- behave like tools, not opponents with personality

### **Statblock (Light Drone)**

- **HP:** Low (≈ 6–8)
- **Guard:** Low
- **Armor:** None

### **Attributes**

- **Combat:** +2
- **Systems:** +1
- **Physical:** 0

### **Actions**

**Stun Pulse (1 Action)**  
Short range. Low damage. Applies stun or reaction penalty.

**Tether Line (1 Action)**  
Restricts movement. Target is slowed, snagged, or briefly pinned.

**Scan Target (1 Action)**  
Marks target. Next attack against that target gains a bonus.

**Evasive Hover (Reaction)**  
Reduce incoming damage or shift out of ideal firing line.

### **Behavior**

- prioritize the nearest or most visible target
- coordinate on marked targets
- retreat if heavily damaged unless specifically ordered to hold
- attempt containment before harm

### **Statblock (Heavy Drone / Optional)**

- **HP:** Medium (≈ 10–12)
- **Guard:** Medium

**Pulse Burst (2 Actions)**  
Area attack. Medium damage. Can push or stagger targets slightly.

👉 Use sparingly. Heavy drones should signal real escalation.

## **Encounter Design Guidelines**

Combat in Cloudfall should never feel rewarding in itself. It should feel consequential.

The purpose of security encounters is not to create balanced tactical set pieces for their own sake. It is to increase pressure, delay the players, and make them more visible inside the system. Every fight should cost them something: time, access, goodwill, cover, or future options.

A good way to think about combat in this scenario is that it serves three functions:

- **pressure**
- **delay**
- **visibility**

### **Early Combat (Recommended)**

**1–2 Light Drones**  
Fast, low-risk, and ideal for teaching the players how the system responds to disruption.

### **Mid-Level Combat**

**2 Security + 1 Drone**  
Controlled and tactical. Enough pressure to feel serious, but still clearly a containment response.

### **Escalation Combat**

**3–4 Security or 1 Heavy Drone**  
Use only if the players push hard, become openly hostile, or force the system into active intervention.

## **Important GM Principle**

Combat is not the solution in Cloudfall.

After every fight:

- system awareness increases
- access becomes harder
- surveillance intensifies
- the players become easier to frame as the problem

A simple system feedback line helps a lot:

> “Behavior flagged: hostile.”  
> “Access restrictions updated.”

That line should matter more than the damage.

## **Design Intent**

None of these NPCs are meant to function as classic enemies. Even when they oppose the players, they are not acting out of hatred, sadism, or personal rivalry. They are components of a larger machine.

They are:

- extensions of the system
- responses to disruption
- tools of classification, containment, and controlled correction

That is the core tone to preserve.

> The system does not fight.  
> It contains.
