# 🧭 Act 2 — Arrival at the Station

## Scene Purpose

This act establishes the station as a physical place, a failed piece of Belt infrastructure, and the scene of a recent and unfinished conflict.

It should communicate four things quickly:

- the players are vulnerable
- the station is damaged but not dead
- a violent struggle happened here very recently
- there are still survivors inside

The pacing should stay deliberate. This is not yet the action phase. It is the transition from shipboard survival into exploration, tension, and discovery.

---

# Part 1 — Docking, EVA, and the Dead Station

## GM Summary

The _Asterion_ approaches and docks with the hidden station. The players leave the ship in EVA suits, enter through a damaged docking section, and begin to explore the outer non-pressurized parts of the station. They find battle damage, dead bodies from two different factions, and a damaged but potentially repairable freighter. Before they reach the rotating inner habitat, they receive the first clear hint that the station is still inhabited.

---

## Situation

- the _Asterion_ is damaged and only barely maneuverable
- the station does not assist docking
- the outer station is partly dead, partly damaged, and partly intact
- the players do not yet know who fought here or who is still alive

The _Asterion_ remains their only secure fallback. Make that clear.

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Approach

> As the station grows in the forward view, it becomes clear that this is not a normal habitat and never was. The structure is built into the shell of a mined-out asteroid, its industrial core half-buried in old excavation voids and service shafts. Around one section, a damaged rotating habitat still turns, unevenly but persistently, as if the station is forcing itself to remain alive.
> 
> Large parts of the structure are dark. Other sections flicker with unstable light. There are no running signals, no guidance systems, and no response to your approach. By any reasonable standard, this place should have been abandoned long ago.
> 
> It hasn’t been.

---

## Phase 1 — Docking

The players must dock manually.

This is a good moment to emphasize that the station is not just damaged, but illegal, improvised, and outside the usual standards of Belt infrastructure. No reciprocity lights. No automated docking logic. No distress acknowledgement.

### Suggested Checks

**Piloting / Coordination**

**Success**

- clean docking
- stable seal
- no added strain on the ship

**Partial Success**

- rough docking
- minor additional damage or seal stress
- airlock remains usable, but not comfortably

**Failure**

- hard impact
- unstable seal, docking clamp stress, or delayed leak risk

**Engineering Support**

- can stabilize clamps or reduce docking risk
- good support action for non-pilots

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Docking

> The final approach is slow and awkward, every correction shaped by the damage the ship has already taken. The docking structure ahead is visibly scarred, with one berth torn open and another still barely intact. There are no lights to guide you in and no automated systems waiting to catch the approach.
> 
> The last meters pass in tense silence. Then the hull makes contact with a heavy metallic shudder, and the clamps engage. The ship settles against the station, not gently, but firmly enough to hold.

---

## Phase 2 — EVA Preparation

The players cannot assume the station is fully pressurized or safe to enter directly.

They must:

- suit up
- decide who leaves the ship
- choose what equipment to bring

### GM Guidance

Emphasize:

- helmets stay on
- communications may be delayed or limited
- the _Asterion_ is damaged, but still safer than the station

This is also a good point to remind players that load matters. If they over-equip here, it should feel like a deliberate choice.

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Airlock

> The inner hatch closes behind you with a heavy mechanical finality, and the airlock begins to cycle. Pressure drops steadily. The temperature shifts. For a few seconds, there is only the low vibration of the ship and the quiet work of the system.
> 
> Then the outer door opens, and the station lies beyond — dark, silent, and close enough now to feel real.

---

## Phase 3 — EVA Exterior

The players move through vacuum and zero-g from the _Asterion_ into the station’s docking section.

### Environment

- zero gravity
- no sound except suit audio and contact vibration
- drifting debris and exposed damage
- visible remains of the surrounding battle

### Suggested Checks

**EVA / Coordination**

**Success**

- clean movement
- no suit strain
- players remain in control

**Failure**

- awkward drift, collision, tether complication, or minor suit stress

This should not become slapstick. EVA movement should feel slow, deliberate, and slightly exhausting.

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Exterior

> From the outside, the station does not look abandoned so much as damaged beyond any sane operating standard. Hull sections are scorched, panels have been torn away, and one of the docking ports has been completely destroyed, its outer structure twisted outward by force. Debris drifts around the installation in slow, silent arcs, not randomly, but as the remains of something violent and recent.
> 
> Nearby, the broken hull of a much larger ship hangs against the black, fractured and hollow. It is immediately obvious that the debris field you crossed did not come from chance collision or ordinary salvage damage. A battle was fought here.

---

## Phase 4 — Entry Corridor

The players enter through a damaged but usable access corridor in the station’s outer docking section.

### Conditions

- still vacuum
- still zero-g
- no atmosphere, no sound, no gravity
- visible signs of recent corridor fighting

This section should feel less like “haunted dungeon” and more like the shell of a working infrastructure node that was hit hard and fast.

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — First Interior

> Your lights cut into a corridor that is still entirely without atmosphere. Loose fragments drift where pressure once held them down, and the walls are marked by tight, deliberate scorch patterns that speak of controlled fire rather than accident. Ahead, a body hangs caught against the wall by a snagged harness line, its helmet shattered inward.
> 
> Nothing moves. The stillness feels less like abandonment than interruption, as if the place was simply forced to stop in the middle of whatever it had been doing.

---

## Phase 5 — Exploration of the Outer Section

The players move deeper into the damaged docking area and outer service corridors.

### What They Should Find

#### Dead Station Personnel

- improvised armor
- patched suits
- close-range wounds
- equipment consistent with smugglers, salvagers, or station operators rather than formal military

#### Dead Recovery Team Personnel

- clean, standardized gear
- no insignia or deliberately stripped insignia
- better armor, more professional equipment
- wounds consistent with a hard push into defended corridors

A useful rule of thumb:

- about 3 dead station personnel for every 1 dead recovery operator

That ratio helps communicate that the attackers were stronger individually, but were eventually overwhelmed by defenders using terrain and numbers.

### Suggested Checks

**Perception / Analysis**

**Success**

- players identify two distinct factions
- this was not random violence or an accident

**Combat Analysis / Security / Intellect**

**Success**

- the better-equipped team advanced aggressively
- the defenders fought from cover and attritional positions
- this was a boarding action, not an internal firefight alone

---

## Phase 6 — The Freighter

The players reach a secondary docking bay and find the station freighter.

This is an important scene. The freighter should immediately register as more than scenery. In Belt terms, it means mobility, claim, cargo, bargaining power, and the possibility of leaving.

### Description

- medium freighter, not elegant, but built for real Belt work
- damaged by battle, but not destroyed
- still docked, not drifting
- likely repairable

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — The Freighter

> The corridor opens into a broader docking space, and there, secured inside the bay, is another ship. It is smaller than a true bulk hauler but larger than a shuttle, built in the blunt, practical style of Belt freight work. Its hull is scarred and stripped in places, and several sections show clear impact and heat damage, but the overall frame is still intact.
> 
> It did not drift here by accident. It was docked deliberately, and whatever happened afterward was serious enough to keep it from leaving.

---

### Suggested Check

**Engineering / Systems**

**Success**

- the freighter is damaged but repairable
- with time, parts, and access, it could become an escape option

**Failure**

- state remains unclear
- players know it is not dead, but cannot assess how recoverable it is

---

## Phase 7 — First Signs of Life

Before the spin-gravity transition, the players should receive the first real indication that the station is still active deeper inside.

This should be a hint, not a reveal.

### Possible Signals

- weak localized power readings
- internal system activity
- broken but deliberate short-range transmission
- something that is clearly not just a failing automatic system

### Suggested Checks

**Sensors / Comms / Systems**

**Success**

- isolated internal activity detected
- station is not fully dead

**High Success**

- fragmented transmission or short comm burst
- likely human, not automated

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Signal Hint

> As your systems continue to recalibrate against the station’s damaged structure, it becomes clear that this place is not entirely dead. Somewhere deeper inside, isolated systems are still running on limited and unstable power. For a moment, a signal sharpens just enough to resolve into something recognizable.
> 
> Not an automated ping. Not a system loop.
> 
> A transmission.
> 
> Weak, broken, and incomplete — but intentional.

---

## End of Part 1

By the end of this section, the players should understand:

- the station is active
- the battle was recent
- at least two sides were involved
- there is a second ship
- survivors are likely still present deeper inside

# Part 2 — Spin Transfer and First Contact

## GM Summary

The players move beyond the dead outer docking section and toward the only surviving habitable part of the station. To reach it, they must pass through the station’s spin transfer system: an improvised industrial lift that bridges the static outer structure and the rotating inner habitat. This transition introduces unstable spin gravity, thin atmosphere, and the first clear signs that parts of the station are still being actively inhabited.

From there, the players enter the surviving inner sections and are likely to make first contact with one of the two remaining factions.

---

## Scene Purpose

This section should:

- make the station feel structurally believable
- introduce unstable spin gravity as a physical experience
- reinforce that the station is a failing retrofit, not a normal habitat
- transition the tone from dead wreckage to inhabited danger
- prepare the first faction encounters

---

## Phase 1 — Reaching the Transfer System

The dead outer docking corridors do not connect directly to the inhabited sections of the station. Instead, the players eventually reach an old industrial transfer node: a reinforced shaft and cargo lift system used to move people, equipment, and freight between the static outer structure and the rotating habitat.

This is an important worldbuilding moment. The station was not originally built as a coherent habitat. It was assembled out of old mining and cargo infrastructure. The transfer system should make that obvious.

### What the Players Realize

- the outer station is static and remains in zero-g
- the inhabited section is mounted on a rotating structure deeper inside
- the connection between them is mechanical, rough, and visibly improvised
- this is a retrofit solution, not elegant station architecture

### Suggested Checks

**Engineering / Systems**

**Success**

- players understand how the transfer system works
- they identify worn couplings, unstable timing, or failing dampers
- they can reduce risk before activation

**Failure**

- the lift can still be used
- its roughness and instability come as more of a surprise

**Perception / Analysis**

**Success**

- old mining and cargo markings make the station’s industrial past obvious
- the lift was clearly designed for heavy material transfer, not comfort

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Transfer System

> The corridor ends at a reinforced shaft built deep into the inner structure of the station. What waits there is not a normal passenger elevator, but an industrial transfer cage — the kind of system once used to move cargo pallets, machinery, and maintenance crews between sections that were never originally designed to fit together.
> 
> Heavy rails run along the shaft walls. The cage itself is enclosed in mismatched plating, patched braces, manual overrides, and old warning markings half-buried under newer repairs. Everything about it suggests that this system was rebuilt, modified, and kept alive long past its intended service life.
> 
> Somewhere below and inward, the rotating section of the station continues to turn. This is the bridge between the dead exterior and the part that still matters.

---

## Phase 2 — The Spin Transfer

Once activated, the transfer lift carries the players from the static outer station into the rotating habitat. The transition is controlled, but rough. The station’s spin is uneven, and the transfer system compensates imperfectly.

This is where the players first feel spin gravity as something physical and unpleasant rather than abstract.

### What Happens

- the cage begins in zero-g
- as it moves deeper into the station, rotation gradually asserts itself
- the handover from static structure to rotating habitat is imperfect
- the players feel a shifting sense of “down” before stable footing arrives

### Suggested Checks

**Coordination / Low-G Movement**

**Success**

- players brace correctly
- they stay under control during the transition
- they arrive steady enough to move immediately

**Failure**

- awkward loss of footing, impact against the cage, or dropped gear
- brief disorientation or nausea after arrival

**Endurance / Fitness** _(optional)_  
Use this if you want the transition to feel physically punishing.

**Success**

- the player keeps composure and adapts quickly

**Failure**

- vertigo, nausea, or short-lived movement penalty

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Spin Transfer

> Inside the cage there is still no gravity, only handholds, restraints, and the low vibration of machinery that does not sound healthy enough for comfort. When the system engages, the movement is immediate but uneven, as if several compensators are trying to solve the same problem in slightly different ways.
> 
> At first nothing seems to change. Then your body notices it before your eyes do. A faint shift in orientation creeps into your sense of balance, followed by the first suggestion of weight. Your boots begin to catch against the deck, lightly at first, then with enough force to matter.
> 
> The station’s rotation does not arrive as a clean pull. It comes in imperfectly, through an old industrial system that was never meant to make this feel smooth. The cage shudders, overcorrects, and steadies again. By the time it settles, gravity exists — but only just, and not in any way that feels entirely trustworthy.

---

## Phase 3 — Entering the Pressurized Habitat

The transfer cage opens into the boundary zone of the rotating habitat. This is the first breathable interior the players have encountered, but it should not feel safe. The air is present, not healthy. The section is alive, not stable.

### Environment

- thin but usable atmosphere
- weak, inconsistent spin gravity
- sound returns in a muted, enclosed way
- systems remain visibly stressed
- helmets should remain on unless players have a very good reason to remove them

### Suggested Checks

**Environmental / Systems / Medical** _(optional)_

**Success**

- players recognize that atmosphere is survivable but degraded
- helmets can stay sealed without issue
- they understand this is a life-support edge case, not a safe habitat

**Failure**

- they cannot assess air quality clearly
- they know only that the section holds pressure

---

## 🎙️ Read Aloud — Entering the Habitat

> The inner hatch bears the signs of retained pressure. Frost clings to the edges of the seal, and dust shifts in the faint movement of air beyond it. When the door opens, the silence of vacuum gives way to the enclosed sounds of a station that is still barely functioning: distant structural creaks, low vibrations through the deck, and the tired hum of machinery working somewhere behind the walls.
> 
> The gravity here is weak and inconsistent, enough to stand in but never enough to trust. Loose objects rest where they have fallen. Dust gathers in seams and corners. The corridor around you feels lived in, patched, rerouted, and repeatedly repaired rather than truly maintained.
> 
> This is not a dead ruin. It is a place that has been kept alive by compromise, improvisation, and necessity.

---

## Phase 4 — Reading the Inhabited Section

At this point the players are no longer in the dead shell of the station. They are in the surviving habitat: the part that people are still using, defending, and trying to keep operational.

This should be the moment where the station stops feeling abandoned and starts feeling occupied.

### What the Players Can Notice

- patched conduits and bulkhead seals
- recently moved tools or storage crates
- signs of ration use, maintenance, and habitation
- service areas that have been kept functional while other sections were abandoned
- directional hints toward either defensible corridors or more lived-in spaces

### GM Guidance

This is the best moment to steer first contact through environment rather than fiat.

If the players follow:

- strong signals, clean kill-zones, or more defensible corridors → they likely reach the recovery team first
- atmosphere, habitation signs, or service-maintained spaces → they likely reach the Old Guard first

The choice should feel spatial and logical, not arbitrary.

---

## Encounter Option 1 — Recovery Team

### Typical Route Trigger

Players move toward:

- the strongest surviving signal
- the most defensible corridor network
- command-adjacent or tactically useful areas

### 🎙️ Read Aloud — Recovery Team Contact

> The corridor ahead is suddenly cut by a hard beam of light. It does not sweep or search. It fixes itself in place with deliberate control, holding the passage like a boundary. Beyond it, a figure is visible behind cover, armored and steady, weapon raised but not yet firing. Others shift in the half-light behind that first silhouette, disciplined enough to stay mostly concealed.
> 
> Their posture tells you what their equipment already suggested. These are not scavengers, not station workers, and not people improvising their way through a crisis. They are trained, armed, and still in control of themselves.

### NPC Overview

**Corporate Recovery Team**

- heavily armed
- disciplined and professional
- injured and low on life support
- focused on the relic and mission completion

### Interaction Tone

- controlled
- interrogative
- suspicious
- potentially cooperative only if the players seem useful

---

## Encounter Option 2 — Old Guard

### Typical Route Trigger

Players move toward:

- habitat corridors
- life support access
- service-maintained spaces
- areas that feel lived in rather than fortified

### 🎙️ Read Aloud — Old Guard Contact

> The lighting shifts before you see anyone. Emergency strips flicker along patched walls and improvised repairs, and then voices carry down the corridor — unfiltered, tired, and immediately alert to your presence. Figures step out from partial cover with weapons raised, but not with the stillness or precision of drilled soldiers.
> 
> Their gear is mismatched, worn, and practical. Their stance is defensive rather than theatrical. These are people who have been living here, working here, and trying to hold this place together for longer than they ever intended.

### NPC Overview

**Pirate Old Guard**

- lightly equipped
- more familiar with the station
- in somewhat better environmental position
- focused on survival, continuity, and control of what remains

### Interaction Tone

- cautious
- reactive
- more openly human than the recovery team
- negotiation feels more natural, but not necessarily safer

---

## GM Guidance — First Contact

Neither faction should attack immediately unless the players do something overtly aggressive.

Both sides will:

- assess the players
- test their usefulness
- establish control over the interaction
- try to determine whether the newcomers shift the balance of the station

The important tonal change here is this: the players are no longer exploring a battlefield. They are now moving between surviving interests inside a failing Belt installation.

---

## End of Act 2

By the end of this act, the players should understand:

- the station is still inhabited
- two hostile factions remain
- the rotating habitat is unstable and degrading
- the environment itself is becoming a threat
- escape will require leverage, cooperation, or violence

---

## GM Notes

- keep the pace controlled
- let physical detail and atmosphere do some of the work
- do not rush the first encounters
- make the two factions feel culturally distinct, not just mechanically different
- let route choice influence first contact whenever possible
