## OKL – Act 1

Act 1 begins aboard the **Kestrel**, a small [[Jovian League]] patrol craft on Saturn-system response duty. The ship is modern, clean, highly automated, and built for short, hard burns rather than long-range deployment. Its role is rapid intervention, interception, and first-response insertion, not heavy ship-to-ship combat. In the Saturn system, that makes it exactly the kind of vessel that would be sent first when a remote outpost goes dark. [[Enceladus]] is an especially plausible flashpoint for that kind of mission: isolated, high-value, scientific, and politically too sensitive to ignore. [[Nyx Data Systems]] also fits the setup well as a secretive information megacorp that would plausibly hide sensitive work behind deniable structures and compartmentalized oversight.

This act should feel controlled, professional, and tense, but not yet chaotic. It is the last stretch of order before the players enter a broken environment.

### Scene Goal

Act 1 establishes six things:

- the tone of the mission
    
- the professionalism of the team
    
- the isolation of the operation
    
- the practical problem of entry
    
- the fact that other response assets are already on the way
    
- the first sense that something is wrong beyond a simple takeover
    

Act 1 ends **just before touchdown or breach entry**.

---

## Aboard the Kestrel

The **Kestrel** is not large. It is a compact state patrol craft with a small permanent crew and no redundant troop section waiting behind the player characters. Only eight people are aboard:

- the five marines
    
- one pilot
    
- one comms and sensor operator
    
- one engineer
    

The ship is well maintained, disciplined, and functional. Equipment is clean. Corridors are narrow but not miserable. It feels like a professional state vessel that expects long patrol rotations and competent personnel, not like a rusting civilian hauler or an improvised combat boat.

The players should feel that this ship is safe.

That matters because they are about to leave it.

---

## Supporting Crew

These three NPCs stay aboard the **Kestrel** and do not join the landing team, but they should feel present and useful.

### Marek Ionescu — Pilot

Quiet, very controlled, and extremely precise. He does not dramatize the approach and rarely wastes words. He flies like someone who has done hard approaches in bad conditions before.

Use him for:

- calm delivery of practical risk
    
- comments on burn profile and approach vector
    
- reminders that the team will not have a clean extraction unless they create one
    
- comments on approaching traffic and insertion windows
    

### Lina Haddad — Comms / Sensor Operator

Focused, fast, and slightly more verbal than the others. She is the one confirming what the ship can and cannot know.

Use her for:

- sensor updates
    
- confirming the jammer
    
- inability to isolate meaningful station traffic
    
- relaying small changes in power signature, heat, or emissions
    
- tracking inbound traffic, including corporate response assets
    

### Tobias Krämer — Engineer

Dry, technically precise, a little pessimistic in the way experienced engineers often are. He tends to speak in system logic rather than dramatic language.

Use him for:

- analysis of docking damage
    
- warning about structural risks
    
- comments on breach points, maintenance shafts, and access options
    
- preparing insertion tools and checking suit integrity
    
- practical assessment of what outside support can and cannot do once the players are inside

## Possible Interactions Aboard Ship

Before the briefing or during prep, the players can move around the ship, ask questions, check gear, or talk to the crew.

### Things they can do

**Talk to the pilot**  
Marek can explain that the station approach is manageable, but the insertion is not. He can also make clear that the Kestrel is not going to sit close and visible for long if the tactical picture gets worse. If asked, he will also point out the obvious: in open space, other responding ships are not going to miss them, and they are not going to miss other responding ships.

**Talk to comms**  
Lina can confirm that the station is not silent. It is worse than silent. There is active noise, signal pollution, and interference consistent with an internal jammer. There are no clear voice channels, no usable emergency traffic, and no clean handshake.

If the players ask about other traffic, Lina can tell them that the Kestrel is not the only ship reacting to the incident. Long-range tracking suggests at least one additional inbound response craft on a delayed intercept, likely associated with corporate recovery or security channels. At this stage, that is not automatically suspicious. It is exactly what large corporations do when one of their facilities goes dark.

**Talk to the engineer**  
Tobias can walk them through the access problem. Docking damage is real. If they want a clean lock, they probably do not have one. If they want a safe insertion, that depends on what part of the station is still structurally sound.

If asked about the incoming corporate vessel, he can point out that corporate support is useful right up until it starts deciding what counts as a problem.

**Inspect gear and suits**  
Players can use the time to define loadout, check weapons, confirm suit seals, secure breaching gear, prep med supplies, or establish small personal habits. This is a good scene for tone and character work.

**Ask for tactical assumptions**  
The supporting crew cannot tell the players what is inside, but they can give practical possibilities:

- jammed stations often mean internal control attempts
    
- damaged docking can mean sabotage, fighting, or deliberate denial of entry
    
- if someone wanted to stop a clean extraction, destroying the dock would make sense
    
- if corporate recovery is already inbound, the players will not stay the only external authority on site for long
    

**Request entry advice**  
The crew can recommend options, but should never pick one for the players.

**Request contact with incoming corporate assets**  
This is entirely plausible. The Kestrel can attempt contact, or the corporate vessel may contact the Kestrel first once both parties are within useful range. The important point is that the players can know another team is coming without knowing what that team actually intends to do once it arrives.

## Read-Aloud: Mission Briefing

Use this after the players have assembled or settled into the briefing space.

> The command feed arrives with a fractional delay and a slight compression shimmer, clean enough to read, cold enough to feel impersonal.
> 
> A Jovian League officer appears on screen in formal duty frame, posture rigid, voice controlled.
> 
> “Kestrel Unit. You are being redirected to a Nyx-associated research outpost in the Enceladus sector.”
> 
> “We have received a fragmented distress burst indicating an active internal security event. Communications are degraded or actively suppressed. Docking infrastructure is reported damaged.”
> 
> “Current assessment allows for hostile seizure, internal sabotage, or extremist action. Situation remains unconfirmed.”
> 
> “Your mission is as follows: approach the station, gain entry, assess conditions on site, secure surviving key personnel if possible, and stabilize the situation pending follow-on response.”
> 
> “Additional response elements may be inbound through corporate recovery channels. Coordinate where practical. League intervention authority remains in effect until relieved by competent command.”
> 
> “Rules of engagement remain controlled. Avoid unnecessary casualties. Preserve operational flexibility.”
> 
> A brief pause.
> 
> “At this time, you are the nearest League asset capable of intervention.”
> 
> “Proceed.”

### What the briefing does not tell them

Do **not** mention:

- the true nature of the research
    
- that the inbound corporate team is effectively a cleanup force
    
- the fact that the jammer was activated by station leadership
    
- the internal ethical conflict behind the incident
    

At this stage, the mission should still feel like a difficult but conventional intervention with possible corporate coordination later.

---

## Standard Procedure: Get Ready for Deployment

After the briefing, the ship shifts into deployment mode.

This should feel routine, practiced, and impersonal.

Use short scene details:

- lockers open
    
- suit diagnostics scroll
    
- mag clamps click into place
    
- weapons are checked and rechecked
    
- med packs are sealed
    
- helmets are locked but not yet closed
    
- mission lights shift from patrol mode to deployment mode
    
- the ship becomes quieter, not louder
    

No one needs to shout. Everyone aboard knows what emergency prep looks like.

If you want a strong tonal line, have one of the support crew say something mundane and practical, such as:

- “Seal check in thirty seconds.”
    
- “If you cut through, do it once.”
    
- “No clean comms once you’re inside.”
    
- “Bring your own exit.”
    
- “If corporate gets there while you’re still inside, don’t assume they’re waiting for you.”
    

This phase is a good place to let the players:

- finalize gear
    
- ask last questions
    
- establish personal habits or squad rituals
    
- decide who carries what into the station

## The Burn

The Kestrel does not move like a civilian shuttle. Most of the time it waits, drifts, and patrols conservatively. But when it has to respond, it burns hard.

Describe the response burn as controlled violence:

- acceleration couches taking weight
    
- systems compensating cleanly
    
- no dramatic shaking, just pressure and force
    
- the sense that the ship is doing something expensive and deliberate
    

### Transfer to the Pilgrim

When the insertion plan is finalized, the team is moved out of the Kestrel and into the **Pilgrim-class lander**.

The transfer is quick and procedural. A short pressurized passage or docking collar links the patrol craft to the lander, and the difference between the two ships is immediate. The **Kestrel** feels tight but orderly, built for patrol work and long shifts. The **Pilgrim** feels denser, more mechanical, and far less comfortable — a vehicle built to survive descent, land hard, and lift out again.

Inside, everything is arranged around deployment. Crash couches, restraint frames, cargo tie-downs, suit brackets, breaching gear, med packs, and equipment lockers fill most of the usable space. There is no room for ceremony. Marines file in, clip weapons and loose gear into place, and strap into acceleration restraints along the inner hull. Once harnesses are locked, movement is limited and conversation usually drops off.

The launch from the Kestrel is not dramatic. There is a muted separation jolt, a few attitude corrections, then the heavier push as the Pilgrim commits to descent. The team does not get a panoramic view of Enceladus below. They get vibration through the frame, status lights, hull noise, and the occasional clipped update from the pilot. Final approach feels mechanical and controlled rather than cinematic: thruster pulses, changing engine tone, small corrections, and the growing sense that the lander is balancing itself against uncertain ground.

Touchdown comes as a hard transfer of weight through the hull and landing frame, followed by a brief settling shudder. Then the restraints release, the compartment changes from transit to deployment mode, and the team unclips, checks seals, grabs gear, and stacks at the hatch.

### Read-Aloud: Burn to Approach

> The patrol drift ends without drama. One moment the ship is quiet and level in its own routines; the next, the Kestrel commits.
> 
> The burn comes on hard and clean. Weight drives down through your frame as the reactor feeds the torch and the ship stops pretending to be patient. Loose thoughts disappear under procedure. Displays brighten. Hull systems reconfigure. Somewhere below, the engine stack becomes the most important thing in your world.
> 
> Nobody speaks for a few seconds. There is no need.
> 
> The Kestrel is no longer waiting. It is coming in.

This does not need rules unless you want them. It is mostly tone.

## Approach to the Station

As the Kestrel closes, the station becomes visible in detail.

What the players can confirm:

- the station is still structurally viable
    
- major docking infrastructure is damaged or unusable
    
- the station is not broadcasting meaningful distress traffic
    
- noise and interference are still active
    
- there are intermittent power signatures and heat sources
    
- no clean tactical picture is possible from outside
    
- other inbound response traffic is likely already tracking both the station and the Kestrel
    

### Read-Aloud: First Visual on the Outpost

> The outpost hangs against black space with the stillness only damaged machinery can have. It is not wrecked. That would almost be easier.
> 
> The structure is intact enough to remain occupied, but the docking section is visibly compromised — twisted framing, damaged approach hardware, sections gone dark. No running lights guide you in. No control traffic answers.
> 
> The station is not silent. It is drowning in its own noise.
> 
> Static crawls across the sensor bands where clean signal should be. Power still moves through the hull in broken intervals. Heat blooms come and go inside the structure, too scattered to read as orderly traffic, too persistent to ignore.
> 
> Something is alive in there.
> 
> You just do not know what shape it has yet.

## Information the Crew Can Provide on Approach

### Marek

- approach is stable
    
- no clean dock
    
- Kestrel can hold position briefly, not indefinitely
    
- if extraction goes bad, pickup may require improvisation
    
- if another vessel is inbound, it will be tracking the Kestrel well before arrival
    

### Lina

- jammer is active and local
    
- no usable station traffic
    
- noise is too dense for voice recovery
    
- passive returns suggest intermittent internal activity, not total abandonment
    
- another inbound ship or response element is detectable on a later intercept line
    
- its profile is consistent with delayed corporate recovery or security response
    

### Tobias

- docking damage looks deliberate or at least selectively catastrophic
    
- some access points may still exist outside the main dock
    
- emergency airlocks, maintenance shafts, or damaged hull sections are the most plausible entry routes
    
- any forced breach carries decompression and structural risks
    
- once a second team arrives, the players may lose control of both the site and the narrative around what happened there

## Optional Scene: Contact with Corporate Response

Because there is no meaningful stealth in open space, it is entirely logical for the Kestrel and the inbound corporate vessel to become aware of each other during the approach.

This contact should feel normal at first.

The point of the scene is **not** to reveal the corporate team as villains. It is to establish them as a known factor before they become a problem later.

### If the players or crew initiate contact

The Kestrel may attempt a formal identification request, standard deconfliction hail, or simple status exchange.

### If the corporate vessel initiates contact

Use a clean, professional, slightly over-controlled tone.

> “Jovian League patrol vessel Kestrel, this is Nyx recovery element Aegis-3. We are inbound to support site stabilization and personnel recovery.”
> 
> A slight pause.
> 
> “Please confirm current approach status and estimated time to insertion.”

At this stage, nothing in the transmission has to sound openly hostile. In fact, it works better if it does not.

The players should hear:

- corporate response is coming
    
- they are first on site
    
- they will not be alone for long
    

What they should **not** yet hear is:

- cleanup
    
- evidence control
    
- narrative management
    
- elimination of witnesses
    

This scene is most effective if it feels bureaucratically plausible.

## Entry Planning

The players should now decide how they intend to enter.

Present options as practical choices, not puzzle solutions. Each one should feel valid, but none should feel safe.

### Likely Entry Options

**Emergency airlock**  
Most direct. Possibly the cleanest route inward if still functional. Also the most likely to be watched, locked down, or trapped.

**Maintenance shaft**  
Tighter, slower, and less predictable. Good for stealth inside the station, bad for comfort and speed. This is not stealth in the sense of hiding a ship in space. It is only a quieter way to move once the team is already on the hull.

**Hull breach**  
Fast access into a compromised section. Higher environmental risk. Immediate loss of control over where the team enters.

**Forced entry**  
Cutting or creating a breach. Loud, dangerous, and escalatory. Potentially effective, but likely to change the station’s internal response immediately.

The support crew can help assess risk, but the choice belongs to the players.

---

## GM Guidance for Act 1 Tone

Keep Act 1 grounded.  
Do not rush to combat.  
Do not dump the conspiracy.  
Do not let the scene become cinematic heroics too early.

Act 1 should feel like:

- professionals preparing carefully
    
- a ship doing exactly what it was built to do
    
- incomplete information becoming operational pressure
    
- the awareness that outside actors are already converging on the incident
    
- the last moment of clarity before ambiguity starts
    

The players should end this act ready, committed, and just about to cross into the station.

---

## End of Act 1

Act 1 ends the moment the insertion begins for real:

- just before they touch the hull
    
- just before the airlock cycles
    
- just before they cut through
    
- just before they cross the breach line
    

That cut is important.

Act 2 begins with entry, exposure, and the first real human contact inside.
