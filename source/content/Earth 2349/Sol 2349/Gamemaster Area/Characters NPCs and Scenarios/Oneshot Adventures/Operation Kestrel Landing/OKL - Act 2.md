## Act 2 – Surface Insertion and Entry

Act 2 begins the moment the team leaves the **Kestrel** and transfers into the **Pilgrim-class lander** for surface insertion on **Enceladus**.

From that point on, the players are committed. The last layer of shipboard control falls away: the clean routines of the Kestrel, the immediate protection of its crew, and the certainty of a secure extraction platform. What follows is colder, slower, and more exposed.

This act is about **descent, landing, approach, entry, uncertainty, first contact, and first contradiction**.

The players should not yet understand the full situation, but they should begin to realize that the mission briefing is incomplete and that the people inside are not easily divided into victims and aggressors.

---

## Scene Goal

Act 2 establishes six things:

- the Pilgrim insertion is controlled, tense, and physically committing
- the exact landing site is a player choice with practical consequences
- the station is damaged, stressed, and only partially controlled
- the chosen access route matters
- the first armed humans encountered are capable of talking, not just fighting
- the official story is already beginning to break down

Act 2 ends once the team has:

- landed and moved from the Pilgrim to the station
- gained entry through a chosen access route
- made first contact with the **Purists**
- found the first meaningful evidence that the scientists are hiding something
- and gained a route or channel toward the research survivors

---

## Transfer to the Pilgrim

Once the insertion decision is made, the team leaves the **Kestrel** and transfers into the **Pilgrim-class lander**.

The change in atmosphere should be immediate. The Kestrel is narrow, clean, and controlled. The Pilgrim is denser, harsher, and more mechanical. It is not built for comfort. It is built to descend, land, hold together, and lift off again under load.

Inside, the compartment is arranged for deployment rather than transport. Restraint frames, crash couches, gear tie-downs, sealed med packs, breaching tools, field cases, and suit brackets fill most of the usable volume. Marines strap in, lock gear down, seal helmets or prepare to seal them, and reduce themselves to procedure.

There is no speech unless the table wants one. This is a short, practiced transition from patrol craft to landing vehicle.

---

## Read-Aloud: Separation and Descent

> The Pilgrim separates with a dull mechanical jolt that travels through the frame instead of the air.
> 
> For a few seconds the motion is almost delicate — attitude corrections, alignment burns, the careful clearing of the Kestrel’s hull. Then the profile changes.
> 
> The lander commits.
> 
> Restraints pull tight. Vibration deepens. Thruster pulses step through the hull in hard, measured intervals as the Pilgrim drops toward the ice.
> 
> There is no panoramic view. No cinematic plunge. Only instrument glow, transmitted engine force, and the sense that the machine around you is balancing mass, thrust, and terrain in real time.
> 
> The Kestrel is now above you.
> 
> The surface is below.
> 
> And from this point on, the station is your problem.

---

## Choosing the Landing Site

The Pilgrim does **not** have to set down at a fixed default location.

The players may choose where they want to land relative to the outpost, based on what they value most:

- speed
- stealth
- cover
- proximity to a specific access point
- a safer extraction position later
- or simply avoiding the most obvious approach

Make this a practical tactical choice, not a puzzle. The crew of the Kestrel and Pilgrim can advise, but they should not decide for the players.

### What the players know before landing

The Kestrel can provide a limited but useful surface picture:

- the station structure is intact enough for local operations
- the main docking and transfer section is damaged
- there is no meaningful station guidance traffic
- interference remains active
- thermal and power signatures inside the station are intermittent and incomplete
- the surface around the outpost is uneven but manageable for a trained pilot
- the Pilgrim can land near multiple access vectors, but not directly “on top of” every problem at once

### What the choice should affect

The landing site should shape:

- how exposed the team is on the ice
- how long the exterior approach takes
- which access route feels most natural
- what they see first on the outside of the station
- and how much attention their insertion is likely to draw

---

## Suggested Landing Approaches

You do not need a complicated tactical map unless you want one. For play, three broad landing choices are enough.

### 1. Close Landing

The Pilgrim sets down relatively near the damaged main approach side of the station.

This is the fastest route to action. It gives the team a short exterior movement and the quickest access to the most obvious entry points, but also makes them easier to detect and gives them less time to observe before committing.

Good for:

- rapid intervention
- direct pressure
- players who want to get inside immediately

Risks:

- more visible insertion
- less time to assess the hull
- easier for stressed defenders inside to interpret the team as an immediate assault force

### 2. Offset Landing

The Pilgrim lands at a moderate distance from the station, off the main approach line.

This is usually the best balanced choice. It allows the team to approach under some cover from terrain, hull angle, or station geometry, while still keeping the insertion manageable.

Good for:

- mixed caution and speed
- teams that want options
- a more deliberate exterior approach

Risks:

- slightly longer movement on the surface
- more time exposed outside
- players must choose their entry point more consciously

### 3. Distant or Concealed Landing

The Pilgrim lands farther away, using terrain or line-of-sight reduction to keep the lander itself less obvious.

This gives the players the most control over how they approach the station, but increases travel time, surface exposure, and the chance of minor EVA complications before entry even begins.

Good for:

- cautious teams
- stealth-minded approach
- players worried about extraction security or hostile observation

Risks:

- slower insertion
- longer surface movement in EVA conditions
- more opportunity for navigation errors, line-of-sight problems, or minor suit/equipment complications

---

## Touchdown

Wherever the players choose to land, the touchdown should feel controlled but heavy.

### Read-Aloud: Touchdown

> Final descent comes in pulses.
> 
> The engine tone shifts. Corrections shorten. The vibration changes from continuous force to a sequence of hard controlled adjustments as the Pilgrim trims velocity against the ice below.
> 
> Then comes touchdown.
> 
> Not a crash. Not a gentle arrival either. A heavy transfer of load runs through the landing frame and into the hull, followed by a brief settling shudder as the lander finds its weight and holds it.
> 
> For a second everything is still.
> 
> Then harness locks release.

After touchdown, the compartment changes immediately from transit mode to deployment mode. Marines unclip, recover weapons and mission gear, confirm suit status, and stack at the hatch. The exterior hatch opens only once the pilot confirms stable contact and acceptable local surface conditions.

Act 2 should now move from **descent tension** into **surface movement and station entry**.

---

## Surface Approach

Before the players reach an airlock, hatch, or breach point, give them a short exterior phase.

This does not need to become a giant EVA sequence unless you want it to. Its purpose is to make the landing site matter and to create a clean transition from vehicle insertion to station entry.

On the surface, the players should notice:

- black sky and hard reflected light off the ice
- the station sitting low and unnatural against the landscape
- damaged external structures around the main transfer area
- silent hull sections with patching, scarring, or emergency sealing
- no welcoming lights, no clear traffic control, no visible orderly activity

Depending on landing site, they may also see:

- tool marks or traffic around a service side
- a less damaged auxiliary access section
- discarded external crates or maintenance equipment
- exterior scarring suggesting selective sabotage rather than random destruction

This is the moment to let them choose how they want to enter.

---

## The Station Layout in Play

The map shown for the station is the **main habitable level**. For Act 2, the most important parts are:

- **Main Transfer Lock (Damaged)** — the most direct official entry point
- **Auxiliary Airlock** — small, secondary EVA access
- **External Service Bay** — cargo, workshop, and technical access
- **Maintenance Conduit / Utility Access** — the tight technical route
- **Central Hub** — the main junction of the station
- **Security Station** — overwatch and internal corridor control
- **Server Core** — data, routing, and system traces
- **Research Vault / Lab Sector / Cryo Containment** — the direction of the surviving scientists

For Act 2, think of the station in three zones:

### Outer Access Zone

- Main Transfer Lock
- Auxiliary Airlock
- External Service Bay
- Surface Operations Lock
- Maintenance Conduit

### Habitation / Common Zone

- Crew Quarters
- Mess Hall
- Central Hub
- Maintenance & Power
- Workshop

### Research / Restricted Zone

- Security Station
- Server Core
- Lab Sector
- Cryo Containment
- Research Vault

The players land outside the station, cross the surface, and enter through the **Outer Access Zone**. They encounter tension and signs of violence in the **Habitation / Common Zone**, and only begin pressing toward the **Research / Restricted Zone** by the end of the act.

---

## First Interior Tone

The first moments inside the station should feel physically different from both the **Kestrel** and the **Pilgrim**:

- worse lighting
- worse air
- damaged systems
- too much silence in some places
- too much machine noise in others

There should be no cinematic welcome.  
No automatic alarm just because the players entered.  
No instant firefight unless the players choose a loud entry or force one.

Instead, the station should feel like a structure under internal stress:

- some systems still working
- some doors responsive
- some doors on manual lock
- emergency shutters in place
- visible signs of patching, sealing, and defensive improvisation

### Read-Aloud: First Interior

> The inside of the station feels wrong immediately.
> 
> Not dead. Not abandoned. Wrong.
> 
> The lights are thin and uneven, some strips running clean, others flickering or dimmed to emergency levels. Air moves through the corridor with a strained mechanical hush, as if life support is working harder than it wants to. Somewhere deeper in the structure, metal ticks as stressed panels shift through temperature gradients and load.
> 
> There is no station voice. No traffic control. No clear sign of who is in charge.
> 
> Just sealed doors, stale air, hot electronics, and the sense that something inside this place is still making decisions.

---

## Access Paths

The players should now choose how they actually gain entry. Their earlier landing site choice should make some routes easier or more natural, but it should not fully remove the others.

Each route should shape:

- pace
- first information
- first danger
- first encounter tone

---

## 1. Main Transfer Lock (Damaged)

This is the most obvious and most official entry point. It was built for normal transfer from landed craft and is still connected to the inner station, but it is visibly damaged and partially compromised.

### What it looks like

- outer transfer structure damaged
- blast scarring and structural distortion
- one or more pressure doors no longer cycling automatically
- emergency lighting active
- visible signs of hasty sealing from the inside

### Good outcomes

- quickest direct access into the station
- immediate route toward **Crew Quarters**, **Security Station**, and the upper corridors around **Central Hub**
- strong first clues that this was not a clean takeover

### Bad outcomes

- noisy entry
- time loss
- local pressure instability
- first encounter begins with more suspicion and weapons already raised

### Best first signs here

- damaged pressure door with manual locking bars added from inside
- one dead security staffer near the inner threshold
- improvised trauma kit or dragged med crate nearby
- signs that people tried to hold this entrance rather than flee through it

### Initial encounter variant

This route is best for an early **corridor standoff**.

The players may encounter:

- **2 Purist Riflemen** covering the upper approach toward **Central Hub**
- or **1 Rifleman + 1 Breacher** repositioning a wounded person from the direction of **Crew Quarters**

This is the most direct route to a classic “Stop. Don’t come any closer.” first contact.

---

## 2. Auxiliary Airlock

This is a smaller, secondary EVA access point on the far side of the station. It is less important than the main lock, less comfortable, and less useful for large movement, but exactly because of that it may be less watched.

### What it looks like

- compact two-stage lock
- narrow internal transition
- backup systems still partly functioning
- less physical damage than the main lock
- lower traffic signs overall

### Good outcomes

- quieter insertion
- fewer immediate signs of panic
- a more controlled entry into the upper-right side of the station
- easier early access toward **Lab Sector** edge corridors without entering the core immediately

### Bad outcomes

- slower entry
- risk of getting turned around in low-traffic sections
- easier to stumble into security shutters or dead-end internal controls
- players may enter closer to the sensitive side of the station than they intended

### Best first signs here

- intact but manually overridden inner control panel
- corridor cameras dead or disabled
- sealed rolling crate or abandoned sample transport unit
- signage indicating restricted routing toward **Lab Sector**

### Initial encounter variant

This route works well for a **high-tension, lower-volume first contact**.

The players may encounter:

- **1 Purist Rifleman + 1 Tech / Saboteur**
- or a lone armed Purist who does not open fire, but immediately tries to warn them away from the research side

This is a good entry if you want more suspicion and less immediate violence.

---

## 3. External Service Bay

This is the cargo, workshop, and service-facing access point. It links naturally into **Workshop**, **Maintenance & Power**, and the left-lower side of the station. It is one of the most practical entry points for a competent military team.

### What it looks like

- service lock and cargo handling area
- technical access framing
- signs of tool use, mechanical wear, and recent heavy movement
- larger internal space than the auxiliary airlock
- easier to breach, but harder to fully secure

### Good outcomes

- good cover
- direct access to **Workshop**, **Maintenance & Power**, and **Utility Access**
- strong environmental storytelling
- early clues without immediate scientist contact

### Bad outcomes

- noise carries through workshop and service corridors
- easier to hit technical dead ends
- if things go bad, the players may trigger a defensive fight in service spaces

### Best first signs here

- workbench turned into a casualty station
- maintenance carts positioned as cover
- dropped tools, not just dropped weapons
- a patched bulkhead or foam-sealed pressure crack
- hand-marked arrows or routing notes toward safer sections

### Initial encounter variant

This is the best route for a **messy, human first contact**.

The players may encounter:

- **2 Purist Riflemen** moving a wounded civilian through **Workshop**
- or **1 Breacher + 1 Rifleman** defending a junction between **Maintenance & Power** and **Utility Access**

This route strongly supports the idea that the Purists are armed but not simply executing everyone.

---

## 4. Maintenance Conduit / Utility Access

This is the hardest, tightest, and most uncomfortable route. It is not a proper corridor but a technical entry path. The team enters through service crawlspace, conduit, or narrow utility structure and emerges near **Utility Access** and the lower middle of the station.

### What it looks like

- cramped access crawlspace
- low clearance
- cable trunks, pipe runs, and maintenance framing
- poor visibility
- confusing routing
- a route designed for technicians, not armed teams

### Good outcomes

- most covert internal access
- players may bypass obvious kill zones
- easier to observe station behavior before being observed
- strong atmosphere and good slow build

### Bad outcomes

- slow
- uncomfortable
- bad for large gear
- easy to arrive disorganized
- easy to emerge somewhere tactically awkward

### Best first signs here

- maintenance graffiti or handwritten technician notes
- emergency bypass tags
- patched cable bundles
- dirty footprints or drag marks
- local heat and ventilation inconsistencies showing which zones are still active

### Initial encounter variant

This route is best for a **quiet, uneasy contact** rather than a firefight.

The players may first hear:

- voices through a vent
- movement in the corridor beyond the utility hatch
- a Purist argument about whether “they’re League or corporate”

Possible first contact:

- **1 Tech / Saboteur + 1 Rifleman**
- or **2 Purists** who are not ready for a fight and may talk first if the team does not force the issue

This is the strongest route if you want to reward caution with ambiguity instead of safety.

---

## Early Interior Signs

Before the players meet anyone face to face, give them signs that the station’s story is messy.

Good early signs:

- a barricaded checkpoint built from station furniture and maintenance crates
- damage that looks defensive rather than random
- a sealed med cabinet emptied in a hurry
- dropped tools, not just dropped weapons
- a dead security staffer and signs that someone tried to treat another casualty nearby
- internal cameras disabled in some sectors but not others
- frozen or partial station map displays showing isolated compartments

These details should suggest:

- this was not a clean raid
- people here fought, improvised, and reacted in layers
- someone wanted control, not just destruction

---

## First Contact with the Purists

This is the key scene of Act 2.

Unless the players are openly reckless or immediately escalate, the first encounter with the Purists should begin as an **armed, fragile standoff** rather than a firefight.

The Purists are under stress. They are armed. Some are frightened. Some are angry. Some are committed enough to die here if necessary. But they are not generic raiders, and they are not eager to waste themselves on an unknown League squad unless forced.

### What the Purists know

They know:

- new arrivals have entered the station
- the arrivals are not corporate cleanup yet
- the situation is slipping
- if they lose narrative control now, they may lose everything

They do **not** know yet:

- whether the players are obedient extractors
- whether they can be reasoned with
- whether they are already compromised by the same systems the station was testing

### How to stage the encounter

Make the players see people first, targets second.

Good setups:

- two armed Purists covering a corridor while trying to move a wounded person
- a defensive position built around a junction leading to **Central Hub**
- one Purist trying to keep another from panicking
- a weapon pointed at the players while someone behind it says “Hold”

### Read-Aloud: First Contact

> Movement ahead.
> 
> Not machine movement. Human. Fast, then stopped.
> 
> A shape appears at the far end of the corridor — suit, weapon, breathing hard. Then another. They are not charging. They are not retreating either.
> 
> One of them raises a hand, not quite open, not quite steady. The weapon stays up.
> 
> “Stop.”
> 
> The word comes through suit comms on a local burst, rough with interference.
> 
> “Don’t come any closer.”

---

## Running the Standoff

If the players do not fire immediately, let the scene breathe.

The first exchange should not answer the mystery. It should only open it.

### What the Purists might say

Use short lines. They are not giving speeches under guns.

Possible lines:

- “You don’t know what they were doing here.”
- “This isn’t a hostage situation.”
- “If you’re here to take them out, you’re already too late.”
- “They shut the station down, not us.”
- “Look for yourself before you decide who to shoot.”

### What they should not do yet

Do not have them explain:

- the entire project
- the full ethical stakes
- the corporate angle
- the complete truth of the interface system

At this stage, their role is to create **doubt**, not clarity.

---

## If the Players Open Fire

If the players shoot first, the Purists fight back hard but not suicidally.

They should:

- use cover
- fall back if needed
- try to split the team
- withdraw toward areas that still matter to them

Even in combat, the players should find signs that this is not a simple extremist nest:

- wounded noncombatants nearby
- defensive med supplies
- data storage devices
- handwritten routing notes
- patched compartments meant to preserve, not destroy

A firefight should not erase the moral complexity of the scene.

---

## If the Players Hold Fire

If the players stay controlled, reward that with access to uncertainty.

The Purists may:

- allow a short exchange
- warn them away from the scientists
- tell them to check the station systems
- insist the research staff are not innocent
- offer no alliance, but no immediate attack either

This is the ideal tone for Act 2.

The players should leave the first contact scene with:

- no certainty
- at least one path forward
- and a growing suspicion that their mission parameters are too simple for the reality inside

---

## Moving Deeper

After first contact, the players should gain access to deeper station areas.

This part of Act 2 is not about combat volume. It is about **evidence, contradiction, and atmosphere**.

Give them 2–4 small scenes, depending on pacing.

### Good intermediate scenes on this map

**1. Security Station**  
A compromised internal control point. Good for:

- corridor camera feeds
- shutter overrides
- proof that internal sectors were manually isolated
- first hints that the lockout was not purely external

**2. Maintenance & Power / Workshop**  
Good for:

- improvised treatment area
- patched life-support or pressure repairs
- signs that people were trying to keep the station alive, not just fight over it

**3. Server Core**  
A strong place for the first real system clue:

- jammer still active
- local routing disruptions
- evidence of hidden access layers
- partial logs that do not match the official story

**4. Central Hub**  
Use as a pressure space, not just a hallway. It is the main decision node of the level and should feel exposed, important, and hard to fully control.

---

## First Clues

Act 2 should contain the first real evidence that something is wrong with the station’s research.

Not proof yet. Evidence.

### Early clue types

- internal memos with restricted access tags
- repeated references to adaptive response parameters
- unusual personnel monitoring logs
- neurological telemetry
- incomplete behavioral reports
- hidden or partially scrubbed system access layers

### What these clues should imply

The players should begin to suspect:

- the research was not harmless
- the station leadership was hiding something
- the Purists might be wrong in method, but not entirely wrong in warning

They still should not know the whole truth.

---

## First Contact with the Research Survivors

Do **not** make this the climax of Act 2, but do let the players reach the threshold of it.

By the late part of the act, they should either:

- reach a secured section containing surviving scientists
- establish communication through a door, partition, or local channel
- or find direct evidence that surviving research staff are clustered in the **Lab Sector / Cryo Containment / Research Vault** side of the station

The scientists should sound relieved, controlled, and urgent.

That contrast matters.

Where the Purists feel strained and human, the scientists should initially sound:

- professional
- rational
- desperate in a cleaner way
- eager to be extracted before too many questions are asked

### First impression lines for the scientists

- “Are you League? Good. We need extraction.”
- “The attackers are still active on the station.”
- “Do not trust what they tell you.”
- “We can explain, but not here.”

These lines should pull the players forward without settling the question.

---

## GM Guidance for Act 2

The players should spend this act moving from:  
**intervention certainty**  
to  
**operational ambiguity**

Do not overplay any one faction yet.

The Purists should not become saints.  
The scientists should not become obvious villains.  
The station itself should still feel like a real workplace under catastrophic internal stress, not a horror maze.

Keep the pressure low but rising.

Act 2 works best when the players feel:

- armed
- cautious
- responsible
- and increasingly unsure what “mission success” actually means
