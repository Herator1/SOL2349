There is no single perfect ending to **Operation: Kestrel Landing**.

The scenario is built so that different choices produce different kinds of success:

- professional success
    
- moral success
    
- political success
    
- simple survival
    

The players are unlikely to achieve all of them at once.

This section assumes the station is a **surface outpost on [[Enceladus]]**, not an orbital habitat. That matters for extraction, perimeter control, and escalation. Once the players are on the ground, the situation becomes more territorial and more physical. Routes matter. Access points matter. The **Kestrel** can support approach and extraction, but it cannot magically solve the station from above.

---

## The Lawful Course of Action

From the perspective of disciplined [[Jovian League]] intervention, the most lawful and professionally defensible course is not to “pick the right side.”

It is to:

- stop ongoing violence
    
- secure the site
    
- restore communications if possible
    
- detain armed actors as necessary
    
- secure key personnel
    
- preserve evidence
    
- prevent unauthorized removal or destruction of research
    
- hand the incident upward once competent authority is restored
    

In other words, the cleanest official answer is:

**secure everyone, secure the evidence, and let no faction walk away with full control.**

This is also the hardest path.

The players are undermanned, communications are compromised, the station is in crisis, and a corporate recovery element is already converging on the scene. That tension is intentional. The lawful answer exists, but it is not easy.

---

## Running the Ending

By the end of Act 3, the players should no longer be able to remain neutral through inaction. They do not need to declare an ideology, but they do need to determine what survives the incident:

- the scientists
    
- the research
    
- the evidence
    
- the Purists
    
- the corporate version of events
    
- or some unstable combination of the above
    

The best endings are not defined by body count.

They are defined by:

- who leaves
    
- what data survives
    
- who controls the story
    
- how the League will interpret the mission afterward
    

---

## Outcome 1 — Clean Containment

This is the closest thing to the ideal professional ending.

The players secure the site as best they can, prevent either side from leaving with full control, preserve the most important evidence, and deny the corporate team an easy takeover. Surviving scientists are extracted or detained under guard. Surviving Purists are subdued, detained, or forced to surrender. The research core is secured, not casually handed over. The players restore communications or physically leave with enough evidence to prevent the incident from disappearing into administrative fog.

This is the most lawful outcome from a League perspective.

### Consequences

- the players are likely respected by formal command
    
- the corporate team is frustrated or openly hostile
    
- neither the scientists nor the Purists are satisfied
    
- the truth survives, but only because the players fought to preserve it
    

### Rewards

- strongest professional reputation gain
    
- quiet commendation or increased trust from League channels
    
- future access to better mission support, equipment, or contacts
    
- preserved data, logs, or witness testimony as campaign assets
    
- one or more detainees or survivors who may become future informants, liabilities, or political pressure points
    

### Cost

- tactically the hardest ending
    
- likely confrontation with corporate recovery
    
- high stress, high complexity, no grateful applause
    

---

## Outcome 2 — Scientist Extraction

The players prioritize the scientists and allow at least part of the research to leave the station intact.

This may happen because they believe the mission still centers on recovering key personnel, because they distrust the Purists, or because they judge the research too valuable to destroy on site. In this outcome, the players effectively treat the crisis as a violent interruption of a legitimate project rather than a warning about the project itself.

### Consequences

- the scientists survive
    
- the Purists are defeated, bypassed, or abandoned
    
- the corporate team may initially cooperate
    
- the research survives in whole or in part
    
- the official version of events becomes easier to control
    

### Rewards

- strong short-term mission-success reading in formal channels
    
- cleaner debrief
    
- possible goodwill, favors, or logistical access through [[Nyx Data Systems]]-aligned structures
    
- intact or partially intact research packages as future story material
    

### Cost

- the players may later learn they helped preserve something dangerous
    
- the truth may be buried or reframed
    
- Purist survivors may become future enemies or witnesses against them
    
- the corporate side gains leverage over the incident
    

---

## Outcome 3 — Purist-Aligned Shutdown

The players decide the system is too dangerous to survive and help the Purists destroy, disable, or irreparably compromise it while preserving at least some proof of what was happening on the station.

This does not mean the players have to become ideological allies of the Purists. It only means they conclude that the research cannot be allowed to leave intact under scientist or corporate control.

### Consequences

- the core system is sabotaged, destroyed, or rendered unusable
    
- the scientists are prevented from leaving with full control
    
- the corporate team becomes openly hostile
    
- at least some evidence survives as proof
    

### Rewards

- strongest moral victory if the players believe the system must not spread
    
- preserved truth in the form of data fragments, logs, or testimony
    
- possible future contacts among anti-corporate, body-rights, or civil-liberties actors
    
- highly usable campaign hooks tied to what was saved before the shutdown
    

### Cost

- weak official justification unless the players preserved excellent evidence
    
- likely disciplinary or political complications
    
- possible loss of key personnel
    
- direct long-term hostility from corporate interests
    

---

## Outcome 4 — Corporate Handoff

The players stand down, cooperate fully with the incoming corporate team, or fail to stop them from taking control of the station and its contents.

In the moment, this may look like the most procedurally stable option. More personnel arrive. Corporate ownership is real. The scientists may accept the handoff. Violence may briefly decrease. On paper, it can look like the incident is being managed.

This is the cleanest outcome administratively.

It is not the cleanest outcome morally.

### Consequences

- the corporation controls the site
    
- evidence is filtered, buried, or erased
    
- scientists become protected assets, disappeared assets, or managed witnesses
    
- Purists are neutralized and framed as the full story
    
- the players lose control of the truth
    

### Rewards

- simple paperwork
    
- minimal immediate fallout
    
- an apparently resolved mission
    
- possible short-term relief from command that the situation did not become a visible multi-party disaster
    

### Cost

- the deepest moral compromise
    
- the truth vanishes behind corporate narrative control
    
- the players may later discover how much was hidden
    
- they become participants in containment whether they intended to or not
    

This is a very strong bitter ending.

---

## Outcome 5 — Partial Collapse / Scrambled Exit

This is the messiest and often the most believable ending.

The station crisis spirals under pressure. The players get some people out, lose others, preserve fragments rather than complete truth, and escape under worsening conditions without fully controlling the outcome. No one gets everything they wanted. No one even leaves with a clean story.

This outcome is especially good if you want long-term campaign consequences instead of a neat moral conclusion.

### Consequences

- incomplete evacuation
    
- incomplete evidence
    
- multiple factions suffer losses
    
- the final official account remains contested
    
- survivors from several sides carry their own incompatible versions forward
    

### Rewards

- rich narrative fallout
    
- damaged but valuable data fragments
    
- unresolved investigations
    
- future witnesses, enemies, and reluctant allies
    
- campaign hooks in every direction
    

### Cost

- no clean victory
    
- uncertain debrief
    
- ongoing pressure from League, corporate, and ideological actors
    
- the players may never know the full extent of what was lost

## Reward Philosophy

Rewards in **Kestrel Landing** should not be based mainly on kills or loot. They should reflect the values of [[SOL 2349]]:

- survival matters
    
- professionalism matters
    
- evidence matters
    
- future leverage matters
    

The strongest rewards are often not physical items. They are:

- trust
    
- access
    
- secrets
    
- testimony
    
- proof
    
- political insulation
    
- future favors
    
- future enemies who now know the players matter
    

---

## Reward Categories

### Operational Rewards

These reflect how competent the team looked under pressure.

Examples:

- improved standing with League command
    
- access to better gear on later missions
    
- greater trust from military or patrol contacts
    
- priority support in future naval or response scenarios
    

### Information Rewards

These are often the most valuable rewards in the scenario.

Examples:

- copied research fragments
    
- jammer logs
    
- station command traces
    
- evidence of hidden modulation architecture
    
- corporate comm intercepts
    
- names of shell companies, subsidiaries, or compartmentalized project structures
    

### Relationship Rewards

These depend on who survives and how the players acted.

Examples:

- a surviving scientist owes the team a debt
    
- a Purist insider trusts them enough to talk again
    
- the **Kestrel** crew becomes personally loyal
    
- corporate actors mark them as useful, dangerous, or unreliable
    

### Material Rewards

Keep these limited and believable.

Examples:

- specialized med supplies
    
- advanced sensor packages
    
- hardened field electronics
    
- data-storage modules
    
- prototype signal scramblers or interface blockers
    
- compact corporate security gear recovered from the cleanup team
    

---

## Best Official Ending

From a formal League perspective, the best outcomes are usually:

- **Clean Containment**
    
- **Scientist Extraction**
    

These are easiest to defend in a structured debrief.

---

## Best Moral Ending

From a more human or ethical point of view, the strongest outcomes are usually:

- **Purist-Aligned Shutdown**
    
- **Clean Containment**
    

These preserve the greatest chance that the truth survives without simply being handed to the strongest institution.

---

## Best Campaign Ending

For long-term play, the richest outcomes are usually:

- **Partial Collapse / Scrambled Exit**
    
- **Clean Containment**
    

These leave enough unresolved to matter later.

---

## Hardest but Most Admirable Ending

If you want one hidden GM benchmark for “the best the marines could realistically do,” it is:

**Clean Containment.**

That is the ending where the players behave most like disciplined intervention professionals rather than accidental partisans or passive witnesses.

It is also the ending most likely to cost them politically.

---

## Final GM Advice

Do not reward the players only for being right.

Reward them for:

- preserving life where possible
    
- keeping evidence intact
    
- refusing easy lies
    
- acting professionally under uncertainty
    
- understanding that control of the story is part of the battlefield
    

The incident on Enceladus is not only about who survives the station.

It is about who gets to define what happened there.
