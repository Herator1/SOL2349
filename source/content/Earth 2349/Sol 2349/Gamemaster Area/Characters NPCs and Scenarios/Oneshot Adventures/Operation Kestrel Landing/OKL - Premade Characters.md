The following five characters are designed as the player-facing marine team for **Operation: Kestrel Landing**. They are not generic grunts. Each fills a clear operational role inside a small [[Jovian League]] rapid-response unit and gives the players a distinct perspective on the station crisis.

They should feel highly trained, professional, and capable — but still human. These are not super-soldiers. They are a compact intervention team operating with limited numbers, limited certainty, and no guarantee of reinforcement.

## Notes

Talia is built as a **pressure commander**, not a frontline duelist. She is strongest when she can:

- keep the squad coordinated with **Command Uplink**
- push tense conversations with **Persuasion Suite**
- manipulate doors, locks, and local systems with **Systems Interface Node**
- stay viable in a firefight through solid Reflexes, Fitness, and Firearms
- maintain authority when the mission starts falling apart

Talia Serrin is the officer in command of the Kestrel landing team. She is disciplined, composed, and used to making decisions before the full picture is available. Her background is in station security response, boarding control, and small-unit crisis intervention. She is at her best when a situation is unstable but not yet fully broken.

Serrin is not charismatic in a warm sense. She leads through clarity, control, and competence. She does not waste words, and she does not give emotional reassurance she does not believe in. Her strength is that she can keep a team functioning under uncertainty. Her weakness is that she tends to hold responsibility too tightly, especially when the mission becomes morally unclear.

She is an excellent choice for players who want to steer the group, manage pressure, and decide what “professional conduct” really means once the official story begins to fall apart.

**Squad function:** decision-maker, tactical coordinator, mission authority  
**Typical gear profile:** command suit package, rifle, tactical uplink, compact sidearm  
**Player hook:** Do you stay inside the mission, or redefine it when the mission itself stops making sense?

## Notes

Naima is built as a **frontline combat medic**. She is strongest when she can:

- stabilize allies under fire
    
- keep the squad functional through attrition
    
- handle survival and environmental pressure
    
- stay combat-capable without becoming a pure gunfighter
    
- make hard triage decisions when there is not enough time to save everyone

Naima Rourke came into military service through emergency medicine rather than pure combat ambition. Before entering League service, she worked in civilian trauma response around industrial stations and transport nodes, where pressure loss, mechanical injury, and delayed evacuation were part of normal life. She has seen enough preventable death to distrust anyone who speaks too easily about acceptable losses.

Rourke is practical, observant, and emotionally harder to shake than most people first assume. She is not soft, but she is the one most likely to ask whether the team is still protecting people or merely following procedure. She tends to notice pain, hesitation, and fear before anyone else does, which makes her one of the first characters likely to sense that the station crisis is not as simple as command framed it.

She is an excellent choice for players who want to be useful under pressure while also having a strong human angle on the mission.

**Squad function:** stabilization, casualty management, survival under attrition  
**Typical gear profile:** med harness, compact carbine, trauma kit, stims, seal patches  
**Player hook:** How far do you go to save lives when the people you save may carry the problem with them?

### 3. Corporal Dorian Valek — Heavy Gunner

**Role:** Fire Support / Area Denial / Defensive Anchor  
**Rank:** Corporal  
**Origin:** Industrial settlement network on [[Rhea]]

Dorian Valek grew up in a harsh, equipment-heavy environment where machine discipline and personal endurance mattered more than style or rhetoric. He is physically imposing, but his real strength is steadiness. Under fire, he becomes calmer, not louder. When the rest of the room starts to fray, he narrows his world to sectors, angles, and timing.

Valek is loyal, highly dependable, and less politically reflective than some of the others — at least at first. He trusts systems because systems usually keep people alive. That also means he can be slow to question larger institutional assumptions until the evidence becomes impossible to ignore. Once he does start doubting something, however, he tends not to stop halfway.

He is a good choice for players who want to hold ground, protect the team, and embody the physical seriousness of the operation.

**Squad function:** suppression, corridor control, fallback firepower  
**Typical gear profile:** support weapon, heavier armor, backup sidearm, defensive utility gear  
**Player hook:** When the chain of command and the facts begin to diverge, what still counts as loyalty?

### 4. Sergeant Mirek Arden — Breach Specialist

**Role:** Entry / Close Assault / Controlled Force  
**Rank:** Sergeant  
**Origin:** Dockside security and boarding environment in the inner Jovian system

Mirek Arden is the first-through-the-door type, but not because he is reckless. He is trained to solve physical problems quickly: sealed doors, hostile chokepoints, compartment transitions, and moments where hesitation costs more than aggression. He has experience in boarding actions, close-quarters clearing, and low-visibility station fighting.

Arden is blunt, dry, and often sounds more cynical than he actually is. He is not stupid and not bloodthirsty, but he is deeply suspicious of any situation that keeps asking armed professionals to wait just a little longer before acting. He is likely to be one of the first characters to push the team toward decisive intervention, especially if the station starts feeling like a trap.

He is a strong choice for players who want tempo, pressure, and direct action without giving up realism.

**Squad function:** entry lead, close-quarters pressure, breaching and shock response  
**Typical gear profile:** boarding shotgun, breaching charges, cutter tools, reinforced suit package  
**Player hook:** Is speed the only honest answer in a crisis, or just the easiest one to justify afterward?

### 5. Specialist Yara Quist — Recon Operator

**Role:** Recon / Technical Intrusion / Situational Awareness  
**Rank:** Specialist  
**Origin:** research-support habitat ring in the Saturn system

Yara Quist was selected into League service because of her unusual mix of stealth discipline, sensor literacy, and technical patience. She is comfortable in maintenance spaces, partial system maps, and the kind of half-readable data environment most people hate. She notices patterns quickly and trusts anomalies more than official summaries.

Quist is quiet, analytical, and habitually cautious, but not passive. She is often the first to suspect that a surface explanation is incomplete. On Kestrel Landing, she is especially valuable because the station itself is part of the mystery: locks, routes, logs, hidden access layers, and the difference between what the outpost says it is and what it was actually doing.

She is the best choice for players who want to pull truth out of the environment before anyone else can erase it.

**Squad function:** route finding, early warning, system access, evidence recovery  
**Typical gear profile:** compact carbine, recon drone, sensor tools, intrusion kit, light tactical armor  
**Player hook:** When every system on the station is lying by omission, what do you trust first — data, people, or instinct?

## Team Dynamic

This team works best if the players lean into the natural tension between roles:

- **Serrin** pushes for mission coherence
    
- **Rourke** pushes for human cost to stay visible
    
- **Valek** anchors the team physically and tactically
    
- **Arden** pushes momentum and decisive action
    
- **Quist** pushes investigation, caution, and uncomfortable questions
    

That mix is intentional.  
**Operation: Kestrel Landing** works best when the squad is competent enough to survive, but divided enough to care about what survival means.

---

## GM Note

These characters should all feel like believable members of the same small professional response team. They are different in tone and outlook, but none of them should feel out of place on the **Kestrel**.

The scenario becomes stronger if each player quickly understands not only what their character is good at, but what that character is likely to care about once the mission stops being straightforward.
