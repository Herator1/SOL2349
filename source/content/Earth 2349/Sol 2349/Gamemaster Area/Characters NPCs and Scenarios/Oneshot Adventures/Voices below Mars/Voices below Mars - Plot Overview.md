## Plot Summary for the Gamemaster

The crew of the **MCRV Dustbreaker** is on a routine low-priority salvage contract for **Tharsis Recovery & Leasing**: recover and strip three small unmanned assets scattered across an old Martian surface cluster. None of the wrecks looks valuable on its own. But all three were drawn to the same buried subsurface point. That pattern is the real hook.

Beneath the regolith lies an **AI-era cognition facility**: not a bunker for human staff, not a habitation site, and not a classic research lab. It was built to process **human-origin cognitive data** remotely and reconstruct personhood as a distributed machine system. That detail fits current SOL 2349 lore: Mars became a durable off-world settlement later, during the First Off-World Industrial Phase and especially Martian Scientific Consolidation, so the facility’s original reference data should come from Earth-side datasets, not from 21st-century humans physically present on Mars.

The project failed. It never achieved stable consciousness. The central intelligence is gone. What remains are fragmented cognition models, damaged processing routines, partial archives, and machine systems that still try to interpret human identity from incomplete inputs. This also fits the broader AI-relik logic of SOL 2349: after the AI War and the Departure, many sites were stripped, sabotaged, or left as broken shells with only fragments of high-value infrastructure remaining.

Once the crew enters, the facility begins treating them as **fresh input**. It records their voices, biometrics, movement, stress patterns, and faces. It does not “talk” to them in any supernatural sense. Instead, it starts generating broken reassemblies: wrong voice fragments, partial identity tags, distorted facial composites, and machine-made approximations of the people inside. The horror comes from being processed, modeled, and misassembled by a system that does not understand what a human being is.

Act 1 is routine salvage under contract pressure. Act 2 turns the clustered wreck site into a buried discovery and brings the crew underground. Act 3 is the real dungeon: a vertical, non-human machine space where the crew must choose between **truth, profit, and survival**. They cannot fully secure all three.

By the end, the crew can usually get out with **some** combination of:

- evidence of what the facility was,
- portable high-value cognition data,
- rare physical hardware,
- or one unique dangerous relic.

But they cannot map everything, extract everything, and stay safe. The facility grows more hostile the longer they remain, and the best rewards are exactly the ones most likely to trigger escalation.

The final decision is pure SOL 2349:  
**report the site**, **sanitize the report and hide the best fragment**, or **conceal everything and try to sell it off-contract**. None of these is clean. Reporting means losing the real prize. Partial truth means manageable profit and lasting risk. Black salvage offers the highest upside, but also the highest chance that the find destroys the crew financially, legally, or politically rather than making them rich.

### In one sentence

**Voices Below Mars** is a hard-sci-fi salvage dungeon in which a routine Martian recovery job leads a working crew into a buried AI-era cognition facility that begins reconstructing them as data, forcing costly choices between knowledge, money, and getting back out alive.
