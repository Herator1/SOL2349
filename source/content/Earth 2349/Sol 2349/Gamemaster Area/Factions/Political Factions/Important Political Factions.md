The political order of **SOL 2349** is not defined by a single interplanetary government, nor by a clean system of sovereign nation-states extended into space. Power is distributed unevenly across worlds, megacities, habitats, stations, transport corridors, regional alliances, and corporate systems. Some of that power is formal. Much of it is practical. In many parts of human space, the actors that matter most are not those with the strongest legal claim, but those able to keep infrastructure working, movement flowing, and local order intact.

This page focuses on the most important **non-corporate or not purely corporate political factions** in the Solar System. It does not attempt to list every government, local administration, or minor movement. Instead, it highlights the kinds of political actors that shape everyday power below the level of the great megacorporations, but above the level of purely local politics.

Together, these factions form much of the political middle layer of human civilization in **2349**: the powers that negotiate, coordinate, obstruct, stabilize, and contest the worlds between formal sovereignty and raw infrastructure.

---

## 1. [[Planetary and Megacity Power Centers]]

These are the major political authorities rooted in planets, megacities, or dominant urban regions. Some function as conventional governments, others more as administrative power centers, regulatory blocs, or governing shells wrapped around much larger economic systems.

Typical examples include megacity governance blocs on **[[Earth]]**, formal planetary institutions on [[Mars]], and the limited but still meaningful official structures that operate within the corporate and syndicate-dominated world of [[Venus]].

---

## 2. [[Habitat and Station Federations]]

Not all important political actors are tied to planets. Many emerge from constellations of stations, habitats, docks, and orbital settlements that have learned to coordinate for survival, bargaining power, maintenance, or mutual defense.

These include cislunar habitat leagues, Belt station federations, and independent orbital compacts—alliances shaped less by territory in the traditional sense than by pressure integrity, route access, and shared infrastructural dependence.

---

## 3. [[Transit and Corridor Authorities]]

In a civilization built on slow movement and fragile logistics, control over routes can become a form of political power. Some factions derive their importance not from large populations or strong cultural identity, but from their ability to regulate access, coordinate traffic, arbitrate transport disputes, and stabilize critical corridors.

This category includes corridor councils, docking compacts, freight arbitration bodies, and other institutions that govern the movement of cargo, people, and strategic mass between worlds.

---

## 4. [[Regional Blocs and Political Alliances]]

Some parts of the Solar System have developed larger regional formations able to coordinate diplomacy, infrastructure, security, and long-range political interests across multiple worlds or settlement clusters. These blocs are usually looser than traditional states, but more durable than temporary coalitions.

The clearest example is the **[[Jovian League]]**, but similar patterns may also appear in outer-system coordination structures or broader Belt-aligned political networks.

---

## 5. [[Civic and Counter-Corporate Movements]]

Not all important political factions are governments or regional alliances. Some exist as civic coalitions, public-interest blocs, anti-corporate networks, labor-rights campaigns, infrastructure-rights movements, or loosely aligned social forces pushing back against the growing power of corporate and technocratic systems.

These groups vary enormously in legitimacy, reach, and effectiveness, but they remain politically important because they shape public pressure, mobilization, resistance, reform, and the contested idea of who human space is supposed to serve.

---

This structure is intended to map the major types of political actors that shape the Solar System beyond the megacorporations themselves. Some wield authority through law, some through infrastructure, some through coordination, and some through public legitimacy. Together, they help define the fragmented but functioning political order of **2349**.
