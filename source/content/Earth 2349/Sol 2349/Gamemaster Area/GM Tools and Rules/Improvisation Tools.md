This page is for moments when you need an answer fast.

Use it when the players go off-plan, when a scene needs pressure, when you need a complication in ten seconds, or when a location, NPC, or job suddenly becomes more important than expected.

## The Fast Scene Builder

If you need a scene quickly, define four things:

- **place**
- **pressure**
- **people**
- **problem**

That is usually enough.

Example:  
A **fuel transfer hub**, under **time pressure**, with **a nervous dock supervisor and armed contractors**, dealing with **a missing cargo pallet that should never have gone missing**.

## The SOL 2349 Situation Formula

A very reliable default structure is:

**someone wants something**  
**something is stopping them**  
**something is getting worse**

That gives you a scene with motive, friction, and movement.

Example:  
A station engineer wants to restart a cooling loop.  
A safety lockout and missing access codes are blocking it.  
Meanwhile, internal temperatures keep rising.

## The Escalation Ladder

If a scene feels flat, escalate it in stages:

1. inconvenience
2. pressure
3. danger
4. crisis

This is loosely inspired by online GM tools that use progressive danger and clocks to make pressure visible over time.

In _SOL 2349_, a simple version works well:

- **inconvenience** — delay, confusion, bad access
- **pressure** — time loss, attention, worsening conditions
- **danger** — injury risk, legal exposure, system instability
- **crisis** — death, breach, collapse, armed escalation

## The Four Best Complications

When you need a complication, default to one of these:

- **it takes longer**
- **it works imperfectly**
- **it creates exposure**
- **it shifts the problem elsewhere**

These fit very well with consequence-forward GMing, where failed or partial outcomes change the situation instead of simply ending progress.

## The NPC Quick Tool

If you need an NPC fast, define:

- **role**
- **visible trait**
- **immediate goal**
- **what they are afraid of**

Example:  
Dock auditor.  
Tired, precise, underdressed for authority.  
Needs the inspection signed today.  
Afraid the players will notice the numbers were altered.

That is enough to roleplay them.

## The Location Quick Tool

If you need a location fast, define:

- **what it is for**
- **what is wrong with it**
- **what makes it dangerous**
- **what makes it useful**

Example:  
Small salvage processing bay.  
Half the lights are dead and the inventory is wrong.  
Low-grade radiation pockets in one sealed section.  
It has an old cutter rig the crew badly needs.

## The Job Generator Shortcut

If players ask, “So what jobs are available?” build a contract from four parts:

- **client**
- **task**
- **hidden problem**
- **why it matters now**

Example:  
A freight broker.  
Move sealed cargo to a Belt waystation.  
Someone else is already looking for the same shipment.  
The station will lock down in six hours.

## The Mystery Tool

If you need a mystery hook, define:

- **the visible explanation**
- **why it feels wrong**
- **what evidence is missing**
- **what the players may wrongly suspect**

This works especially well in _SOL 2349_, where uncertainty is more valuable than instant explanation.

## The Hazard Tool

For any hazard, answer three questions:

- **what does it damage first?**
- **how do people notice it?**
- **what happens if no one acts?**

Example:  
Coolant leak.  
It first damages system performance.  
People notice rising heat and condensation in the wrong places.  
If ignored, it becomes a reactor-side shutdown crisis.

## The “And / But” Shortcut

When you need a fast outcome, think in terms of:

- **Yes**
- **Yes, but**
- **No, but**
- **No, and**

This kind of consequence-based branching is common in modern improvisation-friendly GM advice and is useful because it keeps scenes moving without demanding binary pass/fail logic.

Example:  
Can they force the hatch?

- **Yes** — it opens.
- **Yes, but** — it opens and triggers a local alert.
- **No, but** — it stays shut, though they find a maintenance bypass.
- **No, and** — the tool snaps and the lock jams.

## The Best GM Questions

When you are stuck, ask:

- What does this place actually do?
- Who wants something right now?
- What is already going wrong?
- What gets worse if the players wait?
- What detail makes this feel like _SOL 2349_ instead of generic sci-fi?

That last question is often the most important.

## A Good Default for SOL 2349

When improvising in this setting, your safest default is:

- make it local
- make it practical
- make it pressured
- make it incomplete

That usually produces something useful.

## In One Sentence

Good improvisation in _SOL 2349_ comes from motives, pressure, complications, and concrete places — not from perfect planning.
