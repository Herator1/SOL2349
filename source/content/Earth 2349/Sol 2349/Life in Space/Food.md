# Food and Cuisine in the Solar System

Food in the Solar System of 2349 reflects the realities of living beyond Earth. While early space colonization relied heavily on ration packs and basic synthetic nutrition, centuries of technological development have dramatically expanded the range and quality of available food.

Modern habitats and colonies rely on a combination of **synthetic food production, hydroponic agriculture, and cultured biological materials**. Many staple foods can now be produced locally in orbital habitats or planetary settlements using closed-loop ecological systems.

Despite these advances, traditional terrestrial foods—often referred to as **“Old Food”**—remain highly prized. These foods require large amounts of land, water, and biological infrastructure that exist primarily on Earth. As a result, they are often rare and expensive in space, transported as luxury goods to wealthy habitats and corporate enclaves.

Food culture across the Solar System therefore ranges from high-end imported delicacies to highly efficient synthetic nutrition designed for long-duration space travel.

# Food Tiers

## 1. Luxury Food (“Old Food”)

|Category|Information|
|---|---|
|Production|Primarily Earth-based agriculture|
|Availability|Rare outside Earth and major inner-system habitats|
|Price|Extremely expensive|
|Cultural Status|Prestige and luxury goods|

“Old Food” refers to traditional agricultural products produced through conventional farming on Earth. These foods require natural ecosystems, large land areas, and extensive biological diversity that are difficult to replicate in artificial environments.

Examples include:

- traditional cheeses
- wines and aged spirits
- meat from livestock
- fresh fruit grown in soil
- natural honey and dairy products

Such foods are commonly served in **high-end restaurants, corporate events, and diplomatic gatherings**. For many spacers, eating authentic Earth-grown food is considered a rare and memorable experience.

## 2. Standard Food (“New Food”)

|Category|Information|
|---|---|
|Production|Hydroponic agriculture, cellular agriculture|
|Availability|Common in most major habitats|
|Price|Moderate|
|Cultural Status|Everyday high-quality food|

Standard food across the Solar System is often referred to as **“New Food.”** These products are specifically designed to be produced in space-compatible agricultural systems.

Common production methods include:

- hydroponic farming
- aeroponic cultivation
- cellular agriculture (cultured meat)
- fungal and microbial fermentation

Examples include:

- cultured chicken or beef
- hydroponic vegetables and herbs
- vat-grown seafood
- fungal protein dishes
- fresh greens from habitat gardens

New Food provides high nutritional value and culinary diversity while remaining practical for large-scale production in space habitats.

## 3. Spacer Food

|Category|Information|
|---|---|
|Production|Synthetic protein production, microbial fermentation|
|Availability|Very common in outer-system habitats and ships|
|Price|Affordable|
|Cultural Status|Functional everyday nutrition|

Spacer Food is designed for **long-duration space travel and high-efficiency production**. It relies heavily on protein synthesis from algae, fungi, microbial cultures, and processed nutrient bases.

These foods are often strongly flavored or heavily seasoned. Lower atmospheric pressure in many habitats reduces the sensitivity of taste and smell, leading to the widespread use of intense spices and sauces.

Typical spacer foods include:

- protein noodles
- algae-based protein bars
- fermented fungal stews
- thick nutrient sauces
- modular meal packs

While inexpensive and widely available, spacer cuisine has developed its own culinary traditions, particularly among long-haul freighter crews and asteroid miners.

## 4. Nutrient Pastes (“Gravies”)

|Category|Information|
|---|---|
|Production|Fully synthetic nutrient processing|
|Availability|Universal|
|Price|Very cheap|
|Cultural Status|Emergency or subsistence food|

Nutrient pastes—often referred to as **“gravies”** by spacers—represent the most basic form of food available in the Solar System. These highly processed mixtures contain complete nutritional profiles but are generally considered unpleasant to consume.

Gravies are commonly distributed in dehydrated powder form and can be rehydrated with water to produce thick nutrient slurries.

Although rarely eaten voluntarily in wealthier habitats, they remain common in:

- deep space missions
- outer system mining stations
- emergency rations
- low-income habitats

Among spacers, gravies are sometimes jokingly described as food that **“keeps you alive, but makes you regret ever leaving Earth’s atmosphere.”**

### Drinks and Alcohol

Beverages in the Solar System have undergone a transformation similar to food production. While traditional alcoholic drinks such as wine, whiskey, or beer are still produced on Earth, their export beyond the planet is extremely limited due to cost, transport constraints, and strict safety regulations aboard spacecraft and habitats.

As a result, an entire industry has emerged dedicated to **recreating alcoholic beverages through synthetic chemistry and controlled fermentation processes**. Early attempts focused on replicating traditional Earth drinks as accurately as possible. Over time, however, the exact taste profiles of many historical beverages became increasingly uncertain, particularly among populations born in space. This eventually led to the creation of entirely new categories of alcohol designed specifically for low-gravity environments and closed ecological systems.

Despite these developments, **alcohol consumption in space is generally lower than on Earth**. Operating spacecraft, maintaining life-support systems, and working in hazardous industrial environments require constant alertness. Many stations enforce strict penalties for intoxication while on duty, and professional crews often avoid alcohol entirely during long missions.

Instead, many spacers prefer **synthetic coffee and tea substitutes**, which are widely produced in orbital habitats. These beverages are typically enriched with caffeine or other mild, legally permitted stimulants that help maintain concentration without impairing performance. Authentic Earth-grown coffee or tea remains extremely rare and expensive, often reserved for luxury markets or special occasions.
