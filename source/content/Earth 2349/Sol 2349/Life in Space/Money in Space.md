# Currency and Economy in Space
Economic activity across the solar system operates within a complex system of local currencies, corporate credit systems, and digital payment networks. Despite this diversity, most interplanetary trade ultimately relies on a widely accepted standard currency known as the **Solar Credit**, commonly abbreviated as **SOL**. While many planets, stations, and corporations maintain their own internal financial systems, the Solar Credit functions as the **primary spacer currency** used for large transactions, interplanetary trade, and long-distance contracts.


# Solar Credits (SOL)
The **Solar Credit** is considered the closest thing to a universal currency in human space.
SOL is used for:
- interplanetary cargo contracts
- spacecraft transactions
- corporate logistics payments
- high-value technological goods
- spacer wages and mercenary contracts

The currency is maintained through a distributed financial network supported by several major economic institutions across the solar system. Because it is widely trusted and relatively stable, SOL is commonly used by spacers, traders, and corporations operating between planetary economies. Although the currency itself is stable, **its purchasing power varies dramatically depending on location**.


# Local Currencies
Most planets and large habitats operate their own internal economic systems. Examples include:
- planetary government currencies
- corporate credit systems
- station-issued work tokens
- various cryptocurrencies used by Belter communities

These local currencies are often optimized for regional economies and daily transactions. However, they typically have limited use outside their home systems. For this reason, long-distance traders and independent crews generally prefer to operate using **Solar Credits**.

# Regional Price Differences
One of the defining features of the interplanetary economy is the enormous variation in prices between different regions of the solar system. Because transporting mass through space requires enormous energy, **location strongly influences the cost of goods**.

Materials are usually cheapest near their point of extraction. Examples include:

- **Helium-3** is relatively cheap in the Jovian system where it is harvested from gas giants.
- The same fuel becomes extremely expensive on **Mars or Venus**, where it must be transported across billions of kilometers.

Similarly, heavy metals are inexpensive in the **Asteroid Belt**, while food and biological materials are often cheaper on **Mars**, where large agricultural biospheres operate. In theory, these price differences create opportunities for profitable trade routes. In practice, transportation costs usually limit such arbitrage.

# Delta-V Economics
In space commerce, **delta-v equals money**. Delta-v refers to the amount of velocity change required to move a spacecraft between locations. Because spacecraft must carry reaction mass and fuel, every maneuver consumes valuable resources. As a result, the cost of transporting cargo depends heavily on orbital mechanics and fuel efficiency. Large cargo transports carefully plan trajectories months in advance to minimize fuel usage. Even small inefficiencies can significantly affect profitability. For many traders, the most valuable skill is not negotiation but **trajectory optimization**.

# Bandwidth and Communication Costs
Another important economic factor is **communication bandwidth**. Data transmission across the solar system is limited by distance, light-speed delays, and infrastructure availability. In the **inner solar system**, high-capacity communication networks allow relatively cheap and reliable data exchange. However, communication becomes increasingly expensive and unreliable further from the Sun.

Challenges include:
- increasing **light lag**
- limited relay infrastructure
- bandwidth prioritization systems

In the outer solar system, high-bandwidth communication links can be extremely expensive, and many settlements operate with limited data connectivity. Because of these limitations, financial transactions and contracts in distant regions often rely on **asynchronous verification systems** and delayed confirmations.

# Economics of the Frontier
These combined factors—transport costs, communication delays, and regional production—create a highly uneven economic landscape. A product that is cheap and common in one region of the solar system may be extremely valuable in another. For traders, explorers, and independent crews, understanding these economic differences can be the key to success.

For many spacers, the solar system is not just a frontier of exploration—but a vast and constantly shifting marketplace shaped by the laws of physics.

Although the Solar Credit is widely used across the solar system, the cost of goods and services varies enormously depending on location, infrastructure, and resource availability.

### Refueling Costs (fusion fuel / reaction mass)
| Location      | Cost (small freighter) |
| ------------- | ---------------------- |
| Jovian system | 5 – 10 SOL             |
| Asteroid Belt | 5 – 40 SOL             |
| Mars / Venus  | 20 – 100 SOL           |

Fuel is significantly cheaper close to its source — especially in the Jovian system, where **helium-3 and reaction mass are harvested at scale**. The further inward it is transported, the more its price increases due to logistics, demand, and infrastructure constraints. These values assume a **small freighter on a standard refueling cycle**. Actual costs can vary heavily depending on burn profile, fuel type (He-3 vs. alternatives), and local availability.

# Economic Reality

For many people in the solar system, a single Solar Credit represents **months of income**. As a result, independent spacecraft crews, explorers, and mercenaries often operate in a high-risk economy where a single successful contract can dramatically change their financial situation. At the same time, the enormous costs associated with spacecraft operation, fuel, and repairs ensure that **even profitable crews rarely remain wealthy for long**.
