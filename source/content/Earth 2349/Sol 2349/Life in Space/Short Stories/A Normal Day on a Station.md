Tomas Adeyemi woke a few minutes before the light strip above his bunk shifted from night-blue to station morning.

That happened often now. He would come awake in the dark and lie still for a moment, listening to the habitat. Ventilation hiss. A pump vibration somewhere in the wall. A door opening down the corridor. Plumbing. A cough. The old ring around Hygiea was cheap by current standards and old enough not to pretend otherwise. It spun just fast enough to provide about **0.3 g**—enough to make life manageable, not enough to make it comfortable. Newer stations in the Belt aimed higher. Better gravity, better retention, better excuses to stay forever. This one had patched bulkheads, narrow corridors, dry recycled air with a faint smell of warm polymer and metal, and just enough comfort to keep people from calling it miserable.

Home, he thought, and immediately disliked the word.

His cabin was small but private, which at his pay grade counted as real success. A bunk, a fold-out desk, a storage wall, a locker, a screen, and two old family photographs he mostly avoided looking at directly. Thirty years earlier he would have called it temporary housing. Back then the plan had been simple: work the Belt for ten years, maybe fifteen at most, make real money, send enough home to Lagos to matter, and go back before the body forgot what a planet felt like.

Then one contract became another.

And another.

He opened the med locker and took out the daily packet. Bone-density support. Muscle retention. Vascular maintenance. Radiation countermeasures. Hormonal balancing for long-term low-g adaptation. A mild mood stabilizer to sand down the edges of bad sleep, station stress, and too many years spent inside sealed systems. It was a good modern regimen—expensive, highly optimized, almost no serious side effects. After long enough, though, even that felt strange. He was no longer sure what his untreated self would look like.

He swallowed the pills with recycled water that tasted faintly of minerals and stainless steel.

Then he washed.

Not a real shower, not before shift. Heated wipes, hygiene mist, no-rinse cleanser, a dry-film razor at the mirror. Water was available, of course. Hygiea traffic was good, and the station was not poor. But there was still a difference between having water and wasting it. In space, wastefulness had a way of making a person look either foolish or soft.

He dressed by habit: station underclothes, work trousers, utility shirt, then the pressure-rated underlayer they wore beneath the dock shell and EVA rig. In theory they were not in suits all day. In practice, dock crews dressed as if an alarm might interrupt any routine. Sometimes it did. Magboots. Gloves clipped to belt. Seal tabs. Patch kit. Personal tools.

The galley at the end of the residential section was already busy with shift overlap. A few people still half asleep, a few already reading work feeds, two techs arguing mildly over a coolant valve incident from the night before. The station’s brief morning imitation of domestic life.

Tomas took a mug of synthetic coffee, black and chemically competent, a bowl of protein flakes softened in cultured oat medium, and two slices of toasted algae bread with yeast spread and pepper oil.

Sima from structural spotted him and came over with her own tray.

“Morning, chief.”

He glanced at the clock. “That depends. Is this still today, or has somebody quietly reassigned us to tomorrow?”

She laughed. “You’re in a better mood than usual.”

“No. I’m in a working mood. It just looks similar from a distance.”

They ate at the wall rail while people moved around them. A logistics supervisor complaining about tow delays. Two younger dockhands talking about a broken game table in the bar. A medic asking if any of the grav-adjust tread rigs had freed up after second shift. None of it important. Which was exactly why it mattered.

By the time he reached the transfer hub, the habitat had fully entered work rhythm. The corridors were denser here: technicians, cargo crews, maintenance staff, admin personnel, all of them moving toward lifts, trams, and hub access points. Near the transfer spine, the station stopped pretending to be residential at all. It became loud, practical, and slightly battered.

The spin transfer lift was another reminder of the ring’s age. Functional. Reliable. Never graceful.

Tomas stepped into the cage with eight others and clipped in from habit more than necessity. As the lift moved toward the station spine, weight softened. His inner ear adjusted a beat late, as always. By the time the hatch opened, the ring’s 0.3 g had fallen away and his body had shifted back into zero.

He still liked that moment.

Zero-g felt cleaner to him than partial gravity. No compromise. No imitation of a planet. Just machinery, momentum, and vacuum telling the truth.

He moved through the spine in practiced pushes, one hand on the guide rails when needed, magboots disengaged until he reached Bay Twelve.

The survey vessel hung in the restraints like a wounded animal under white work lights.

It had once belonged to [[Helios]], which explained why it was still worth saving. Helios survey hulls were not elegant, but they were built to survive more than they had any right to. This one had been towed in from a dead station near one of the quieter Hygiea routes. The salvage notes were incomplete: debris field strike, systems loss, abandonment. No verified fatalities on final record, which usually meant either there had been none or nobody left alive had been in any condition to say otherwise.

The magnetic nozzle had been shredded.

The aft section looked as though a steel storm had passed through it. Radiator vanes torn open. Shield skirts ripped back. The nozzle ring penetrated in multiple places, with at least three superconducting coil segments blown apart hard enough to scar the support truss behind them.

Tomas anchored near the scaffold and brought the local schematic up on his visor.

“All right,” he said over team channel, voice calm, almost conversational. “Nobody gets creative today. Structural first, alignment second, coils after that. If you start improvising, you’re not saving time. You’re just scheduling tomorrow’s problems early.”

A few people laughed. Good.

His team was spread through the bay and aft cavity—twenty-five people over two overlapping shifts, plus three subcontracted coil specialists. Good people. Not always easy people, but competent, which mattered more.

The work settled into rhythm. Tomas did not spend most of the day wrenching on hardware himself unless he had to. His real job was coordination. Checking anchor assignments. Confirming lockout status. Watching sequence and timing. Moving people before a small error became an expensive one.

At one point he caught Eren, one of the newer hires, pushing off too hard with a tool caddy unsecured. He snagged the drifting case with his boot hook before it vanished into the bay frame.

She grimaced. “Sorry. I thought I had it.”

“You nearly did,” he said. “That’s the dangerous version.”

“That is not especially comforting.”

“It isn’t meant to be. It’s meant to be useful.”

She took the case back and re-anchored it properly.

Later he floated through the nozzle cavity with Juno from fields and coils, reviewing the replacement sequence. One of the new superconducting segments was refusing to seat.

Juno frowned at the mount. “The numbers say it should lock.”

“The numbers are being optimistic.”

“That’s a very diplomatic way to describe bad tolerances.”

“It’s an old ship. You have to let it keep a little dignity.”

She shifted position, changed angle, and finally saw it: a slight warp in the aft lock ring, barely visible until the light caught it right.

“There. Damn.”

“Exactly.”

“If we’d forced the coil into that mount, it would’ve drifted under load.”

“And then all of us would have had a much worse afternoon.”

She snorted. “You make disaster sound tired.”

“It usually is. Most disasters start with somebody trying to save twenty minutes.”

That one stayed with her.

Around mid-shift Tomas took a moment alone in a service recess behind the nozzle collar. He anchored one boot, one knee, and looked out through a half-open maintenance aperture toward the edge of the bay. No grand view from here. Just work lights, hull shadows, traffic markers, and the blunt geometry of other ships under repair.

Somewhere beyond all that was Earth.

Lagos.

He had sent enough money home to change real things. School fees. Rent. A roof repair after storm damage. Medical bills. A wedding. Two funerals. One failed business turned into a second attempt that worked better. He had done real good with his years out here. Maybe more than he would have done by staying.

But he had never gone back.

At first there had always been a reason. The next contract paid too well. The timing was bad. The opportunity was too useful to waste. Then the years had accumulated in the way only years can, slowly enough to feel harmless until they no longer were.

There were transition programs for long-term spacers. Medical support, retraining, planetary readaptation. Plenty of people went back planetside.

But after thirty years? After this long in habitats, docks, and partial-g rings? He no longer knew whether what stopped him was physiology, fear, or simple habit.

Probably all three.

Lunch was in the dock break pocket, a widened service compartment with wall restraints, hot dispensers, and the kind of table no one pretended was really a table in zero-g. He took a heated pouch of lentil-protein stew, a compressed grain roll, and a sachet of spicy fish-flavor paste containing very little that had ever been near a fish.

Sima anchored beside him and squeezed sauce into her meal pouch.

“You know,” she said, “if Helios had spent half as much on maintenance as they did on structural overkill, we’d all be out of work.”

Tomas tore open the grain roll. “No. We’d be out of this job. There’s always another ship.”

“That is not the hopeful answer.”

“It’s the accurate one.”

One of the subcontracted coil specialists joined in. “The old Helios hulls really are absurdly overbuilt.”

“They are,” Tomas said. “Which is why they’re still worth buying after somebody’s tried very hard to kill them.”

That got a better round of laughter, and for a few minutes the break pocket felt warm and human in the way only station break rooms ever did—food smell in filtered air, awkward anchored postures, everybody too tired to perform competence instead of simply having it.

The second half of shift went smoothly. One coil seated cleanly. Another needed shimming. The third had to come back out after the mount correction, but by then the team was moving well enough that nobody treated it like a crisis. Tomas liked that most about good crews. Not speed. Not brilliance. The ability to let a problem stay a problem instead of turning it into drama.

By handover, the nozzle frame was stabilized, the damaged support geometry corrected, two superconducting coil assemblies installed, and the ship one long step closer to being saleable again.

Not good as new. Nothing in the Belt was ever good as new.

But useful again.

That was what the yard really sold. Not ships. Continuation.

The ride back to the ring brought the familiar return of weight. First the suggestion of it, then the slow settling of his body as the station’s rotation took hold again. At 0.3 g the habitat still felt light, but after a full day in zero-g even that faint pull was enough to remind him of age.

Back in his cabin he changed, drank half a liter of electrolyte mix, and met Sima outside the gym.

The gym was crowded, as always. On an old low-g station, exercise was not fashion. It was maintenance. Resistance frames, tread rigs, cable stations, endurance rows, balance drills, bone-load platforms. Tomas moved through the routine he had followed in one form or another for years: loaded leg work, upper-body resistance, twenty minutes of endurance, mobility, breath control. Every day. No exceptions unless he was injured or unconscious.

He did not enjoy it.

But he respected what happened if he stopped.

Afterward came a short ration-clean shower, warm forced-air drying, and off-shift clothes: soft station trousers, a dark shirt, an overshirt with one sleeve seam repaired too many times.

The bar sat in a wider junction near the gym and main transit corridor, perfectly placed to catch people from multiple shifts. It was called the Lantern, though most of the station only used the name when outsiders were listening.

It smelled of cooked food, old alcohol, citrus cleaner, and too many people sharing the same air system. The tables were scarred. The lighting was low. The walls were cluttered with old ship badges, dock plates, patched logos from companies that no longer existed, and a cracked survey map of the mid-Belt signed by crews who had moved on, retired, died, or vanished into other contracts.

This was where the station felt most alive.

Not because it was beautiful. Because people came back.

Tomas ordered proper food—vat-grown beef strips in dark sauce, pepper greens, dense grain mash—and a glass of something amber that cost enough to deserve being sipped slowly. Sima joined him, then one of the tug pilots, then Juno, and before long the table had become the usual loose improvised gathering of people who worked too much together.

Someone produced a game set. Not cards exactly, but coded tabs for a modular strategy game that had acquired so many local variants nobody was entirely sure what the official version looked like anymore.

“Are we playing by station rules or civilized rules?” Juno asked.

“This station has rules?” said Sima.

“Barely,” Tomas said. “Which is how you know they belong to us.”

That got the table laughing, and the game carried them pleasantly through the slow part of evening—small bets, bad bluffs, accusations of statistical betrayal, the usual kind of leisure stations produced when people had neither time nor space for anything elaborate.

Later, with the bar thinning toward another shift change, Tomas sat back with his second drink and watched the traffic beyond the junction. Workers coming off shift. A woman carrying a sleepy child. Two teenagers moving too fast and pretending not to notice corridor signage. A pair of dockhands still half in work rig, arguing about parts inventory. Somebody singing badly somewhere down the next passage.

A child on station.

Not many here. More than before.

He wondered, not for the first time, what exactly his life had amounted to. Not a wife. Not children. Not a house on Earth. Not the clean, temporary exile he had once imagined.

But not failure either.

He had done real work. Sent real money home. Helped younger engineers learn the trade without breaking themselves on it. Kept ships alive that would otherwise have become scrap or coffins. Made himself useful in a world where usefulness translated better than almost anything else.

There were worse outcomes.

By the time he returned to his cabin, the corridor had quieted. He brushed his teeth, checked the next shift notes, ignored two personal messages he was too tired to answer properly, and sat for a moment on the edge of the bunk.

Tomorrow would be Bay Twelve again. Coil seating. Field calibration. Nozzle integrity checks. Maybe radiator replacement, if the parts flow held and nobody upstream decided to make that impossible.

Another day given over to steel, vacuum, schedules, and the long half-accidental life he had once promised himself would only be temporary.

He lay back and let the cabin light dim automatically.

The station hummed around him, patient and familiar.

Somewhere impossibly far below, Earth went on without him.

Tomas closed his eyes before the thought could become regret, and fell asleep to the sound of ventilation, pumps, and the old machinery of a place that had kept him far longer than he had ever intended to stay.
