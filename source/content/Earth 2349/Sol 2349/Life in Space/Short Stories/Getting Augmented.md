## Leaving for Mars

By the time Shukra Biotechnologies confirmed his placement, Wei Ren had already done everything right.

He had ranked at the top of his cohort in Delhi Prime, published early, outperformed people older than him, and survived the kind of academic competition that quietly rearranged lives. He had spent years becoming the sort of person who might one day be noticed by one of the great interplanetary institutions.

And Shukra had noticed. The offer was everything it was supposed to be. Permanent placement on Mars. Entry into one of the company’s elite scientific tracks. Full relocation. Housing. Status. Access. A future that would have been impossible for almost anyone from his district a generation earlier.

It also came with a condition that was never quite presented as one.

For long-term assignment in Shukra’s upper research structure, baseline humanity was considered insufficient. Not unacceptable, not inferior in any openly stated moral sense, just poorly fitted to the demands of Martian high-performance science. The official language was always careful: *adaptation, optimization, continuity of function, cognitive resilience, off-world stability*.

The augmentation package was voluntary. Like many things in the inner system, it was voluntary in exactly the way that mattered least. 

He signed.

## Evaluation

The first stage did not feel like the beginning of transformation. It felt like administration.

For nine days he moved through a clinical complex in cislunar orbit owned by a Shukra subsidiary that officially did not perform enhancement, only *adaptation medicine for interplanetary placement candidates*. The building was quiet, white, and softly lit, designed to suggest precision without intimidation. Everyone was polite. No one ever rushed him.

They scanned everything.

He underwent full-body imaging in layered passes: skeletal density, vascular structure, connective tissue resilience, endocrine balance, liver function, gut flora, inflammatory response, cortical mapping, peripheral nerve branching, sleep architecture, eye movement, stress chemistry, memory retention, reaction patterns. His genome was sequenced again despite prior certification. Then re-sequenced through Shukra’s own systems. Then checked against family background, ancestral drift markers, mutation risks, prior medical edits, and long-term compatibility models.

The cybernetics division wanted to know how his nervous system distributed load under pressure. The genetic medicine division wanted to know how his body regulated itself when pushed out of its preferred state. The integration team cared about the spaces in between: where tissue met signal, where gene expression altered recovery, where one modification might quietly destabilize another ten years later.

It was not assembly-line medicine. It was personalization at industrial scale.

That, more than anything, impressed him. Shukra did not merely install systems into people. It adapted systems to them.

He had expected to feel proud during the evaluations. Chosen. Validated.

Instead he felt observed with a degree of completeness that made pride seem childish.

## The Genetic Work

The genemods came first.

Shukra preferred that. Biological adaptation had to settle before the heavier neural integration stages. Some changes would begin quickly through targeted therapy and expression control; others would emerge over weeks and months as his body adjusted to its new regulatory profile.

The doctors explained everything in patient, elegant language.

There would be no dramatic transformation. No sudden superhuman awakening. He would look the same. Sound the same. He would not become stronger in any cinematic sense, nor visibly altered in the way people on Earth still half-imagined when they heard the word *modded*. What would change was the baseline.

His sleep architecture was rewritten for long-haul scientific work in Martian institutional life. After full adaptation, he would need only four hours of true sleep for an extended rest cycle. In theory, this was an extraordinary gain. In practice, it meant his body would recover more shallowly. He would rest more efficiently, but not as deeply. His doctors framed it as acceptable because Mars rewarded continuity more than softness.

A second standard package focused on memory consolidation. Information retention, reviewed data, procedural recall, names, layouts, technical structures—all of it would settle more easily and stay cleaner once learned. It was ideal for elite analytical work, especially in research environments where error cascades began with forgotten details.

The trade-off was described almost casually: under extreme time pressure, improvisation could become harder. A brain tuned toward deep retention and high-resolution recall did not always pivot gracefully in chaos. There were statistical tendencies toward hesitation when forced to choose before pattern certainty emerged.

That bothered him more than the sleep change.

Not because he valued improvisation more than sleep, but because the sentence felt like a small warning embedded inside a sales pitch. Shukra had found a better use for his mind. That did not necessarily mean it was the same use he had imagined for himself.

The treatments themselves were uneventful in the way serious medicine often is. Tailored vectors. Regulation scaffolds. Immune monitoring. Controlled expression triggers. Daily bloodwork. Metabolic watches. Long interviews about sensation, appetite, dreams, and fatigue.

For two weeks he felt almost nothing.

Then he began waking before the clinic lights shifted toward morning, completely alert after four hours of sleep, staring at the ceiling with the uneasy sense that his body had quietly moved on without asking whether the rest of him was ready.

## Preparation for Implantation

The cybernetics stage was more invasive.

Before surgery came another round of modelling, this time not of who he was, but of how his nervous system might be extended without breaking the illusion that it was still only his own. They mapped peripheral nerve pathways in absurd detail, traced conduction delays, identified redundant routes, stress vulnerabilities, scar risks, and regions where interface density could be increased without compromising stability. They simulated adaptation curves. They adjusted contact materials. They altered anchor geometries and interface chemistry to fit his tissues, his signal behavior, his immune profile.

One surgeon explained it to him in the simplest possible terms.

“If an implant feels like a tool,” she said, “the integration is incomplete.”

The goal was not operation but incorporation. His body would not be taught to command a machine. It would be taught to treat one as part of itself.

That required temporary immune suppression during the implantation window, along with aggressive infection control and extremely careful tissue handling. Even in 2349, the body still recognized intrusion when cut open deeply enough. Shukra’s answer was not brute force, but choreography: suppression, insertion, settlement, monitoring, reactivation, adaptation.

He signed more documents before surgery than he had signed for his employment contract. He read them all.

That turned out to be a mistake.

None of the clauses were cruel. None were obviously sinister. But by the end of the stack, he had learned how many different ways a human nervous system could fail to accept being improved.

## Multi-Stage Surgery

The implantation was done in stages.

Shukra did not place complex neural systems all at once unless urgency made caution impossible. Elite staff received the slower path: less dramatic, more expensive, lower risk.

The first surgery established the interface foundation. Conduction lattices were introduced along selected nerve routes. Micro-contact arrays were anchored where later systems would need clean access to signal traffic. Stabilization materials were laid in place so that the body would heal around them rather than merely tolerate them. He spent the next days in a drifting, chemically softened state, wrapped in cooling blankets and sensor mesh, waking to flashes of white ceiling and the sensation that part of him was waiting for instructions that had not yet arrived.

The second stage integrated the core cognitive implant package.

Shukra’s standard science-track choice was the **Analysis Engine**, a neural-cognitive system intended for pattern recognition, inference, and pressure-resistant reasoning. It was not a data screen in the skull. It was not a private assistant whispering answers. The doctors were very clear about that. It did not think for him.

What it did was worse, or better, depending on the day. It shortened the distance between observation and conclusion.

Localized cortical interfaces and deeper signal bridges allowed the system to participate in perception itself, tagging relationships, weighting relevance, tightening causal chains before conscious verbal thought had fully formed. It did not place text in front of his eyes. It did not project neat diagrams into his vision.

It made certain structures feel immediately legible.

At first this arrived as confusion. He would look at a fluid dynamics model and know, with uncomfortable certainty, where instability would emerge before he could explain why. He would hear two researchers arguing across the corridor and feel the shape of the disagreement more quickly than the words themselves. Presented with an unfamiliar instrument panel during training, he found that he could often sense which subsystem mattered before reading the labels.

A week later, during calibration exercises, a technician displayed three layered material-stress maps on a blank wall. Before augmentation, he would have scanned them, compared reference points, checked variance patterns, and then reached a conclusion.

Now he looked once and simply knew where the fracture cascade would begin. The answer came with such speed that he distrusted it.

He stood there for several seconds, waiting for the slower old version of thought to arrive and confirm what the new one had already handed him.

That was the part no brochure could really explain: the disorientation of becoming faster than your own self-trust.

## Learning the New Mind

Rehabilitation was not about healing alone. It was about learning.

He had to practice with the implant until its outputs stopped feeling uncanny. Shukra trained people to use cybernetics the way athletes trained movement or musicians trained timing: repetition, stress exposure, controlled overload, recovery, recalibration.

He learned the difference between seeing information and receiving structure. On a screen, numbers were still numbers. Columns, values, graphs, correlations. Useful, but external.

With the implant engaged, the same information could become something closer to immediate internal form. Not mystical. Not magical. Just brutally fast. A system under strain felt strained. A trend felt unstable before he named the variable behind it. A human conversation carried hidden weightings his old self might only have noticed minutes later.

The change made decision-making quicker, but it also made ordinary interaction feel slightly delayed, as though other people were still walking through doors he had already seen open in his head.

When the implant was pushed harder in supervised exercises, it produced more obvious effects. Focus narrowed. Pattern chains sharpened. Relevant details seemed to lock together with a satisfying mechanical elegance. The cost came afterward: headache pressure behind the eyes, exhaustion, a metallic sourness in the mouth, and once, after he insisted on another run, a violent burst of nausea and hand tremor that sent two med-techs hurrying across the room.

They called it load accumulation. He called it the moment the future stopped feeling glamorous.

## Voluntary

By the end of the program he was still himself, which somehow made the unease harder to categorize.

He still liked the same music. He still spoke too quickly when nervous. He still corrected bad assumptions in his head before he decided whether to do it aloud. His face in the mirror was the same face that had arrived from Earth. But his life had tilted.

He now slept four hours and woke ready. He remembered almost everything he intentionally studied. He could look at a messy set of partial facts and feel connections forming before language caught them. He was better fitted for Mars, better fitted for Shukra, and perhaps for the work he had dreamed about since childhood.

And yet the word *voluntary* remained with him in an irritating, unresolved way. He had wanted this. He had chosen it. 

But in the final week, while watching the ochre disk of Mars grow larger through the transport window, he found himself wondering whether wanting a door badly enough meant you had also chosen the room on the other side.

He pressed two fingers absentmindedly to the skin behind his ear where one of the deeper interface routes had been anchored. The scar was almost invisible now. Soon even he would stop noticing it.

Below him, somewhere inside the ship’s encrypted personnel systems, his file no longer described him as an incoming researcher. It described him as adapted staff.

He looked out at Mars again, at the world he had worked his whole life to reach, and felt pride rise in him exactly as it should have.

It was only afterward, softer and harder to explain, that the doubt arrived.
