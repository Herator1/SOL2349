Although humanity has spread across the entire solar system, there is **no single unified legal authority** governing all colonies and settlements. Instead, the solar system operates under a loose framework of shared principles, treaties, and customary rules that have evolved since the early expansion era.

These norms are often collectively referred to as **Interplanetary Law**, though enforcement varies widely depending on location and political power.


# Jurisdiction

The most fundamental principle of space governance is simple:
**The owner of a station or settlement determines its laws.**

Habitats, stations, mining installations, and arcologies are generally considered sovereign territory of the entity that built or controls them. This entity may be:

- a planetary government
- a megacorporation
- a cooperative settlement
- an independent habitat authority

As a result, the legal environment across the solar system can vary dramatically between locations. Some stations operate under strict democratic governance, while others function as **corporate territories with private security forces and internal legal systems**.

# Human Rights Framework

Most political entities officially recognize a baseline set of **interplanetary human rights**, derived from earlier United Nations treaties established during the early space expansion era.

These rights typically include protections such as:
- basic personal freedoms
- access to life support and medical care
- legal protections against forced labor or execution without trial

In practice, enforcement of these standards is inconsistent, particularly in the Asteroid Belt and in corporate-controlled territories where economic pressures often override humanitarian concerns.

# Taxation and Economic Regulation

Taxation across the solar system varies widely depending on local governance structures. Unlike many historical planetary economies, most interplanetary taxation focuses on **resource flows rather than labor**. Common forms of taxation include:

- cargo transfer tariffs
- docking and transit fees
- resource extraction royalties
- fuel and propellant taxes

Because most economic activity in space involves the movement of physical goods between settlements, these transaction-based taxes are often easier to enforce than traditional income taxation. Each political faction determines its own economic policies, and tax regimes can differ dramatically between planets and stations.


# Artificial Intelligence Prohibition

One of the few universally recognized laws across the solar system is the prohibition of **advanced artificial intelligence development**. Following the catastrophic events of the **AI War and Singularity Event**, nearly all major factions agreed to strict limitations on AI research.

The development of:
- artificial superintelligence
- self-improving machine intelligence
- large-scale autonomous strategic systems
is universally banned.

However, **autonomous expert systems** remain widely used. These systems perform specialized tasks such as navigation, engineering diagnostics, logistics management, and scientific modeling, but lack the capacity for recursive self-improvement or independent strategic reasoning.

# Prohibition of Weapons of Mass Destruction

Another nearly universal agreement among solar system powers is the prohibition of **weapons of mass destruction in space**.

Banned weapon categories include:

- nuclear weapons
- biological weapons
- chemical weapons
- large-scale radiation dispersal systems

The use of such weapons in the fragile infrastructure of space is widely considered an existential threat. Even a single nuclear detonation near a major station or transportation corridor could trigger catastrophic debris cascades or infrastructure failures affecting millions of people. As a result, nearly all factions maintain strict controls over these technologies.

# Restrictions on Warfare

Open warfare between major factions is extremely rare in the modern solar system. While conflicts do occur—particularly in the Asteroid Belt—large-scale wars between planetary powers or megacorporations are prevented by a widely accepted system of **interplanetary conflict rules**, similar in spirit to earlier international laws of war. These norms apply not only to governments but also to megacorporations, which in many cases operate as **quasi-sovereign powers**. Direct attacks on civilian infrastructure, life-support systems, or major transportation routes are considered severe violations of interplanetary law.

# Collective Defense Principle

Perhaps the most important stabilizing rule in the solar system is the **collective response doctrine**. If any faction deliberately attacks critical shared infrastructure—such as:

- major orbital stations
- life-support networks
- navigation systems
- large civilian habitats

then all factions that depend on those systems are considered justified in responding collectively against the aggressor.

Because nearly every major power relies on interconnected transportation networks, resource flows, and shared infrastructure, this rule creates a strong deterrent against escalation. The solar system’s economic and logistical **interdependence** makes large-scale war extremely risky for all participants.

# Stability Through Interdependence

This fragile balance of shared rules, economic interdependence, and mutual deterrence has helped prevent major wars for more than a century. While local conflicts, piracy, and corporate disputes remain common, the destruction of critical infrastructure would threaten the survival of millions of people across the solar system. For this reason, even rival factions generally prefer **covert competition, economic pressure, or limited proxy conflicts** over direct military confrontation.
