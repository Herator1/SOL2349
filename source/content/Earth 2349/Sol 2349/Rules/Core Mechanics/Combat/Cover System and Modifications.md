In _SOL 2349_, survival depends less on armor-class style abstraction and more on **cover, movement, angles, and timely reactions**.

There is **no passive defense stat**. Attacks are resolved against a **base DC**, which is modified by the target’s position, cover, movement, and defensive actions.

## Attack Difficulty

A standard attack targets **DC 10** before modifiers.

This DC may be increased or reduced by:

- cover
- stance and exposure
- target movement
- defensive reactions such as [[Evade]]
- angle of attack
- other situational effects

## Cover

Cover increases the DC of attacks against a target.

|Cover Type|DC Modifier|Description|
|---|---|---|
|No Cover|+0|Fully exposed|
|Light Cover|+2|Partial obstruction such as railings or crates|
|Medium Cover|+4|Solid protection such as corners or machinery|
|Full Cover|—|Cannot be targeted directly|

**Full Cover:** A target behind full cover cannot be attacked directly unless the attacker changes position, gains a new angle, or uses an effect that ignores cover.

Cover only applies from the direction it actually protects.

## Positioning

A character’s body position affects how easy they are to hit.

|Position|Effect|
|---|---|
|Standing|no modifier|
|Crouched|+1 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Prone|+2 DC vs ranged attacks, −2 DC vs melee attacks|

Changing position is usually part of movement or costs **1 Action**, depending on the situation and GM ruling.

## Target Movement

Moving targets are harder to hit at range.

|Target State|Effect|
|---|---|
|Stationary|no modifier|
|Moving|+1 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Fast movement / sprinting|+2 DC vs ranged attacks|
|Erratic motion or unstable drift|+2 DC or Setback, GM discretion|

These modifiers apply only while the target is actually moving in a way that makes targeting harder.

A target that moved earlier in the round but is currently still does not automatically gain a movement bonus.

## Evade

**Evade** is a defensive Reactive Action used against an incoming attack.

### Timing

Evade must be declared **after the attack is declared, but before the attack roll is made**.

### Effect

- Spend **1 Action**
- Gain **+2 DC** against that attack
- Evade counts as **movement** for this round

Evade represents a brief defensive shift, not full repositioning.

- it may include a step, lean, drop, or short lunge
- it does **not** replace a full movement action

### Requirements

Evade must be physically possible.  
If the character is restrained, pinned, in an extremely tight space, or otherwise unable to react meaningfully, the GM may reduce its effect or disallow it.

A character may Evade multiple times in a round only if they still have Actions available.

## Special Cases

### Prone

A prone character can still Evade. This may represent rolling, flattening further, or shifting behind available cover.

### Zero-G or Free Drift

A character in zero-g can Evade, but without stable footing the motion is less reliable.

The GM may:

- reduce the bonus to **+1 DC**, or
- apply **Setback** to the maneuver if the environment is especially unstable

### Restricted Movement

In tight spaces, while restrained, or under heavy encumbrance, Evade may be weaker or unavailable.

## Angles and Exposure

Cover and positioning only matter if they actually protect the target from the attacker’s current line of fire.

A target may lose some or all of its cover bonus if the attacker:

- moves to the side of the cover
- attacks from elevation
- fires from behind
- gains another angle that bypasses the protected side

The GM should adjudicate this pragmatically rather than geometrically.

## Tactical Edge and Setback

When circumstances strongly favor one side, apply the normal resolution rules:

- **Tactical Edge:** roll **2d20**, keep the higher result
- **Setback:** roll **2d20**, keep the lower result

Typical reasons for **Tactical Edge**:

- target is fully exposed
- superior firing angle
- target is distracted or suppressed
- attacker is concealed

Typical reasons for **Setback**:

- poor visibility
- unstable footing
- sensor interference
- firing under extreme stress or from a bad position

Use these only when circumstances are significant. Do not replace normal cover or stance modifiers with Edge or Setback unless the situation calls for it.

## Reactive Attacks

An attack made as a **Reactive Action** is harder to execute.

- Apply **−2 to the attack roll**

The GM may apply further penalties or **Setback** if the attacker is also moving, unstable, overloaded, or reacting under extreme pressure.

## Stacking Rules

The following can normally combine:

- **Cover** modifies DC
- **Position and movement** modify DC
- **Evade** modifies DC
- **Tactical Edge / Setback** modify the roll
- **Reactive Attack penalties** modify the roll

However, avoid excessive stacking of similar effects. If two modifiers represent essentially the same advantage or hindrance, the GM should usually apply the stronger one rather than both.

## Example

A target is behind **medium cover**, **crouched**, and **moving**.

Base attack DC: **10**  
+4 from medium cover  
+1 from crouched  
+1 from movement

**Total DC: 16**

If the attacker fires as a **Reactive Action**, they suffer **−2 to the attack roll**.

If the attacker also has **Tactical Edge**, they roll **2d20** and keep the better result.
