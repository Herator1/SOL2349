In _SOL 2349_, the environment is often as dangerous as any enemy. Vacuum, decompression, radiation, fire, toxic leaks, unstable gravity, and industrial systems can all injure, disable, isolate, or kill characters very quickly.

Environmental hazards are not handled as a separate minigame. They use the same core systems as the rest of the game: **Action Tests**, **Movement**, **Guard, Health, and Wounds**, **Mental Conditions**, and situational GM judgment.

The key difference is that hazards are often **impersonal, immediate, and unforgiving**. They do not need intent to be lethal.

## Core Principle

Environmental hazards may cause:

- direct damage
- loss of position or control
- forced movement
- mental strain
- system instability
- direct Wounds in extreme cases

Some hazards can be resisted, avoided, contained, or escaped. Others must simply be survived.

## Adjudicating Hazards

When resolving an environmental hazard, use this sequence:

1. identify the hazard
2. decide whether it is **avoidable, resistible, or automatic**
3. determine the immediate effect
4. resolve any test, movement, or damage
5. apply any continuing danger if the hazard persists

Use hazards to create pressure, urgency, and tactical change, not just raw damage.

## Hazard Severity

A useful guideline is to think of hazards in three broad categories:

### Minor Hazard

Dangerous, but usually survivable without lasting harm unless ignored.

Examples:

- smoke-filled corridors
- weak radiation exposure
- unstable footing
- low oxygen warning
- moderate heat
- electrical sparks
- cluttered debris fields

Typical effects:

- **Setback**
- reduced movement
- minor Health damage
- temporary penalty
- rising tension

### Serious Hazard

Immediately dangerous and likely to cause real harm without action.

Examples:

- fire in a sealed room
- toxic gas leak
- hull breach nearby
- strong radiation zone
- uncontrolled electrical arc
- hard vacuum exposure
- violent zero-g drift into structure

Typical effects:

- direct damage
- forced movement
- loss of Actions
- significant penalties
- possible Wounds if not addressed quickly

### Extreme Hazard

Catastrophic danger. Survival may depend on immediate response, gear, or luck.

Examples:

- explosive decompression
- major reactor leak
- direct exposure to vacuum without protection
- crushing industrial machinery
- severe fall or collision
- full fire engulfment
- structural collapse

Typical effects:

- major Health damage
- direct Wounds
- incapacitation
- immediate death if no protection or intervention exists

## Common Hazard Effects

Hazards usually apply one or more of the following effects.

### Damage

Hazards may deal damage to:

- **Guard**, if the harm is indirect or partially avoidable
- **Health**, if the body is directly affected
- **Wounds**, if the event is catastrophic or explicitly bypasses lower layers

As a rule of thumb:

- glancing environmental harm often strips **Guard**
- direct bodily harm targets **Health**
- catastrophic trauma may inflict **direct Wounds**

### Forced Movement

Hazards may move a character against their will.

Examples:

- decompression drag
- blast waves
- slips or falls
- recoil from unstable thrust
- collapsing floors
- zero-g drift

Forced movement may:

- change position
- remove cover
- knock a character prone
- push them into another hazard
- require a test to regain control

### Action Pressure

Hazards often create immediate decision pressure.

Examples:

- spend Actions to seal a breach
- move before a compartment vents
- put out a fire
- stabilize a failing system
- escape a flooding or burning area

A hazard does not need to deal damage every round to be dangerous.

### Mental Pressure

Some hazards also create **Mental Conditions**, especially if they involve:

- near suffocation
- exposure to vacuum
- extreme isolation
- helplessness
- witnessing catastrophic injury
- system collapse under pressure

Use [[Mental Strain & Psychological Pressure]] when the hazard attacks composure as much as the body.

## Common Hazard Types

## Vacuum

Vacuum is one of the defining dangers of the setting.

A character exposed to vacuum without proper protection faces:

- immediate physical danger
- rapid loss of control
- possible forced movement
- possible mental shock
- severe consequences if exposure continues

Typical vacuum effects may include:

- immediate **Setback** on most physical and technical actions
- inability to speak normally
- risk of decompression trauma
- risk of direct Health damage or Wounds in severe exposure
- urgent need to reach shelter or restore pressure

Vacuum should be treated as a **serious or extreme hazard**, depending on duration, protection, and circumstances.

## Decompression

Decompression occurs when atmosphere escapes violently through a breach.

It is more dangerous than simple vacuum exposure because it adds:

- sudden force
- flying debris
- loss of footing
- involuntary movement
- rapid environmental collapse

Typical decompression effects:

- forced movement toward the breach
- **Reflexes + Athletics**, **Reflexes + EVA**, or similar test to hold position
- dropped objects or broken cover
- possible collisions
- possible direct Wounds in extreme cases

Explosive decompression should be treated as an **extreme hazard**.

## Radiation

Radiation may be:

- low and cumulative
- acute and dangerous
- localized and technical
- catastrophic after system failure

Radiation often works best as a layered hazard:

- mild exposure creates time pressure and penalty
- stronger exposure causes Health damage, equipment problems, or mental pressure
- severe exposure may inflict direct Wounds or long-term consequences

Radiation is especially effective when characters understand the risk but cannot solve it immediately.

## Fire and Heat

Fire is dangerous not only because of burns, but because it also creates:

- smoke
- panic
- blocked routes
- loss of visibility
- equipment damage
- pressure loss risk

Typical fire effects:

- Health damage
- smoke-related Setback
- spreading danger across the scene
- forced repositioning
- possible oxygen or systems failure

Fire works well as an escalating hazard rather than a flat damage source.

## Toxic Atmosphere

Toxic gas, chemical leaks, and industrial contamination may:

- inflict Health damage
- impair concentration
- reduce movement
- cause suffocation or disorientation
- force sealed-environment procedures

Typical effects:

- Setback on physical or concentration-based actions
- damage over time
- limited safe exposure window
- increased risk without filters or proper gear

## Unstable Gravity

Gravity irregularities include:

- partial spin failure
- shifting thrust
- unstable centrifugal sections
- sudden transition between gravity states
- severe vibration or inertial disturbance

These hazards mainly affect:

- [[Movement]]
- positioning
- ranged combat
- balance
- precision work

Typical effects:

- reduced movement
- forced tests to stay upright or stay anchored
- Setback on attacks or technical actions
- accidental drift, falls, or collisions

## Electrical and System Hazards

Industrial systems can be just as lethal as weapons.

Examples:

- exposed power lines
- malfunctioning machinery
- magnetic locks
- pressure doors
- automated safety systems
- reactor discharges

Typical effects:

- sudden Health damage
- paralysis or motor disruption
- blocked routes
- forced repositioning
- interaction tests under pressure

These hazards are especially useful in engineering-heavy scenes because they reward technical responses, not just endurance.

## Falling and Collision

In _SOL 2349_, impact matters.

Characters may suffer collision hazards from:

- falls
- thrown debris
- failed zero-g movement
- decompression drag
- violent vehicle movement
- sudden impact with walls, rails, or machinery

Typical effects:

- Guard or Health damage
- prone state
- dropped items
- direct Wounds on severe impact

In microgravity and zero-g, collisions are often more important than “falling” in the traditional sense.

## Hazard Duration

A hazard may be:

- **instant** — a single event such as an arc flash or explosion
- **ongoing** — repeated danger such as fire, radiation, or toxic air
- **escalating** — a worsening problem such as structural collapse or spreading decompression

The GM should always decide:

- what triggers the hazard
- how long it lasts
- whether it worsens if ignored
- what actions can reduce or end it

## Avoiding and Resisting Hazards

Characters may respond to hazards in different ways:

### Avoid

Escape the dangerous area, break line of effect, close a hatch, take cover, or move out of range.

### Resist

Use Attributes, Skills, gear, or special adaptations to withstand the effect.

Common pairings may include:

- **Fitness + Endurance**
- **Reflexes + Athletics**
- **Reflexes + EVA**
- **Coordination + Trauma**
- **Intellect + Systems**
- **Presence + Stress Control**, if the main danger is psychological

### Mitigate

Seal the breach, kill the power, suppress the fire, deploy drones, stabilize a system, or use the right equipment.

Environmental hazards should reward preparation and competence, not just toughness.

## Direct Wounds

Some hazards bypass normal survivability layers and inflict **direct Wounds**.

This should be reserved for clearly catastrophic events, such as:

- explosive decompression at close range
- major industrial crushing trauma
- severe collision at high speed
- catastrophic radiation event
- direct exposure to unsurvivable conditions

Direct Wounds should be rare, but feared.

## Hazard and Equipment Interaction

Equipment matters enormously in hazardous environments.

Relevant protection may include:

- EVA suits
- sealed masks
- radiation shielding
- tethers
- mag boots
- fire suppression gear
- drones for remote intervention
- medical and engineering tools

In many cases, proper equipment does not remove the hazard, but changes it from **extreme** to **serious**, or from **serious** to **manageable**.

## Hazard and Combat Interaction

Hazards often overlap with combat.

They may:

- remove cover
- force movement
- interrupt actions
- create Setback
- isolate characters
- injure both sides equally
- make standing still impossible

In _SOL 2349_, the environment should often feel like a third combatant.

## GM Guidance

When using hazards:

- keep the danger clear
- make the consequences understandable
- apply urgency without overcomplicating the scene
- let players solve problems creatively
- escalate only when the situation justifies it

Hazards are most effective when they change decisions, not just subtract Health.

## Example: Hull Breach in Combat

A firefight breaks out in a damaged corridor. A burst ruptures a weakened panel.

The GM rules:

- immediate decompression hazard
- anyone near the breach must test to avoid being dragged
- loose objects are pulled toward the opening
- ranged attacks suffer Setback because of instability
- closing the emergency bulkhead becomes an urgent objective

The hazard does not replace combat. It transforms it.

## Example: Reactor Leak

A damaged reactor chamber floods with radiation.

The GM rules:

- entering the chamber creates an ongoing serious hazard
- each round inside risks Health damage
- prolonged exposure may cause a direct Wound
- technical work inside suffers pressure and time constraints
- proper shielding gear reduces the severity

This turns a technical scene into a survival problem.
