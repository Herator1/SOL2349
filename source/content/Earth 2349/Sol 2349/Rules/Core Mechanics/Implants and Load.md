In SOL 2349, [[Implants (Lists)]] are what separate trained professionals from truly dangerous operators. They are not passive enhancements or background upgrades—they are active systems, designed to push the human body beyond its natural limits for short, decisive moments.

[[Implants (Lists)]] allow characters to act faster, hit harder, survive longer, or interface directly with technology and other systems. But this power is never free.

Every activation places strain on the body and mind. Neural pathways overheat, synthetic interfaces desynchronize, and biological systems are pushed to their limits. This strain is tracked as **Load**—a temporary resource that represents how much stress a character can handle before things start to break down.

Under normal conditions, trained operators manage their systems carefully. They spike their performance when needed, then allow time to recover. But in prolonged engagements or high-pressure situations, restraint becomes a luxury. When a character pushes beyond their safe limits, they risk **Overload**.

Overload is not a binary failure—it is a spectrum of consequences. Minor overload may cause delays, reduced precision, or increased stress. Severe overload can result in system shutdowns, neural feedback, loss of control, or even permanent damage. The more a character exceeds their limits, the more likely catastrophic failure becomes.

This creates a constant tension at the core of the system:

- Do you hold back and conserve your systems?
- Or do you push further, knowing the risks?

Implants are designed for **short bursts of extreme performance**, not sustained superiority. They reward timing, tactical decision-making, and risk management. Used well, they can turn the tide of an encounter. Used recklessly, they can take a character out of the fight just as quickly.

## Overload Rules

When a character exceeds their Load limit, their systems begin to fail under stress. This is resolved through an **Overload Roll**, which determines the severity of the resulting malfunction.

### Trigger

An Overload Roll is made **immediately after an ability is used** that pushes the character beyond their Load maximum.

There is no delay and no grace period—the consequences are instant.

---

### Roll

To resolve Overload, roll:

> **1d6 + Overload Value**

**Overload Value = current Load – maximum Load**

---

### Example

- A character with **Load 5 (max 5)** uses an ability that costs 2 Load
- Their Load increases to **7**

→ Overload Value = **+2**

→ Roll: **1d6 + 2**

---

### Resolution

The result is applied immediately using the **Overload Table**.

- Low results cause minor disruptions (lag, reduced precision, stress)
- Mid results impair performance (loss of actions, system instability)
- High results trigger severe failures (damage, shutdowns, wounds)

The higher the overload, the more dangerous the outcome.

---

### Important Notes

- **Every ability used above the Load limit triggers a new Overload Roll**
- Effects are applied **immediately after the ability resolves**
- Overload does **not prevent the ability**—the action still happens
- Overload effects can stack and escalate within the same encounter
