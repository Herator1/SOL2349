## Overview

Mercury in 2349 is not a world of cities but of **infrastructure, energy and industry**. Closest to the Sun, the planet has become the most important energy and heavy industrial zone of the inner solar system. Vast solar arrays cover large regions of the sunlit hemisphere, converting the immense solar flux into electricity used by enormous processing facilities and transmitted outward through microwave power relays.
The planet remains an extremely hostile environment. Surface temperatures range from extreme heat on the day side to deep cold on the night side, and the lack of atmosphere leaves the surface exposed to radiation and micrometeorites. Human facilities therefore exist mostly **underground, inside heavily shielded installations, or in orbit**.

Most of Mercury’s industry is **highly automated**. Autonomous mining machines, construction drones and maintenance systems operate continuously across the planet’s industrial zones, overseen by relatively small human crews. Entire complexes function with minimal direct human involvement.
Despite the harsh conditions, Mercury is strategically essential. Its enormous energy production and materials processing support industries on Venus, orbital manufacturing around Earth and ship construction across the inner system. For this reason, the planet’s infrastructure is largely controlled by powerful **megacorporate industrial consortia**.
To most people, Mercury is not a destination but a **work frontier**—a remote, dangerous and immensely productive industrial world dominated by machines.

## Industry and Economy

Mercury’s economy is almost entirely focused on **energy generation and heavy industrial processing**. The planet receives several times the solar energy available near Earth, making it ideal for massive solar power installations. Fields of high-efficiency photovoltaic arrays and solar thermal plants generate enormous amounts of electricity.
Much of this power is used locally in **large-scale metallurgical and materials processing facilities**, where ore extracted from the crust is refined into structural metals and advanced alloys. The extreme energy availability allows manufacturing processes that would be impractical elsewhere.

Mercury also exports **energy itself**. Microwave transmission stations beam power to orbital receivers and energy platforms, which redistribute it throughout the inner solar system.
Control of these facilities is divided among competing **megacorporate consortia**, whose industrial zones dominate large portions of the planet’s sunlit hemisphere. Because Mercury’s energy output is critical to the inner system economy, infrastructure conflicts are rare and tightly regulated.

## Mining and Infrastructure

Mercury’s surface is dominated by sprawling industrial zones rather than settlements. Mining operations extract iron, nickel and a variety of rare metals essential for advanced manufacturing and spacecraft construction.

Most mining sites are operated by **autonomous extraction systems**. Ore is transported via sealed maglev cargo lines or mass-driver launch systems to nearby processing facilities before being shipped to orbit.

The most visible structures on Mercury are its enormous **solar array fields**, which stretch across hundreds of kilometers in some regions. These installations feed energy into planetary power grids connected to large microwave transmission complexes that export power to orbital stations.

Above the planet, several major **industrial docking platforms and logistics stations** handle the transport of refined materials and equipment. Robotic maintenance fleets ensure that Mercury’s infrastructure operates continuously despite the extreme environment.

## Population

Mercury has a relatively small population of **six to eight million people**, most of them working on temporary contracts.

Workers typically live in **underground habitats, mobile industrial bases or orbital stations** that protect them from radiation and temperature extremes. Their role is primarily to supervise automated systems, maintain infrastructure and manage logistics.

Mercury therefore has a highly **transient society**. Most residents stay only a few years before returning to more hospitable parts of the solar system, making the planet less a place to live than a demanding but highly paid industrial assignment.
