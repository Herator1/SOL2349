# Overview

Earth remains the **center of human civilization**, but no longer its uncontested pinnacle. With a population dwarfing every other world, it is still the political and cultural heart of humanity. Yet the planet carries deep scars from the mid-21st century. The AI War did not end cleanly; it left behind shattered infrastructure, destabilized regions, and in its final phase, the use of nuclear and radiological weapons across key strategic zones.

Large parts of the northern hemisphere—particularly North America, Europe, Russia, and China—were rendered temporarily uninhabitable due to **radiation, infrastructure collapse, and cascading ecological damage**. In the decades that followed, billions migrated southward. This shift reshaped the global population map and led to the rise of new urban centers that would eventually become the megacities of the modern era.

Over centuries, parts of the north recovered. But instead of rebuilding the old world, humanity chose a different path. Vast regions were deliberately **left to rewild**, turning former industrial and urban zones into controlled ecological reserves. These areas remain sparsely populated, quiet, and in some places still marked by the remnants of past destruction.

# Megacities

Modern Earth is defined by a handful of immense megacities, each functioning as a self-contained civilization. These urban giants dominate the southern hemisphere and host populations larger than entire planets elsewhere in the solar system.

Among the most influential are:

### **Nova São Paulo**

A sprawling arcology cluster dominating South America, Nova São Paulo is the **financial engine of Earth** and the primary power base of the **Solaris Financial Group**. The city rises in dense vertical layers of corporate towers, data centers, and trading hubs, where vast amounts of capital move through high-frequency markets and interplanetary financial systems. Everything here revolves around **money, risk, and control of information**.

But beneath its polished surface lies one of the most extreme examples of inequality on Earth. The upper levels of the arcologies are home to the financial elite—secure, self-contained environments with controlled climate, private security, and access to advanced technology. These **gated vertical districts** operate almost like separate worlds, insulated from the chaos below.

At ground level and in the lower infrastructure layers, vast populations live in overcrowded districts with limited access to resources. Informal economies, debt systems, and corporate dependency define daily life for millions.

Power in Nova São Paulo is not centralized. Instead, it is constantly negotiated between:
- megacorporate interests
- financial institutions
- local power structures

This creates a city that is incredibly dynamic—but also unstable. In Nova São Paulo, wealth is measured in kilometers—  the higher you live, the further you are from everything else.

### **Greater Lagos**

Stretching along the West African coast, Greater Lagos is a vast, continuous urban region of ports, shipyards, markets, and dense residential zones. It is a city defined by **movement and improvisation**—goods, people, and information flow constantly through its layered infrastructure. Formal systems exist, but much of the city runs on informal networks, local knowledge, and rapid adaptation. Trade is everywhere, from massive container terminals to street-level economies, making Lagos one of the most dynamic and resilient places on Earth. But this flexibility comes at a cost.

Large parts of the city operate in a **grey zone between regulation and reality**. Infrastructure is often overloaded, governance fragmented, and corporate control uneven. Smuggling, black markets, and shadow logistics are deeply embedded in daily life. Entire districts function outside formal oversight, while critical systems—power, water, data—are sometimes unstable or privately controlled. In Greater Lagos, opportunity is everywhere—  
but so is risk.

### **Mombasa Corridor**

Running along the East African coastline into the Arabian Peninsula, the Mombasa Corridor is a **strategic trade artery** linking ocean, land, and orbital routes. Long, linear, and highly structured, it is shaped by infrastructure: ports, transport lines, and energy networks. Corporate influence is strong here, with several major logistics and resource companies maintaining tight control over key segments of the corridor.

### **Delhi Prime**

The largest continuous urban region on Earth, Delhi Prime is a **planet-scale megacity defined by density**. Billions live within its vast, layered infrastructure of old and new systems. It is a center of culture, manufacturing, and population-driven economy, where advanced technology coexists with immense human scale. Control is diffuse, with governments, corporations, and local systems overlapping in complex ways.

### **New Sydney Arc**

A highly developed Australasian megacity, New Sydney Arc is one of the most **stable and technologically advanced regions on Earth**. Clean, efficient, and closely integrated with off-world infrastructure, it serves as a major hub for space traffic and high-end industry. Strong corporate governance—often led by NovaChem and affiliated partners—ensures order, but at the cost of strict regulation and limited autonomy.

These cities are not merely large—they are **planet-scale environments**, with internal economies, infrastructure layers, and social hierarchies that rival entire regions of space. For many citizens, life never extends beyond the boundaries of their megacity.

# Rewilded North

The northern hemisphere stands in stark contrast to these dense urban zones. After the devastation of the AI War and subsequent nuclear exchanges, much of the region was abandoned for decades. Radiation zones, collapsed supply systems, and environmental instability made large-scale habitation impossible.

Over time, as conditions stabilized, the decision was made not to fully repopulate these areas. Instead, vast territories were designated as **rewilding zones**, allowing ecosystems to recover on a continental scale.

Today, these regions are a mix of:

- restored forests and wetlands
- controlled nature reserves
- scattered research stations and small settlements

Ruins of old cities still exist beneath the vegetation, silent reminders of a previous era of human dominance.

---

# Underwater Cities

As sea levels rose and coastal pressures increased, humanity expanded not only across the land but also into the oceans. This led to the development of **semi-submerged and fully underwater cities**, some of the most unique habitats on Earth.

These environments range from shallow-water arcologies to deep-sea research and habitation complexes. Life in these cities is fundamentally different—enclosed, pressurized, and often isolated from the surface world.

Originally built out of necessity, underwater cities have since evolved into:

- specialized research hubs
- secure infrastructure zones
- discreet locations for sensitive operations

They represent one of the most distinctive forms of human settlement in the entire solar system.

# Economy

Despite its immense population and influence, Earth is **not the economic leader of the solar system**. Compared to Mars, Venus, or even parts of the Belt, its infrastructure is uneven and often inefficient at scale.

While megacities host advanced technology and wealthy elites, large portions of the population have limited access to high-end systems. In many cases, the most advanced medical treatments, cybernetics, or industrial technologies are more accessible off-world than on Earth itself.

This imbalance has driven a steady wave of **migration into space**, as individuals seek opportunity, access, or simply a different life beyond the constraints of Earth.

---

# Society

Earth is a world of extremes. Wealth and poverty, advanced technology and failing infrastructure, dense urban life and empty wilderness all exist side by side.

The megacities are vibrant, overcrowded, and highly stratified. Meanwhile, the rewilded zones offer space, silence, and isolation—but little opportunity.

For many, Earth is still home.  
For others, it is something to escape.

---

# Role in the Solar System

Earth remains the **foundation of human civilization**. It provides population, identity, and political weight. Every major faction in the solar system is shaped, directly or indirectly, by its relationship to Earth.

But it is no longer the only center of progress.

In SOL 2349, Earth is not where humanity’s future is being built—  
it is where that future began.
