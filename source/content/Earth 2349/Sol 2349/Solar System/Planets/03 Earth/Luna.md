# Overview

Luna is one of the most important and densely developed off-world locations in the Solar System. As Earth’s closest celestial neighbor, it serves as the **primary gateway between planetary civilization and space infrastructure**. More than any other off-world settlement, Luna connects the enormous population and economic weight of Earth with the transport networks, industries, and orbital systems that sustain interplanetary civilization.

Its proximity to Earth and the maturity of the Earth–Moon transport corridor have allowed Luna to develop into a **highly urbanized, economically powerful, and strategically indispensable world**. Travel times are short by interplanetary standards, transport routes are well established, and the Moon’s low gravity makes it far more efficient than Earth as a launch point for cargo, construction material, and industrial output.

The result is a landscape shaped by mass drivers, transfer ports, habitat complexes, logistics hubs, and industrial zones rather than by open settlement. Luna is not a frontier in the old sense. It is an extension of near-Earth civilization—dense, developed, commercially vital, and deeply entangled with megacorporate power.

A large share of that power is concentrated in the hands of **[[NovaChem]]**, whose industrial presence, infrastructure control, and political influence make the corporation one of the defining forces in lunar life.

---

# Gateway to Space

Luna’s greatest strategic advantage is its position within the Earth–Moon system. It functions as the main transfer point for cargo leaving Earth, passengers traveling off-world, and industrial goods entering space-based supply chains. For much of the inner system, Luna is the point where planetary civilization ends and interplanetary logistics begin.

Compared with launches directly from Earth, transporting goods from Luna is significantly cheaper due to its low gravity and lack of atmosphere. Because of this, many large cargo movements, construction operations, and interplanetary missions begin from lunar orbit or from infrastructure linked to the lunar surface.

This makes Luna not merely a nearby colony, but a **structural necessity** for civilization beyond Earth.

---

# Transport Infrastructure

The Earth–Moon system contains some of the most advanced transport infrastructure in human space, and Luna sits at its center. The Moon’s industrial economy depends on continuous movement: cargo from Earth, processed materials from the surface, manufactured goods for orbit, and passengers heading deeper into the Solar System.

Large-scale **mass driver systems** accelerate cargo into orbit without the need for conventional rockets, making Luna one of the most efficient export platforms in the inner system. These launch tracks handle raw materials, industrial feedstocks, prefabricated components, and supplies for orbital construction and long-range shipping.

Regular transfer between Earth and Luna is maintained through rotating shuttle networks, high-efficiency transfer ships, orbital tug systems, and dense supporting traffic in cislunar space. Additional rail-assisted launch systems on Earth, partial tether infrastructure, and tightly synchronized transfer windows further increase throughput. As a result, Luna is one of the few off-world destinations accessible at comparatively low cost, including for non-elite travelers.

---

# Economy and Industry

Luna specializes in **low-gravity manufacturing, logistics, and industrial processing**. Reduced structural stress, easier handling of large components, and low-cost export to orbit make the Moon ideal for industries that benefit from scale, precision, and efficient mass movement.

Major sectors include advanced materials production, chemical processing, spacecraft components, structural assemblies for orbital construction, and processing of locally extracted regolith and subsurface materials. Much of this industrial output feeds directly into orbital habitats, shipyards, stations, and interplanetary transport systems.

While Luna has its own mining base, its true importance lies less in raw extraction than in **turning material flow into usable infrastructure**. The Moon is one of the main places where Earth’s industrial demand, off-world resources, and orbital construction capacity intersect.

---

# Habitat Complexes

Most of Luna’s population lives in vast **subsurface or enclosed habitat complexes** designed to protect against radiation, vacuum, and severe surface temperature variation. These range from dense worker settlements and transport-linked residential sectors to corporate arcologies, research habitats, and high-end enclosed districts for executives, investors, and long-term specialists.

Some of the largest complexes rival terrestrial cities in scale, though their internal structure is more controlled, modular, and infrastructure-dependent. Life on Luna is highly artificial in the literal sense: every breathable volume, every agricultural section, every transit corridor, and every public zone exists because it is actively maintained.

This produces a society that feels urban and permanent, but also mechanically fragile. Lunar civilization is stable only so long as its systems remain functional.

---

# Tourism

Luna is one of the few off-world locations where **tourism forms a significant part of the economy**. Its proximity to Earth, relatively mature transport links, and iconic status make it an attractive destination for visitors seeking a first off-world experience.

Tourists are drawn by the low-gravity environment, the view of Earth from the lunar surface, controlled surface excursions, luxury observation habitats, and the prestige of visiting humanity’s oldest off-world settlement. For wealthy travelers, Luna is a polished gateway to space. For segments of the Earth middle class, it is one of the few off-world destinations that remains at least marginally attainable.

---

# Corporate Power — NovaChem

Luna is the primary power base of **[[NovaChem]]**, one of the most influential megacorporations in the Solar System. The corporation controls major industrial zones, chemical production facilities, logistics assets, and significant portions of the infrastructure linking lunar industry to orbit.

NovaChem’s influence shapes much of Luna’s political and economic reality. The Moon is formally more complex than a pure company colony, but in practice corporate priorities heavily influence development, labor structures, public order, and infrastructure access. Economic efficiency, throughput, and long-term industrial stability tend to take precedence over broader civic concerns.

This does not make Luna lawless. On the contrary, many parts of it are orderly, safe, and highly functional. But that order exists within a system whose center of gravity is unmistakably corporate.

---

# Society

Lunar society is highly stratified. Corporate elites, senior technical specialists, and high-value professionals live in secure and well-serviced arcologies, while industrial workers, support staff, and transient populations occupy denser habitat zones tied closely to logistics and production infrastructure.

At the same time, Luna generally maintains a relatively high standard of living compared with many other industrial regions in human space. Its proximity to Earth, strategic importance, and mature infrastructure make full systemic collapse unlikely and ensure constant investment in functionality.

The result is a society that is efficient, artificial, heavily managed, and often unequal—but also far more stable than many frontier settlements elsewhere in the Solar System.

---

# Role in the Solar System

Luna is not just a colony. It is the **critical interface between Earth and space**.

It connects Earth’s population and economy with orbital industry, transport infrastructure, and interplanetary trade routes. Without Luna, large-scale human activity beyond Earth would be slower, more expensive, and far less efficient.

If Earth is the demographic heart of civilization, Luna is the first great machine that lifts that civilization into space.
