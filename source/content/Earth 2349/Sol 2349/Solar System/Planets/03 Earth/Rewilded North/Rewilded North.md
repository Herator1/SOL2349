The **Rewilded North** is one of the defining geographic consequences of Earth’s violent 21st-century history. During the wars and systemic collapses of the early 21st century—culminating in the final phases of the **AI War**—large parts of the northern hemisphere were devastated by infrastructure failure, radiological contamination, cascading ecological damage, urban collapse, and targeted strikes against strategic systems.

The regions hit hardest included much of:
- **North America**
- **Europe**
- **Russia**
- **China**

These were once among the most densely urbanized, industrialized, and technologically central parts of old Earth. In the aftermath of the wars, however, they became zones of mass displacement, system breakdown, and long-term instability. Much of the surviving population moved southward over generations, contributing to the explosive rise of the great megacity belts in South America, Africa, India, and the wider global south.

Not all of the north was abandoned permanently. Some regions recovered. Some cities survived or were rebuilt. But large parts of the old northern world were never resettled at anything close to their former density.

Instead, they were allowed—or forced—to become something else.

---

## A Different Kind of Recovery

The Rewilded North is not simply a wasteland, nor is it a restored civilization. It is a broad and uneven belt of territories in which human withdrawal, environmental recovery, historical trauma, and selective reoccupation coexist.

In many regions, former urban and industrial land was deliberately designated as:
- ecological recovery territory
- restricted environmental reserve
- contamination-management zone
- low-density research landscape
- protected non-development region

Over time, forests returned, wetlands expanded, former transport corridors disappeared under vegetation, and ruined industrial belts were slowly reclaimed by climate, biology, and time. The result is one of the strangest landscapes on Earth: vast northern territories where traces of past human dominance remain visible, but no longer define the land completely.

In some places, this recovery is celebrated as one of Earth’s great ecological achievements.  
In others, it is remembered more bitterly—as what happens when civilization never really comes back.

---

## Ruins of the Old World

One of the most haunting features of the Rewilded North is the survival of the **old cities**.

Across parts of North America, Europe, Russia, and China, the ruins of former urban civilization still stand in fragmented form: collapsed towers, buried transit lines, corroded industrial belts, abandoned suburbs, cracked data corridors, flooded underground systems, and the remnants of infrastructure built for a world that no longer exists.

Some areas were dismantled or stabilized long ago. Others were simply left in place, too damaged, too contaminated, too politically sensitive, or too economically irrelevant to justify full recovery.

This means that many rewilded zones are not empty in the romantic sense.  
They are landscapes in which nature and ruin coexist.

A forest may grow through an old logistics district. Wetlands may spread across former highways. A half-collapsed skyline may still stand above land no one has lived in for a century.

---

## Human Settlement Today

The north is not entirely uninhabited. There are still **cities, enclaves, and productive regions**, especially in areas that remained comparatively viable or could be stabilized over time.

Important examples include:
- parts of the **central United States**
- sections of **Mexico**
- more viable and defensible **mountain regions of Europe**
- selected urban and industrial pockets in the wider north that retained strategic value

But these settlements are sparse relative to the scale of the old northern population. Even where modern cities exist, they are usually smaller, more controlled, and far less dominant than their pre-war predecessors. The north no longer defines Earth’s demographic or political center of gravity. It exists as a partial remnant of an earlier world.

This is one of the great historical reversals of modern Earth.

---

## Protected Zones and Empty Regions

Large sections of the Rewilded North are now functionally **near-empty** by old planetary standards. Some are fully protected ecological reserves. Others remain restricted due to contamination, political agreements, historical preservation status, or the danger of buried infrastructure.

Particularly empty or near-empty zones can be found in:
- large stretches of the **former Canadian and northern U.S. interior**
- wide areas of **eastern Europe and western Russia**
- sections of the **Siberian recovery belt**
- abandoned industrial and former strategic corridors in **northern China**
- fragmented former high-density zones where recovery was judged not worth the cost

These are not all equally dead. Some contain research stations, ranger networks, protected biozones, remote settlements, extraction outposts, or monitoring infrastructure. But at continental scale, they are radically underpopulated compared to the old world.

In practical terms, they are some of the only places on Earth where one can still travel for great distances and encounter more wilderness than civilization.

## AI War Relics

The Rewilded North also contains some of the most sensitive and dangerous relic sites on Earth: the remains of **AI War strategic infrastructure**.

Among them are former:
- hardened computation complexes
- autonomous command bunkers
- buried relay fields
- restricted datacenter fortresses
- anti-AI strike zones
- destroyed or sealed **super-AI research and processing sites**

These locations remain politically radioactive even when their physical danger has faded. Artificial intelligence beyond tightly restricted limits is illegal across most of human civilization, and the sites associated with the AI War are subject to some of the harshest exclusion rules on Earth. Many remain sealed, monitored, or classified as permanent restricted zones.

And yet, they attract people.

Treasure hunters, relic scavengers, black-market tech seekers, fringe researchers, conspiracy cults, and off-grid expeditions have all been drawn to the ruins of old AI infrastructure, hoping to find lost computational hardware, forbidden archives, pre-war data caches, or technological remnants valuable enough to justify the risk.

Most find nothing worth surviving for.  
Some never come back.

## Nature, Memory, and Politics

The Rewilded North is not just an environmental region. It is also a political and moral one. To some, it stands as proof that damaged worlds can recover if human systems step back. To others, it represents abandonment on a continental scale—territories written off after catastrophe because the new centers of power no longer needed them.

That tension shapes how the north is remembered. It is at once:
- a recovery project
- a graveyard of old civilization
- a living ecological reserve
- a restricted historical archive
- a frontier for researchers, scavengers, and those trying to live outside the southern megacity system

It remains one of the few parts of Earth where the past is not buried beneath constant redevelopment.  
It is still visible in the land.

---

## Role on Earth

The Rewilded North is one of the great counterweights to the megacities of modern Earth. Where the southern urban belts represent density, continuity, and human expansion, the north represents retreat, memory, and the limits of reconstruction.

It is a region of silence, regrowth, ruins, and forbidden remnants—one of the last places on Earth where civilization does not feel total.

In the 21st century, the north was the center of the world.  
In 2349, much of it has become what remains after the center is gone.
