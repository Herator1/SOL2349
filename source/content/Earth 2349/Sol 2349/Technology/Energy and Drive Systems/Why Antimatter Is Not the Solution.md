Antimatter is one of those ideas that sounds like a perfect science-fiction answer. On paper, it is almost absurdly attractive. Matter–antimatter annihilation converts mass into energy with unmatched efficiency, which makes it look like the ultimate fuel for deep-space travel. If a civilization has fusion torch drives, orbital megastructures, and advanced industry, then surely antimatter engines must be the obvious next step.

They are not.

The first problem is production. Antimatter does not occur in useful natural quantities. It has to be made, and making it is extraordinarily inefficient. Even today, laboratories such as CERN can produce and store antimatter only in tiny amounts for research, not in anything remotely approaching industrial propellant scale. Antimatter is therefore not something you mine or pump. It is something you manufacture at enormous cost using large accelerator infrastructure. ()

The second problem is storage. Ordinary fuel can sit in tanks. Antimatter cannot touch normal matter at all. The moment it does, it annihilates. That means antimatter must be confined in electromagnetic traps, stored as charged particles or anti-atoms under extremely controlled conditions, and protected from vibration, failure, contamination, and power loss. Even modern antimatter experiments are built around the fact that storing antimatter is difficult, delicate, and limited. Scaling that up from laboratory quantities to ship-grade fuel is not a matter of bigger tanks. It is a matter of turning the entire fuel system into a precision containment machine. ()

The third problem is radiation. Antimatter annihilation is not a clean blue glow from a neat engine bell. It produces extremely energetic radiation and secondary particles that are deeply unpleasant for structures, electronics, and human beings. NASA propulsion analyses explicitly flag high-energy gamma radiation, large shielding demands, transport of antimatter to the engine, massive thermal rejection requirements, and the need to place payload far away from the reaction zone as central design problems. In other words, even if a ship could afford the antimatter, much of the vessel would still end up being built around containing, distancing, shielding, and surviving the consequences of using it. ()

Then there is the military problem. A society that routinely manufactures, transports, and stores significant antimatter quantities has effectively normalized one of the most terrifying weapon materials imaginable. A containment failure is catastrophic. Deliberate weaponization is even worse. Antimatter is not just a fuel issue; it is a strategic-security nightmare. Any state, megacorporation, or military bloc capable of producing large antimatter reserves would instantly become a civilization-scale proliferation concern. That alone is enough to ensure that most societies in **SOL 2349** treat antimatter as tightly controlled, politically toxic, and far too dangerous for normal civilian propulsion. This is an inference from the production, storage, and radiation realities above, rather than a single technical law. ()

There is also a simpler reason antimatter never becomes the default answer: by the time a civilization has mature fusion torch drives, powerful beam-energy infrastructure, high-performance electric propulsion, and an industrialized inner Solar System, it no longer _needs_ antimatter badly enough to justify the risk. Fusion is already good enough to make the Solar System usable. Antimatter does not solve a normal transport problem. It creates a new category of cost, hazard, and political instability in exchange for performance that only a tiny number of missions would truly require. That makes it interesting for black-budget research, prestige projects, military deterrence concepts, and theoretical long-range missions, but not for routine shipping.

So the answer is not that antimatter is weak. Quite the opposite. It is too energetic, too difficult to manufacture, too hard to store, too dangerous to use casually, and too easy to turn into a weapon. In a setting like SOL 2349, that makes antimatter propulsion the kind of technology people talk about with fascination, fear, and a certain professional respect—but not the kind of thing you want parked next to a civilian docking ring.
