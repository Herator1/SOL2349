Guided warheads are the dominant weapon system in space combat across the Solar System. Unlike direct fire weapons, they are not simply fired—they are **launched**, given an initial solution, and then operate as independent vehicles attempting to intercept their target.

In practical terms, every missile or torpedo is a **small spacecraft**. It carries propulsion, guidance systems, onboard processing, and a warhead. What differentiates them is not just size, but how much **Δv (maneuvering capability), sensor quality, and payload** they can carry.

---

# The Nature of a Guided Weapon

Once launched, a guided warhead is no longer under direct control. It follows a predicted intercept path while continuously adjusting its trajectory based on available sensor data.

This creates a fundamental dynamic:

> A missile is not trying to “hit” where the target is — it is trying to arrive where the target will be.

To achieve this, guided warheads rely on three core elements:

- an initial **target solution** (provided by the launching ship)
- onboard **guidance and tracking systems**
- sufficient **propulsion reserves** to correct their course over time

If any of these elements fail or are degraded, the effectiveness of the weapon drops significantly.

---

# Delta-V: The Real Limitation

The most important constraint of guided weapons is not range, but **maneuvering capability**.

Every missile carries a limited amount of propellant. This determines how much it can:

- accelerate
- change direction
- correct for evasive maneuvers

At extreme distances, even small course corrections consume valuable Δv. A target that maneuvers early forces the missile to continuously adjust, potentially exhausting its ability to track.

This is why:

- long-range engagements rely on **prediction and sensor quality**
- closer engagements favor **reaction and maneuvering**

A missile that runs out of Δv does not explode—it simply **misses**.

---

# Guidance and Targeting

Guided warheads depend heavily on the **quality of targeting data**.

At launch, the firing ship provides an initial intercept solution based on:

- sensor data
- trajectory analysis
- relative motion

During flight, the missile refines this solution using onboard sensors or updated targeting data.

However, space combat is an environment of **active interference**.

Ships attempt to degrade incoming guidance through:

- electronic countermeasures
- signal jamming
- false targets and decoys

As a result, a missile’s effectiveness is not only determined by its own systems, but by the **information battle** surrounding it.

---

# Torpedoes vs Missiles

While both are guided weapons, they serve different roles.

**Torpedoes** are larger, slower, and more specialized.  
They typically carry:

- more propellant (higher Δv)
- larger warheads
- more advanced guidance systems

They are designed for:

- long-range engagements
- high-value targets
- strategic strikes

However, their size makes them easier to detect and intercept.

---

**Missiles** are smaller, faster, and deployed in larger numbers.

They trade:

- payload size  
    for
    
- maneuverability and saturation
    

Missiles are effective because they overwhelm defenses. Even if many are destroyed, a few may still reach the target.

---

# The Final Approach

As a guided warhead nears its target, the engagement enters a critical phase.

At this point:

- time to impact is short
- maneuvering windows are minimal
- defensive systems engage at maximum intensity

This is where **Point Defense systems** attempt interception. Even a near miss can be dangerous. In vacuum, there is no shockwave in the traditional sense, but high-velocity fragments, radiation, or directed energy release can still damage external systems.

This is why warheads are designed not only for direct impact, but also for **proximity detonation**.

---

# Why Missiles Dominate

Despite their vulnerabilities, guided warheads remain the primary weapon in space combat.

They offer:

- flexibility across long distances
- the ability to engage without precise alignment
- pressure on defensive systems
- scalability through salvos

Most importantly, they shift combat from a question of pure firepower to one of:

- detection
- prediction
- coordination

In SOL 2349, a missile launch is not just an attack—it is the beginning of a **dynamic interaction between two ships**, where sensors, maneuvering, and defensive systems all determine the outcome.
