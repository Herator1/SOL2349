Space combat in **SOL 2349** is not defined by cinematic dogfights, but by the interaction of complex systems operating under strict physical limits. Every engagement is shaped by a balance between detection, energy management, weapon systems, and defensive capabilities. Ships are not simply armed platforms—they are tightly integrated machines where propulsion, sensors, heat management, and combat systems are inseparable.

At its core, space combat begins long before weapons are fired. Ships continuously emit heat and electromagnetic signatures, making complete invisibility impossible. Instead of hiding, vessels attempt to manage how and when they are detected, relying on sensor systems and electronic warfare to control the flow of information. In many engagements, the outcome is already influenced by which ship establishes a reliable target solution first.

Once combat escalates, ships bring a range of weapon systems to bear, each governed by different physical principles. Guided weapons such as missiles and torpedoes act as autonomous interceptors, capable of adjusting their trajectory over time. Direct fire systems, including kinetic weapons and energy-based arrays, trade flexibility for immediacy and precision. These systems are not interchangeable—they define how a ship fights, at what distance it is effective, and how it pressures an opponent.

Defensive systems are equally critical. In the absence of natural cover, survival depends on a combination of maneuvering, electronic countermeasures, and active interception. Point defense systems form the last line of protection, attempting to destroy incoming threats moments before impact. At the same time, the ship’s structure and internal systems must absorb and survive whatever cannot be stopped.

All of this is constrained by one overriding factor: heat. Every action—accelerating, firing weapons, processing sensor data—generates waste heat that must be dissipated. A ship cannot operate at peak performance indefinitely without risking system degradation or failure. Combat therefore becomes a process of managing limits, deciding when to commit power, and when to hold back.
